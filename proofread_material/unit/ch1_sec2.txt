\section{Propositional Formulas} \label{sec:propFormulas}
\index{propositional formula|(}

We now give the formal definitions of the syntax of
propositional formulas. After that, the next section will
define the truth value of a formula in terms of the
truth values of its variables.

Propositional formulas will be expressions\index{expression},
that is strings
of symbols. The permitted symbols are:
\begin{enumerateLoose}
\item Variables $p_1, p_2, p_3, \ldots$,\index{variable}
\item Propositional connectives\index{propositional connective}
$\lnot$, $\land$, $\lor$, $\limplies$ and~$\liff$, and
\item Parentheses $($ and $)$.
\end{enumerateLoose}

\begin{definition}\label{def:propositionalFmla}
The set of propositional formulas\index{propositional formula}
is inductively defined by:
\begin{itemizeLoose}
\item[(a)] $p_i$~is a propositional formula, for any $i\ge 1$.
\item[(b)] If $A$ is a propositional formula, then so is $\lnot A$.
\item[(c)] If $A$ and $B$ are propositional formulas, then so are
$(A \lor B)$ and $(A \land B)$ and $(A \limplies B)$ and $(A \liff B)$.
\end{itemizeLoose}
\end{definition}
It is implicit in the definition that rules (a)-(c) are the only
ways of generating propositional formulas. It is important that
parentheses are included exactly as indicated.

\begin{example}
\vbox{
Examples of propositional formulas include:
\[
p_1 \quad\quad  p_2 \quad\quad p_{1000} \quad\quad \lnot p_2  \quad\quad \lnot \lnot p_2
\]
\[
(p_1 \limplies (\lnot p_2 \limplies p_1)) \quad\quad ((p_1\limplies \lnot p_2)\limplies p_1)
\]
\[
\lnot( (p_1 \liff \lnot\lnot\lnot p_2) \limplies (\lnot p_2 \lor (p_1\land p_1)) ).
\]
}
\noindent
On the other hand, $p_1\land p_2$ is not a propositional formula due to missing parentheses,
and $(\lnot p_3)$ is not a propositional formula due to having extra parentheses.
Likewise, $p\lor r$ is not a propositional formula due to using variables other than the $p_i$'s.
\end{example}

\paragraph*{Informal abbreviations for formulas.}
The formal definition of formulas requires the inclusion
of many open and close parentheses. These are useful for
uniquely parsing formulas, but often detract from readability.
Thus it is common to informally abbreviate formulas
by omitting parentheses when writing out formulas.
For instance, we can always omit writing the outermost parentheses
without any ambiguity about what the formula is.
However, it is important that we know how to reinsert parentheses
to recover the actual formula. We do this according to the following
rules of precedence\index{precedence of connectives}:
\begin{itemizeLoose}
\item The negation sign, $\lnot$, has the highest precedence.
\item The connectives $\land$ and $\lor$ have the second highest precedence.
\item The connectives $\limplies$ and $\liff$ have the lowest precedence.
\item If parentheses are omitted amongst connectives of the same precedence,
they are associated from right to left.
\end{itemizeLoose}
Some examples are:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{cc}
Informal & Actual \\
abbreviation & formula \\
\hline
$\lnot p_1 \lor \lnot p_2 \lor p_3$
   & $(\lnot p_1 \lor (\lnot p_2 \lor p_3))$ \\
$\lnot p_1 \limplies \lnot p_2 \limplies p_3$
   & $(\lnot p_1 \limplies (\lnot p_2 \limplies p_3))$ \\
$\lnot (p_1 \limplies \lnot p_2) \limplies p_3$
   & $(\lnot (p_1 \limplies \lnot p_2) \limplies p_3)$ \\
$p_1 \lor \lnot p_2 \limplies \lnot p_3 \land p_4$
   & $((p_1 \lor \lnot p_2) \limplies (\lnot p_3 \land p_4))$
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
In addition to omitting parentheses, variable names
such as $p,q,r,\ldots$ are frequently
used instead of $p_1, p_2, p_3,\ldots$. This is just to improve readability; formally
only variables $p_i$ can appear in formulas.

The truth functions for $\land$, $\lor$, and $\liff$ are associative, so the convention
about associating from right to left is not so important for them. However,
$\limplies$ is not associative, as $p\limplies (q \limplies r)$ is not
equivalent to $(p \limplies q) \limplies r$. The formula
$p \limplies (q \limplies r)$ is, however, equivalent truth-functionally to the
formula $p \land q \limplies r$.
This is often a convenient and useful way to write a formula,
and it is for this reason
that we have chosen to associate from
right to left.

It is best to avoid writing things like $A \lor B \land C$
or $A \limplies B \liff C$, to avoid confusing readers. According
to our conventions they mean $A \lor (B \land C)$
or $A \limplies (B \liff C)$, but other authors use different conventions.

\paragraph*{Proofs by induction and recursive definitions.}
The reason for being so careful about giving a formal mathematical definition
of propositional formulas is that we will use it to prove theorems about
propositional formulas. That is to say, we do not merely use formulas,
we also prove theorems about formulas.

Definition~\ref{def:propositionalFmla}
gave an inductive definition\index{recursive definition}
for formulas. This allows properties of formulas to be proved
by induction.  Specifically, suppose we wish to prove that
every propositional formula satisfies some property~$\mathcal Q$.
To prove this by induction, it suffices to show that:\index{proof by induction}
\begin{itemizeLoose}
\item[(a)] Every formula $p_i$ satisfies property~$\mathcal Q$,
\item[(b)] If $A$ is a propositional formula that satisfies property~$\mathcal Q$,
then the formula $\lnot A$ also satisfies property~$\mathcal Q$, and
\item[(c)] If $A$ and~$B$ are propositional formulas
that each satisfy property~$\mathcal Q$,
then the formulas
$(A \land B)$, $(A \lor B)$, $(A\limplies B)$, and $(A\liff B)$
all satisfy property~$\mathcal Q$.
\end{itemizeLoose}
The first case, (a), serves as the base case.
Cases (b) and~(c) serve as the induction steps.
This means there are two induction steps to be proved,
or even five induction steps
if case~(c) has to be treated as four different induction steps.

The third case, (c), of Definition~\ref{def:propositionalFmla} for
propositional formulas treats the case where the principal connective
is $\land$, $\lor$, $\limplies$, or~$\liff$. It requires that parentheses
be included so that the formula has the form $(A \circ B)$, for $\circ$ one
of the four binary connectives. The presence of all these
parentheses means that there is a
unique way to parse any propositional formula. That is to say, if $C$~is
a propositional formula, then there is a unique way to express it in one
of the forms (a)-(c) of Definition~\ref{def:propositionalFmla}. This
is called the \idxem{unique readability} property and means that
propositional formulas can be uniquely parsed.

The inductive definition of propositional formulas in
Definition~\ref{def:propositionalFmla} and the unique readability
property together allow using inductive definitions (also called
``recursive definitions'') to define functions of propositional
formulas. The general framework for defining a function~$h(A)$ by recursion on the
complexity of the formula~$A$ is as follows:\index{recursive definition}
\begin{itemizeLoose}
\item[(a)] For the base case, we define the value of $h$ for
a propositional formula~$p_i$.
\item[(b)] For the first case of recursion, we suppose that the value of~$h$ for
a formula~$A$ is already defined. We use this to define the value of~$h$ for
the formula~$\lnot A$.
\item[(c)] For other cases of recursion, we suppose that the values
of~$h$ on the formulas $A$ and~$B$ have already been defined.
We use these to define the value
of~$h$ for the formula $(A\circ B)$ where $\circ$~is
one of the connectives $\land$, $\lor$, $\limplies$, or~$\liff$.
\end{itemizeLoose}
The \idxnm{unique readability} property means that there is exactly one
way to express a propositional formula in one of the forms $p_i$ or $\lnot A$
or $(A \circ B)$ to apply recursively one of the cases (a), (b), or~(c).
Thus the value~$h$ is uniquely defined for all formulas.

A prominent use of definition by recursion is the
Definition~\ref{def:truthProp} given
in the next section
for the truth value of a propositional formula.
Section~\ref{sec:proofsByInduction} gives some examples of proofs
by induction on the complexity of formulas.
