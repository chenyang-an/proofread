\section{The Soundness and Completeness Theorems}

The Soundness and Completeness Theorems for first-order logic
express the fact that $\FO$ is a good
proof system.  The \idxnm{Soundness Theorem} states that
$\FO$ is ``\idxnm{sound}'' in the sense that any $\FO$-theorem~$A$ is a
logically valid formula.
Likewise, if $A$ is provable using hypotheses from a set~$\Gamma$ of sentences, then
$\Gamma$ logically implies~$A$. This is a basic property for
any proof system for first-order logic, since we of course only want to
have proofs of formulas that are logically implied.

The \idxnm{Completeness Theorem} states the converse, namely that $\FO$ is ``\idxnm{complete}''.
This means firstly that if $A$ is
logically valid, then $A$ has an $\FO$-proof. And, secondly, that if
$A$ is a logical consequence of a set~$\Gamma$ of sentences, then $\Gamma \vdash A$.
In other words, $\FO$ is strong enough to give
proofs of all logical validities and all logical implications.
This is very remarkable!

We now state the Soundness and Completeness Theorems more carefully and
prove the Soundness Theorem. After that, Section~\ref{sec:completenessProofFO} will
present the more difficult proof of the Completeness Theorem.
\begin{theorem}[\idxnm{Soundness Theorem} for $\FO$]
\label{thm:soundFO} Let $A$ be a formula and $\Gamma$~be a set of sentences.
\begin{itemize}
\item[\rm (a)] If $\Gamma$ is satisfiable, then $\Gamma$ is consistent.
\item[\rm (b)] If $\Gamma \vdash A$, then $\Gamma \vDash A$.
\end{itemize}
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[\idxnm{Completeness Theorem} for $\FO$]
\label{thm:completeFO} Let $A$ be a formula and $\Gamma$~be a set of formulas.
\begin{itemize}
\item[\rm (a)] If $\Gamma$ is consistent, then $\Gamma$ is satisfiable.
\item[\rm (b)] If $\Gamma \vDash A$, then $\Gamma \vdash A$.
\end{itemize}
\end{theorem}


Putting the Soundness and Completeness Theorems together gives

\begin{corollary}
Let $A$ be a formula and $\Gamma$ be a set of sentences.
\begin{enumerate}
\item $\Gamma$ is consistent if and only if $\Gamma$ is satisfiable.
\item $\Gamma \vDash A$ if and only if $\Gamma \vdash A$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{corollary}

When there are no hypotheses, so $\Gamma = \varnothing$, we have:
\begin{corollary}
Let $A$ be a formula.
Then $\vDash A$ if and only if $\vdash A$.
\end{corollary}

Parts (a) and~(b) of the Soundness Theorem are easily seen to
be equivalent to each other. Likewise, parts (a) and~(b) of
the Completeness Theorem are easily seen to be equivalent to
each other.  To illustrate this, we first show that part~(b)
of the Soundness Theorem implies part~(a).

Choose $B$ to be a formula which is both $\FO$-provable
and logically valid. For instance, take $B$~to be a
a PL1 axiom. For $\Gamma$ a set of sentences, we have
\begin{implications}
\multicolumn{3}{l}{$\Gamma$ is inconsistent} \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $\Gamma\vdash \lnot B$ & By choice of~$B$ and Theorem~\ref{thm:inconsistencyFO} \\
& $\Rightarrow$ & $\Gamma\vDash \lnot B$ & By part~(b) of the Soundness Theorem \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $\Gamma$ is unsatisfiable & By choice of~$B$ as logically valid.
\end{implications}
That proves that part~(b) of the Soundness Theorem implies part~(a). To prove part~(b)
of the Completeness Theorem from part~(a), recall that $\forall(A)$ is the universal
closure of~$A$ and argue as follows:
\begin{implications}
\multicolumn{3}{l}{$\Gamma \vDash A$} \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $\Gamma \vDash \forall(A)$ & By Theorem~\ref{thm:genTautImpSent}(b) \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $\Gamma \cup\{ \lnot \forall(A)\}$ is unsatisfiable & Theorem~\ref{thm:ContradictionSemanticFO}(b) \\
& $\Rightarrow$ & $\Gamma \cup\{ \lnot \forall(A)\}$ is inconsistent & By part~(a) of the Completeness Theorem \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $\Gamma\vdash \forall(A)$ & Theorem~\ref{thm:ProofByContradictionFO} (Proof by Contradiction) \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $\Gamma\vdash A$ & Theorem~\ref{thm:proofGeneralizations}(b) \\
\end{implications}

We still need to prove part~(b) of the Soundness Theorem
and part~(a) of the Completeness Theorem. These proofs will
be presented in the next sections.  After that,
Section~\ref{sec:compactnessFO} will
state and prove the Compactness Theorem for $\FO$ for the
general case where $\Gamma$ is a set of formulas.

\paragraph*{The proof of the Soundness Theorem.} We already proved that part~(b) of
the Soundness Theorem implies part~(a). Now we prove part~(b).
\begin{proof}[Proof of part~(b) of the Soundness Theorem]
Assume that $\Gamma$ is a set of sentences
and $\Gamma \vdash A$.  We must show $\Gamma \vDash A$.
Let
\[
B_1,\, B_2,\, \ldots, B_k
\]
be an $\FO$-proof of~$A$ from $\Gamma$. We will prove by induction on~$i$
that $\Gamma \vDash \forall (B_i)$. More specifically, we
fix a structure~$\mathfrakA$ such that $\mathfrakA \vDash \Gamma$.
Then we show by induction on~$i$ that $\mathfrakA\vDash B_i[\sigma]$ holds for
every object assignment~$\sigma$. It is important to note that
$\sigma$~is not fixed; instead, the induction hypothesis holds for
all object assignments~$\sigma$.

The proof breaks into cases
depending on how $B_i$ is inferred:
\begin{description}
\item[Case 1:] Suppose $B_i$ is a hypothesis, $B_i \in \Gamma$.
By assumption, $\mathfrakA \vDash B_i$, so this case is trivial.
\item[Case 2:] Suppose $B_i$ is axiom from PL1-PL4, and thus
a tautology. Then, for all~$\sigma$, $\mathfrakA \vDash\penalty10000 B_i[\sigma]$ by
Theorem~\ref{thm:tautImpliesEquiv}(a).
\item[Case 3:] Suppose $B_i$ is one of the equality axioms EQ1-EQ3.
Since the definition of truth interprets $=$ as true equality
(see Theorem~\ref{thm:EQ123instances}),
$\mathfrakA\vDash B_i[\sigma]$ holds for all~$\sigma$.
\item[Case 4:] Suppose $B_i$ is one of the equality axioms EQ${}_f$ or EQ${}_P$.
By Lemma~\ref{lem:equalityAxioms}, $\mathfrakA\vDash B_i[\sigma]$ holds for all~$\sigma$.
\item[Case 5:] Suppose $B_i$ is a UI axiom $\forall x\, C(x) \limplies C(t)$.
Then $\mathfrakA\vDash B_i[\sigma]$ holds by Theorem~\ref{thm:equalityAxiom_1}.
\item[Case 6:] Suppose $B_i$ is introduced by Modus Ponens
from $B_j$ and~$B_k$ where $B_k$~is $B_j\limplies B_i$. Fix an arbitrary
object assignment~$\sigma$. By the induction
hypotheses, $\mathfrakA\vDash B_j[\sigma]$
and $\mathfrakA\vDash (B_j\limplies\penalty10000 B_k)[\sigma]$.
The definition of truth for~$\limplies$,
yields that $\mathfrakA\vDash B_j[\sigma]$ holds.
\item[Case 7:] Suppose $B_i$ is introduced by a
Generalization (GEN) inference. Then $B_i$~has the
form $C\limplies \forall x\, D$ and for some $j<i$,
$B_j$~has the form $C\limplies D$ where $x$~is not free in~$C$.
The induction hypothesis states that, for all~$\sigma$,
$\mathfrakA \vDash (C\limplies D)[\sigma]$. Thus, by
the definition of truth,
$\mathfrakA \vdash \forall x\, (C\limplies D)[\sigma]$
for all~$\sigma$.  Therefore, by Example~\ref{example:prenexACI},
$\mathfrakA \vdash (C\limplies \forall x\, D)[\sigma]$ holds
for all~$\sigma$.
\end{description}
That completes the proof of the Soundness Theorem.
\end{proof}
