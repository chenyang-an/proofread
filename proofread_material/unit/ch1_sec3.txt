\section{Definition of Truth in Propositional Logic}\label{sec:propTruth}
\def\cursectionname{Definition of Truth}\index{truth, definition of|(}

The previous section defined the {\em syntax} of propositional
formulas; now we define their {\em semantics}, namely, what it means
for a propositional formula to be true or false.  The truth
or falsity of a formula depends on the truth values of the variables
appearing in it. For instance, the formula $p_1 \limplies p_2$
could have value either True~($\True$) or False~($\False$) depending
on the values of $p_1$ and~$p_2$. Accordingly, we start with an
assignment~$\varphi$ of truth values to the propositional variables.
\begin{definition}\label{def:truthAssignment}
A \idxem{truth assignment}~$\varphi$ is a mapping
\[
\varphi : \{p_1,p_2, p_3 ,\ldots\} \rightarrow \{\True, \False\} 
\]
from the set of propositional variables to the set of truth
values $\True$ and~$\False$.
\end{definition}
A truth assignment~$\varphi$ to the variables gives
a truth value~$\varphi(p_i)$ to each variable~$p_i$. We can
extend~$\varphi$ to
give truth values to all propositional formulas:
\begin{definition}[Definition of Truth for Propositional Formulas]
\label{def:truthProp}
Suppose $\varphi$ is a truth assignment. The function~$\varphi$ is
extended to have domain the set of all propositional formulas
by defining $\varphi$ by recursion as:
\begin{enumerateLoose}
\item If $A$ is $\lnot B$, then
$ \displaystyle \varphi(A) = \left\{ \begin{array}{ll}
\False & \hbox{if $\varphi(B) = \True$} \\
\True & \hbox{otherwise}
\end{array}\right.$
\item If $A$ is $(B \lor C)$, then
$ \displaystyle \varphi(A) = \left\{ \begin{array}{ll}
\True & \hbox{if $\varphi(B) = \True$ or $\varphi(C)=\True$} \\
\False & \hbox{otherwise}
\end{array}\right.$
\item If $A$ is $(B \land C)$, then
$ \displaystyle \varphi(A) = \left\{ \begin{array}{ll}
\True & \hbox{if $\varphi(B) = \True$ and $\varphi(C)=\True$} \\
\False & \hbox{otherwise}
\end{array}\right.$
\item If $A$ is $(B \limplies C)$, then
$ \displaystyle \varphi(A) = \left\{ \begin{array}{ll}
\True & \hbox{if $\varphi(B) = \False$ or $\varphi(C)=\True$} \\
\False & \hbox{otherwise}
\end{array}\right.$
\item If $A$ is $(B \liff C)$, then
$ \displaystyle \varphi(A) = \left\{ \begin{array}{ll}
\True & \hbox{if $\varphi(B) = \varphi(C)$} \\
\False & \hbox{otherwise}
\end{array}\right.$
\end{enumerateLoose}
\end{definition}

It is important to note how the definition of truth 
follows the informal meanings of the connectives as
discussed in Section~\ref{sec:introProp}.

\begin{example}
Let $A$ be the formula $(p\lor q) \limplies (p \land q)$.
There are only two variables $p, q$ in this formula, so
the truth value~$\varphi(A)$ of~$A$ depends on
$\varphi(p)$ and $\varphi(q)$. In particular, $\varphi(A)$
does not depend on $\varphi(r)$ for
any other variable~$r$. Since $\varphi(p)$ and $\varphi(q)$
can only be $\True$ or~$\False$, there are only four ways to
set the truth values of $p$ and~$q$.  These are
pictured in the \idxnm{truth table} shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:truthpOqpNq} in the first two columns.
The second-to-last column of the truth table gives the
corresponding values of $\varphi((p\lor q) \limplies (p \land q))$.

In the second line of Figure~\ref{fig:truthpOqpNq},
$\varphi(p) = \True$ and $\varphi(q)= \False$. Hence,
with two applications of the definition of
truth, $\varphi( p\lor q ) = \True$ and $\varphi(p\land q) = \False$.
With one more application of the definition of truth,
$\varphi((p\lor q) \limplies (p \land q)) = \False$. That is,
$A$ is false when $p$~is true and $q$~is false.

Let $B$ be the formula $(p\land q) \limplies (p \lor q)$.
The final column of Figure~\ref{fig:truthpOqpNq}
shows the values of $\varphi(B)$ for all possible
assignments of values to $p$ and~$q$.
Since $\varphi(B) = \True$ for all four assignments, 
we call $B$ a ``\idxnm{tautology}'' or
say that $B$ is ``tautologically valid''.   On the other hand, $\varphi(A)$ is
$\True$ for some truth assignments and false for others. Therefore, $A$~is
not a tautology. However, $A$ is ``\idxnm{satisfiable}'', since there
is a truth assignment that makes $A$ true.
\end{example}
