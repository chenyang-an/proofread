\section{Prenex Formulas}\label{sec:prenex}

A ``\idxnm{prenex formula}'' is a formula in which all quantifiers
are at the front of the formula. For example, $\forall x\, \exists y\, \forall z\, (A\land B)$ is
a prenex formula, but $\forall x\, \exists y\, (A\land \forall z\, B)$ is not.
It turns out that every first-order formula can be reexpressed in prenex form.

\begin{definition}\label{def:prenex}
The \emph{prenex formulas} are inductively defined by:
\begin{enumerate}
\item If $A$ is \idxnm{quantifier-free}, i.e., if $A$~contains no
quantifiers, then $A$ is a \idxnm{prenex formula}.
\item If $A$ is a prenex formula and $x_i$ is a variable, then
$\forall x_i\, A$ and $\exists x_i\, A$ are prenex formulas.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

An alternative definition of prenex formulas can be given in terms of not having
a quantifier in the scope of a propositional connective.
Definition~\ref{def:scopeQuantifier} already defined the scope
of a quantifier. The scope of a propositional connective is
defined similarly:
\begin{definition}\label{def:scopePropositional}
Let $A$ be a formula. Let $B$ be a subformula of the form $\lnot C$.
The \idxem{scope} of this negation sign is the subformula~$C$.
Now let $B$ be a subformula of the form $C \circ D$ with $\circ$ a
binary propositional connective ($\land$, $\lor$, $\limplies$, or~$\liff$).
The scope of this connective~$\circ$ is the two subformulas $C$ and~$D$.
\end{definition}

Clearly, a formula~$A$ is prenex\index{prenex formula} if and only if
no quantifier occurs in~$A$ in the scope of a propositional connective.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:prenex}
Every formula~$A$ is logically equivalent to a \idxnm{prenex formula}.
\end{theorem}

The next lemma is a crucial ingredient in the proof
Theorem~\ref{thm:prenex}.

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:prenexHelpers}
Let $A$ and $C$ be formulas, and assume $x$ is not free in~$C$.
Then
\begin{itemize}
\item[(a)] $C \land \exists x\, A \tautEq \exists x\,(C \land A)$
\item[(b)] $C \land \forall x\, A \tautEq \forall x\,(C \land A)$
\item[(c)] $C \lor \forall x\, A \tautEq \forall x\,(C \lor A)$
\item[(d)] $C \lor \exists x\, A \tautEq \exists x\,(C \lor A)$
\item[(e)] $C \limplies \forall x\, A \tautEq \forall x\,(C \limplies A)$
\item[(f)] $C \limplies \exists x\, A \tautEq \exists x\,(C \limplies A)$
\item[(g)] $(\forall x\, A) \limplies C \tautEq \exists x\,(A \limplies C)$
\item[(h)] $(\exists x\, A) \limplies C \tautEq \forall x\,(A \limplies C)$
\end{itemize}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:prenexHelpers}]
We will sketch the proof, and leave some of the details to
the exercises.  First,
part~(e) was already proved in Example~\ref{example:prenexACI}.
We claim that parts (a), (c), and~(h) are essentially equivalent to part~(e).
For instance, to prove part~(h), we note that
$(\exists x\, A) \limplies C$ is tautologically equivalent to
$\lnot C \limplies\penalty10000 \lnot \exists x\, A$. The latter
is logically equivalent to $\lnot C \limplies\penalty10000 \forall x \, \lnot A$
by Theorem~\ref{thm:negQnegBis}. That in turn is logically
equivalent to $\forall x\, (\lnot C \limplies \lnot A)$ since part~(e) holds
in general. This last formula is logically equivalent to
$\forall x\, ( A \limplies  C)$, and thus part~(h) is proved.
Similar arguments prove parts (a) and~(c) from~(e) by
expressing $\land$ and $\lor$ in terms of $\limplies$ and~$\lnot$.

Now let's prove part~(d). As is proved in Exercise~\ref{exer:EorEorEAandAandA},
$\exists x\,(C \lor A)$ is logically equivalent to
$\exists x\,C \lor \exists x\, A$.\footnote{The application of
Exercise~\ref{exer:EorEorEAandAandA}
does not need the assumption that $x$ is not free in~$C$.}  And since
$x$ is not free in~$C$,
Exercise~\ref{exer:QxNotFree} states that $\exists x\, C$ is logically
equivalent to~$C$. It follows that
$\exists x\,(C \lor A)$ is logically equivalent to
$C \lor \exists x\, A$, so (d) is proved.  Parts (b), (f), and~(g)
are essentially equivalent to part~(b), analogously to the way
that parts (a), (c), (e), and~(h) were essentially equivalent to each other.
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:prenex} is now proved by using the lemma; the main
new thing is that we have to handle the case where $x$ is free in~$C$
by using alphabetic variants.

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem \ref{thm:prenex} on prenex formulas]
Without loss of generality, $A$~does not contain the symbol~$\liff$
since any subformula $B\liff C$ can replaced by the tautologically
equivalent $(B\limplies C) \land (C\limplies B)$.

We modify $A$ step-by-step by moving quantifiers in front
of propositional connectives. Each step will reduce the
number of pairs of occurrences of
propositional connectives ($\lnot$ or $\circ$)
and quantifiers $Q x$ such that $Qx$ lies the scope of the propositional
connective. If $A$ is not in prenex form, then there must be
at least one subformula of the form $\lnot Q x\, B$ or $Q x\, B \circ C$
or $C \circ Q x\, C$.

Suppose $A$ has a subformula $\lnot Q x\, B$ where $Q x$ denotes
either $\forall x$ or $\exists x$.  Let $\overline Q x$ denote
the \idxnm{dual quantifier} $\exists x$ or $\forall x$, respectively.
Then $\lnot Q x \, B$ is logically equivalent to $\overline Q x\, \lnot $.
Replacing $\lnot Q x \, B$ in~$A$ with $\overline Q x\, \lnot B $
yields a formula that is logically equivalent to~$A$
and has one less pair of occurrences of propositional connectives
and quantifiers with the quantifier in the scope of the propositional
connective.

Now suppose $A$ has a subformula $(Qx\, B) \lor C$.  If $x$ does not
appear free in~$C$, then replace this subformula with the
logically equivalent $Q x \,(B \lor C)$; the logical equivalence
follows from part (c) or~(d) of Lemma~\ref{lem:prenexHelpers}.
On the other hand, if $x$ does appear free in~$C$, choose a new
variable~$y$ that does not appear anywhere in~$A$.  Then $Q y\, B(y/x)$
is an alphabetic variant of~$Qx\, B$ and thus is logically
equivalent to~$Qx\, B$. Since $y$ was chosen to be a variable that
is not used at all in~$A$, it has no free occurrence in~$C$.
Therefore, $Q y \,(B(y/x) \lor C)$ is logically equivalent to
$(Qx\, B) \lor C$. Replacing $(Qx\, B) \lor C$ with $Q y \,(B(y/x) \lor C)$
in~$A$ yields a logically equivalent formula. It also reduces the number of
pairs of propositional connectives and quantifiers with the quantifier inside the
scope of the logical connective.

The remaining cases where $A$ has a subformula of the
form $((Q x\, B) \circ C$ or $(C \circ (Q x\, B)$ are similar.
These are proved using the other logical equivalences from
Lemma~\ref{lem:prenexHelpers}.

We leave the rest of the details of the proof to the reader.
\end{proof}


\begin{example}
Let $A$ be the formula  $\forall x\, P(x) \lor \exists y\, Q(z,y)$.
Using prenex operations, we have the tautological equivalences
\[
\forall x\, P(x) \lor \exists y\, Q(z,y)
   ~\tautEq~ \forall x\, ( P(x) \lor \exists y\, Q(z,y) )
   ~\tautEq~ \forall x\, \exists y\, ( P(x) \lor Q(z,y) ).
\]
Thus $A$ is logically equivalent to the prenex formula
$\forall x\, \exists y\, ( P(x) \lor Q(x,y) )$.

It is possible to pull out the quantifiers in a different order:
\[
\forall x\, P(x) \lor \exists y\, Q(z,y)
   ~\tautEq~ \exists y\, ( \forall x\, P(x) \lor Q(z,y) )
   ~\tautEq~ \exists y\, \forall x\, ( P(x) \lor Q(z,y) ).
\]
So $A$ is also logically equivalent to the
prenex formula $\exists y\, \forall x\, ( P(x) \lor\penalty10000 Q(x,y)$.
Of course, these two prenex forms of~$A$ are logically
equivalent to each other as well.
\end{example}

\begin{example}
Now let $A$ be the formula
$\forall x\, P(x,y) \limplies \forall x\, \exists y \, Q(x,y)$. The variable~$x$
is quantified twice, and the variable~$y$ has both free and bound occurrences.
Accordingly, we must work with alphabetic variants to convert~$A$ into
prenex form.  This can be done step-by-step as:
\begin{implications}
\multicolumn{3}{l}{$\forall x\, P(x,y) \limplies \forall x\, \exists y \, Q(x,y)$} &  \\
& $\tautEq$ & $\exists x\, (P(x,y) \limplies \forall x\, \exists y \, Q(x,y)$ & Lemma~\ref{lem:prenexHelpers}(g) \\
& $\tautEq$ & $\exists x\, (P(x,y) \limplies \forall x'\, \exists y \, Q(x',y)$ & Alphabetic variant \\
& $\tautEq$ & $\exists x\, \forall x'\, (P(x,y) \limplies \exists y \, Q(x',y)$ & Lemma~\ref{lem:prenexHelpers}(e) \\
& $\tautEq$ & $\exists x\, \forall x'\, (P(x,y) \limplies \exists y' \, Q(x',y')$ & Alphabetic variant \\
& $\tautEq$ & $\exists x\, \forall x'\, \exists y' \, (P(x,y) \limplies Q(x',y')$ & Lemma~\ref{lem:prenexHelpers}(f) \\
\end{implications}
Just as in the previous example, it would be possible to pull the quantifiers out in
a different order. Indeed, the initial block of quantifiers could also
end up as ``$\forall x'\, \exists x\, \exists y'$'' or ``$\forall x'\, \exists y'\, \exists x$''.
\end{example}
