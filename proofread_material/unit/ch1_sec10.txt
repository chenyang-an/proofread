¥section{Propositional Substitution}¥label{sec:substitutionProp}

¥index{substitution!propositional|(}

Propositional substitution means substituting a formula‾$B$
for a variable‾$p_i$ inside some formula‾$A$.
In other words, it means
replacing a variable‾$p$ in‾$A$ with
a formula‾$B$. There are several reasons to look carefully
at substitution. First of all, we need to prove that if
$A$‾is a tautology, then so is the formula obtained by
substituting $B$ for every occurrence of‾$p$ in‾$A$. This will be important
for the proof system‾$¥PL$ defined in the next chapter. Second, we need to
prove that if $B$ and $C$ are tautologically equivalent, and we replace
an occurrence of‾$B$ as a subformula of‾$A$ with‾$C$, then the result is
tautologically equivalent to‾$A$. This is a fact we have already used
more than once, notably in Section‾¥ref{sec:adequate} when discussing
adequate sets of connectives. For example, the proof of
Theorem‾¥ref{thm:notImpliesAdequate} replaced subformulas $B¥lor C$ with
$¥lnot B ¥limplies C$, and argued that this preserved tautological equivalence.
A third reason is that studying the
substitution of propositional formulas for variables
will give a glimpse of the issues we will encounter with substitution in
first-order logic in later chapters.

We use the notation $A(B/p)$ to mean the formula
that results from‾$A$ after replacing every occurrence of the variable‾$p$
in‾$A$ with the formula‾$B$.  More generally, we write
$A(B_1,¥ldots,B_k/p_{i_1},¥ldots,p_{i_k})$ to denote the
result of replacing, in parallel,
each occurrence of each‾$p_{i_j}$ in‾$A$ with‾$B_j$. The formal definition is
below, but first we consider a few examples.

¥begin{example}
Let $A$ be the formula $(p_1 ¥limplies (p_2 ¥limplies p_1))$.
Letting $B$ be $((p_2¥land¥penalty10000 p_3)¥limplies p_1)$, we have
$A(((p_2¥land p_3)¥limplies p_1)/p_1)$ is the formula
¥[
(((p_2¥land p_3)¥limplies p_1) ¥limplies (p_2 ¥limplies ((p_2¥land p_3)¥limplies p_1))) .
¥]
As this example shows, it is permitted that $p_1$ appears in‾$B$.

For another example, $A(p_1/p_1)$‾is the same as‾$A$.

Finally, let $C$ be $(p_2¥land p_3)$ and $D$ be $(p_1¥land p_4)$. Then
$A(C,D/p_1,p_2)$ is the formula
¥[
((p_2¥land p_3) ¥limplies ((p_1¥land p_4) ¥limplies (p_2¥land p_3))) .
¥]
Note that the two $p_1$'s in‾$A$ were replaced with‾$C$ and the $p_2$
in‾$A$ was replaced with‾$D$. But the $p_1$ and $p_2$ that appear in
$C$ and‾$D$ do not change. This reflects the fact that the substitution
of $C$ and‾$D$ for occurrences of $p_1$ and‾$p_2$ is done ``in parallel''.

If substitutions are to be done sequentially¥index{substitution!sequential}
instead of in parallel, we would
use the notation $A(C/p_1)(D/p_2)$.  This would mean first substituting
$C$ for‾$p_1$ in‾$A$ and then substituting $D$ for‾$p_2$ in the resulting
formula. You should convince yourself that this is the same as
$A(C',D/p_1,p_2)$ where $C'$‾is $C(D/p_2)$.
¥end{example}

¥begin{definition}¥label{def:substitutionProp}¥index{substitution}¥index{substitution!parallel}
Let $k¥ge 1$ and $A,B_1,¥ldots, B_k$ be propositional formulas
and $p_{i_1},¥ldots, p_{i_k}$ be (distinct) propositional variables. Then
the formula $A(B_1,¥ldots,B_k/p_{i_1},¥ldots,p_{i_k})$ is
recursively defined as follows:
¥begin{enumerateLoose}
¥item If $A$ is the variable‾$p_{i_j}$ for some $1¥le j ¥le k$,
then $A(B_1,¥ldots,B_k/p_{i_1},¥ldots,p_{i_k})$ is‾$B_j$.
¥item If $A$ is a variable‾$p_¥ell$ with $¥ell ¥notin ¥{i_1,¥ldots, i_k¥}$,
then $A(B_1,¥ldots,B_k/p_{i_1},¥ldots,p_{i_k})$ is‾$p_¥ell$.
In other words, $A$‾is
unchanged by the substitution.
¥item If $A$ is $¥lnot C$, then $A(B_1,¥ldots,B_k/p_{i_1},¥ldots,p_{i_k})$
is $¥lnot C(B_1,¥ldots,B_k/p_{i_1},¥ldots,p_{i_k})$.
In other words, it
is $¥lnot C^¥prime$ where $C^¥prime$ is $C(B_1,¥ldots,B_k/p_{i_1},¥ldots,p_{i_k})$.
¥item If $A$ is $C ¥circ D$ for $¥circ$ a binary connective,
then $A(B_1,¥ldots,B_k/p_{i_1},¥ldots,p_{i_k})$ is
$C(B_1,¥ldots,B_k/p_{i_1},¥ldots,p_{i_k})¥circ D(B_1,¥ldots,B_k/p_{i_1},¥ldots,p_{i_k})$.
In other words, it
is $C^¥prime ¥circ D^¥prime$
where $C^¥prime$ is as before and $D^¥prime$‾is $D(B_1,¥ldots,B_k/p_{i_1},¥ldots,p_{i_k})$.
¥end{enumerateLoose}
¥end{definition}

The reader is warned that sometimes different notations are used for substitution.
For example, some authors will introduce a formula‾$A$ as $A = A(p)$, and then
write $A(B)$ to denote what we mean by $A(B/p)$. This notation $A(B)$ has the
advantage of being less cluttered and more readable, but it has the disadvantage
of being a little ambiguous about whether $A(B)$ means the result of
replacing all of the occurrences of‾$p$ with‾$B$ or just some of the occurrences.
For the time being, we will stick with the notation $A(B/p)$,
and it always means replacing all of the occurrences of‾$p$.

It is also fairly common to use the notation $A(B_1/p_1,¥ldots,B_k/p_k)$
instead of $A(B_1,¥ldots,B_k/p_1,¥ldots,p_k)$ for parallel¥index{substitution!parallel} substitution.

¥begin{definition}¥label{def:instanceProp}
Any formula of the form $A(B/p_i)$ is called an ¥idxem{instance} of‾$A$.
¥end{definition}

The next theorem states that
if $B$ and $C$ have the same truth value,
then the formulas $A(B/p)$ and $A(C/p)$ have the same truth value.
It is a straightforward result,
but is the crucial fact needed to prove the rest of our theorems
on substitution of propositional formulas.

¥begin{theorem}¥label{thm:substituteProp1}
Let $A$, $B$ and $C$ be formulas, and $p_i$ be a variable.
Also, let $¥varphi$ be a truth assignment.
If $¥varphi(B) = ¥varphi(C)$, then $¥varphi(A(B/p_i)) = ¥varphi(A(C/p_i))$.
¥end{theorem}

¥begin{proof}
Let $B$, $C$, $p_i$ and‾$¥varphi$ be fixed. We prove that the
theorem holds for all‾$A$, by using induction on‾$A$.
¥begin{description}
¥item[Base case ¥#1:] Suppose $A$ is the propositional variable‾$p_i$.
Then $A(B/p_i)$ and $A(C/p_i)$ are just $B$ and‾$C$. So by the hypothesis
that $¥varphi(B) = ¥varphi(C)$, we have immediately
$¥varphi(A(B/p_i))=¥varphi(A(C/p_i))$.
¥item[Base case ¥#2:] Suppose $A$ is a variable‾$p_j$, with $j ¥neq i$.
Then $A(B/p_i)$ and $A(C/p_i)$ are both just‾$A$, so of course
$¥varphi(A(B/p_i))=¥varphi(A(C/p_i))$.
¥item[Induction step ¥#1:] Suppose $A$ is $¥lnot D$, so
$A(B/p_i)$ is $¥lnot D(B/p_i)$ and $A(C/p_i)$ is $¥lnot D(C/p_i)$.
The induction hypothesis
states that $¥varphi(D(B/p_i))=¥varphi(D(C/p_i))$.  
By the definition of truth for‾$¥lnot$, we have immediately
that $¥varphi(A(B/p_i))=¥varphi(A(C/p_i))$.
¥item[Induction step ¥#2:] Suppose $A$ is $D¥circ E$ where $¥circ$ is one of $¥land, ¥lor, ¥limplies, ¥liff$.
Thus
$A(B/p_i)$ is $D(B/p_i)¥circ E(B/p_i)$ and $A(C/p_i)$ is $D(C/p_i)¥circ E(C/p_i)$.
There are two induction hypotheses: that $¥varphi(D(B/p_i))=¥varphi(D(C/p_i))$
and that $¥varphi(E(B/p_i))=¥varphi(E(C/p_i))$. By the definition of
truth for‾$¥circ$, $¥varphi(A(B/p_i))=¥varphi(A(C/p_i))$. $¥qedhere$
¥end{description}
¥end{proof}

Theorem‾¥ref{thm:substituteProp1} was stated relative to a single truth assignment.
If we strengthen the hypothesis to say that $¥varphi(B)=¥varphi(C)$ for
{¥em all} truth assignments, we get the following corollary.

¥begin{corollary}¥label{coro:substituteTaut}
Suppose $B ¥tautEq C$.
Then $A(B/p_i) ¥tautEq A(C/p_i)$.
¥end{corollary}

This corollary has already been used earlier, for instance in
the proof of Theorem‾¥ref{thm:notImpliesAdequate}. In that proof,
we asserted that if a subformula $(B¥lor C)$ of‾$A$ is replaced
with $(¥lnot B¥limplies C)$, then the resulting formula‾$A'$ is
tautologically equivalent to‾$A$. Of course, we have so far only
discussed substituting a formula for a {¥em variable}, not for
a subformula. Nonetheless, we can recast substitution so as to apply
it to replacing a subformula with another formula.

As an example, let $p_¥ell$ be a variable that does not appear in‾$A$,
and let $A^*$‾be the formula obtained from‾$A$ by replacing
the subformula $(B¥lor C)$ with‾$p_¥ell$. Then
$A$‾is the same as $A^*((B¥lor C)/p_¥ell)$ and $A'$‾is
the same as $A^*((¥lnot B ¥limplies C)/p_¥ell)$. The corollary
states that $A^*((B¥lor C)/p_¥ell)$ and $A^*((¥lnot B ¥limplies C)/p_¥ell)$
are tautologically equivalent. That is, that $A$ and‾$A'$ are
tautologically equivalent. This is exactly what was needed for the
proof of Theorem‾¥ref{thm:notImpliesAdequate}.

The above corollary talked about substituting two tautologically
equivalent formulas into the same formula‾$A$.  The next theorem
talks about substituting $A$ into two tautologically equivalent
formulas.

¥begin{theorem}¥label{thm:substituteProp2}
Suppose $B ¥tautEq C$. Then $B(A/p_i) ¥tautEq C(A/p_i)$.
¥end{theorem}

¥begin{proof}
Let $¥varphi$ be an arbitrary truth assignment. We need to
show that $¥varphi(B(A/p_i)) =¥penalty10000 ¥varphi(C(A/p_i))$.
Let $p_¥ell$ be a variable that does not appear in any of $A$, $B$, or‾$C$.
Define $¥varphi^¥prime$ to be the truth assignment such
that $¥varphi(p_¥ell) = ¥varphi(A)$ and otherwise agrees
with‾$¥varphi$. In other words,
¥begin{equation}¥label{eq:truthassignVariant}
¥varphi^¥prime(p_j) ‾=‾ ¥left¥{ ¥begin{array}{ll}
¥varphi(A) ‾‾‾ & ¥hbox{if $j=¥ell$} ¥¥
¥varphi(p_j) ‾‾‾ &¥hbox{if $j¥neq ¥ell$.}
¥end{array} ¥right.
¥end{equation}
We call $¥varphi'$ a ``$p_¥ell$-variant¥index{variant!$p_¥ell$-variant}'' of‾$¥varphi$
since $¥varphi$ and‾$¥varphi^¥prime$ agree on all variables except
possibly‾$p_¥ell$.
Note that $¥varphi$ and‾$¥varphi'$ agree on all variables
that appear in $B(A/p_i)$ or in $C(A/p_i)$.  We have
¥begin{threecolproof}
$¥varphi(B(A/p_i))‾‾=‾$ & $¥varphi^¥prime(B(A/p_i))$ & By Theorem‾¥ref{thm:varsDependOnProp} since $p_¥ell$ does not appear in $B(A/p_i)$ ¥¥
$=‾$ & $¥varphi^¥prime(B(p_¥ell/p_i))$ & By Theorem‾¥ref{thm:substituteProp1} since $¥varphi^¥prime(A) = ¥varphi^¥prime(p_¥ell)$ ¥¥
$=‾$ & $¥varphi^¥prime(C(p_¥ell/p_i))$ & By $B ¥tautEq C$ since $p_¥ell$ does not appear in $B$ or $C$ ¥footnotemark ¥¥
$=‾$ & $¥varphi^¥prime(C(A/p_i))$ & By Theorem‾¥ref{thm:substituteProp1} again ¥¥
$=‾$ & $¥varphi(C(A/p_i))$ & By Theorem‾¥ref{thm:varsDependOnProp} again.$¥qedhere$
¥end{threecolproof}%
¥footnotetext{Informally, $B(p_¥ell/p_i)$ and $C(p_¥ell/p_i)$ are obtained
from $B$ and‾$C$ by renaming $p_i$ to‾$p_¥ell$, and therefore $B¥tautEq C$ implies
$B(p_¥ell/p_i) ¥tautEq C(p_¥ell/p_i)$.
Strictly speaking, we should introduce another truth assignment‾$¥varphi^¥pprime$ which
is the $p_i$-variant of‾$¥varphi'$ with $¥varphi^¥pprime(p_i) = ¥varphi^¥prime(p_¥ell)$. Then we argue
$¥varphi^¥prime(B(p_¥ell/p_i))=¥varphi^¥pprime(B)=¥varphi^¥pprime(C)=¥varphi^¥prime(C(p_¥ell/p_i))$.}
¥end{proof}

¥begin{corollary}¥label{coro:instanceTaut} ‾‾¥ ‾
¥begin{itemize}
¥item[(a)] Any instance of a tautology is a tautology.
¥item[(b)] If $A¥vDash B$, then $A(C/p_i) ¥vDash B(C/p_i)$
¥item[(c)] If $A_1,¥ldots, A_k ¥vDash B$, then $A_1(C/p_i),¥ldots A_k(C/p_i) ¥vDash B(C/p_i)$.
¥end{itemize}
¥end{corollary}

¥begin{proof}
Let $A$ be a tautology, and $A(B/p_i)$ be an instance of‾$A$. To prove‾(a),
we must show that $A(B/p_i)$ is a tautology.  Let $p_¥ell$ be a variable
that does not occur in‾$A$. We have $A ¥tautEq (p_¥ell ¥lor ¥lnot p_¥ell)$
since both $A$ and $p_¥ell ¥lor ¥lnot p_¥ell$ are tautologies.
Therefore, by the previous theorem, substituting $B$ for‾$p_i$,
¥[
A(B/p_i) ¥tautEq (p_¥ell ¥lor ¥lnot p_¥ell),
¥]
since $p_i$ does not appear in $(p_¥ell ¥lor ¥lnot p_¥ell)$.
Therefore, $A(B/p_i)$ is a tautology.

Part‾(b) follows easily from part‾(a). First, $A¥vDash B$ holds iff
and only if $¥vDash¥penalty10000 A¥limplies¥penalty10000 B$. Likewise,
$A(C/p_i) ¥vDash B(C/p_i)$ holds if and only if
$¥vDash A(C/p_i) ¥limplies¥penalty10000 B(C/p_i)$. Finally,
$A(C/p_i) ¥limplies B(C/p_i)$ is equal to
$(A¥limplies B)(C/p_i)$. Thus (a) implies‾(b).

Part‾(c) is proved similarly to part‾(b) using the fact that
$A_1,¥ldots, A_k ¥vDash B$ holds if and only if
$A_1¥land ¥cdots¥land  A_k ¥limplies B$ is a tautology.
¥end{proof}
