\section{Examples of Tautologies and Tautological Equivalences}\label{sec:TautExamples}
\def\cursectionname{Tautologies and Tautological Equivalences}

Here we list some of the more common tautologies and tautological
equivalences.  These can all be proved with the method of truth tables
(or decision trees).
They can also be proved using the proof system~$\PL$
that will be introduced in
Chapter~\ref{chap:PropProofs}.


\vbox{
\paragraph*{Simple tautologies on a single variable.}
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{c@{~~~-~}l}
$p \lor \lnot p$ & Law of the Excluded Middle\index{excluded middle} \\
$\lnot (p \land \lnot p)$ & Noncontradiction \\
$p \limplies p$ & Self-Implication \\
$p \liff p$ & Self-Equivalence \\
$\lnot \lnot p \liff p$& Double Negation\index{double negation} \\
$ ( \lnot p \limplies p ) \liff p$ & Equivalence with Implication from Negation
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
}


\vbox{
\paragraph*{Simple tautological equivalences.}
\nopagebreak
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{c@{~~~-~}l}
$\lnot ( p \lor q ) \tautEq (\lnot p \land \lnot q) $ & De Morgan's Law\index{De Morgan's law} \\
$\lnot ( p \land q ) \tautEq (\lnot p \lor \lnot q) $ & De Morgan's Law \\
$p \lor p \tautEq p$ & Idempotency\index{idempotency} of $\lor$ \\
$p \land p \tautEq p$ & Idempotency of $\land$ \\
$p \land q \tautEq q \land p$ & Commutativity\index{commutativity} of $\land$ \\
$p \lor q \tautEq q \lor p$ & Commutativity of $\lor$ \\
$p \liff q \tautEq q \liff p$ & Commutativity of $\liff$ \\
$p \land (q \land r) \tautEq (p \land q) \land r$ & Associativity\index{associativity} of $\land$ \\
$p \lor (q \lor r) \tautEq (p \lor q) \lor r$ & Associativity of $\lor$ \\
$p \liff (q \liff r) \tautEq (p \liff q) \liff r$ & Associativity of $\liff$ \\
$p \land (q \lor r) \tautEq (p \land q) \lor ( p \land r )$ & Distributivity\index{distributivity} of $\land$ over $\lor$ \\
$p \lor (q \land r) \tautEq (p \lor q) \land ( p \lor r )$ & Distributivity of $\lor$ over $\land$ \\
$p \limplies q \tautEq \lnot q \limplies \lnot p$ & Contrapositive\index{contrapositive} (or Transposition) \\
$p \limplies ( q \limplies r) \tautEq (p \land q) \limplies r$ & Exportation\index{exportation}
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
}

\paragraph*{Some tautologies often used as axioms.}
Next we list some tautologies that are also used as axioms
for Hilbert-style propositional proof systems. They are stated
with explicit variables $p,q,r$; however, generally a
Hilbert-style system allows any \idxnm{substitution instance} of an \idxnm{axiom}
to be an axiom.

There are many other possible axioms that have been used for 
propositional proof systems; only a few of them are listed below.

Chapter~\ref{chap:PropProofs} defines a Hilbert-style
proof system $\PL$ that uses (all substitution instances of)
the first four tautologies below as its axioms.
For the second axiom (among others),
be careful to understand how parentheses should be added to make it
a fully parenthesized formula!
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{c@{~~~-~}l}
$p \limplies (q \limplies p)$ & An axiom for $\limplies$. An axiom of~$\PL$.\index{PL, proof system!axioms} \\
$(p\limplies q \limplies r) \limplies (p\limplies q) \limplies (p\limplies r)$ & An axiom for $\limplies$. An axiom of~$\PL$ \\
$ \lnot p \limplies (p \limplies q)$ & An axiom for $\lnot$. An axiom of~$\PL$ \\
$ (\lnot p \limplies p) \limplies p$ & An axiom for $\lnot$. An axiom of~$\PL$ \\
$ (p \limplies q) \limplies (p \limplies \lnot q) \limplies \lnot p$ & An axiom for $\lnot$ \\
$ p\land q \limplies p$ & An axiom for $\land$ \\
$ p\land q \limplies q$ & An axiom for $\land$ \\
$ p \limplies q \limplies p\land q$ & An axiom for $\land$ \\
$p \limplies p\lor q$ & An axiom for $\lor$ \\
$q \limplies p\lor q$ & An axiom for $\lor$ \\
$(p\limplies r) \limplies (q\limplies r) \limplies (p\lor q \limplies r)$
     & An axiom for $\lor$\label{eq:lastOrAxiom} \\
$( p \limplies q) \limplies (r\lor p) \limplies (r \lor q)$ & \parbox[t]{2in}{Principle of Summation \newline
   \hspace*{1em}(from {\em Principia Mathematica})} \\
$((p\limplies q) \limplies p) \limplies p$ & \idxnm{Peirce's Law}
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\paragraph*{Tautological equivalences defining $\lor$, $\land$ and $\liff$.}
The following three tautological equivalences can be used to define $\lor$, $\land$
and~$\liff$ in terms of $\lnot$ and~$\limplies$. This corresponds to the fact
that $\{\lnot, \limplies\}$
is an \idxnm{adequate} set of connectives,
as will be discussed later in Section~\ref{sec:booleanFunctions}.
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{c@{~~~-~}l}
$p \lor q \tautEq \lnot p \limplies q$ & Definition of $\lor$ in $\PL$ \\
$p \land q \tautEq \lnot (p \limplies \lnot q)$ & Definition of $\land$ in $\PL$ \\
$p \liff q \tautEq (p \limplies q) \land (q \limplies p)$ & Definition of $\liff$ in $\PL$
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\paragraph*{Some tautological implications often used for inferences.}
Any valid tautological implication can be used for inferences; thus there are
many possible valid inference rules.  Some of the common
ones include the following:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{c@{~~~-~}l}
$ p,\ p\limplies q \vDash q$ & \idxnm{Modus Ponens} \\
$\lnot q, \, p\limplies q \vDash \lnot p$ & \idxnm{Modus Tollens} \\
$ p\limplies q, \, q\limplies r \vDash p \limplies r$ & \idxnm{Hypothetical Syllogism}
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
The proof system $\PL$ will allow only Modus Ponens as an inference rule (see Definition~\ref{def:PLproof} in
the next chapter).
A \idxnm{Modus Ponens} inference will be written in the form
\begin{prooftree}
\AxiomC{$A$}
\AxiomC{$A\limplies B$}
\BinaryInfC{$B$}
\end{prooftree}
where $A$ and~$B$ are permitted to be any well-formed formulas.
This inference rule means that if the two formulas $A$ and $A\limplies B$
have already been inferred, then $B$ may be inferred as well.

Similarly, \idxnm{Modus Tollens} and \idxnm{Hypothetical Syllogism} correspond to the
inference rules
\begin{center}
\AxiomC{$A\limplies B$}
\AxiomC{$\lnot B$}
\BinaryInfC{$\lnot A$}
\DisplayProof
\hspace*{2em} and \hspace*{2em}
\AxiomC{$A\limplies B$}
\AxiomC{$B\limplies C$}
\BinaryInfC{$A\limplies C$}
\DisplayProof
\end{center}
Although these will not be actual rules of inference for~$\PL$, 
they will be seen to be ``derived\index{derived rule}'' rules 
of inference in
Corollaries \ref{coro:HypSyl} and~\ref{coro:modustollens}.
