\section{Satisfaction and Logical Implication}\label{sec:implication}

The definition of truth for first-order formulas was just given in the
previous section. This lets us define the notions of
``satisfiability'', ``logical validity'' and ``logical implication''.
We start with validity.

\paragraph*{Validity of first-order formulas.}
We assume that a language~$L$ has been fixed,
and all formulas are $L$-formulas and structures are $L$-structures.

\begin{definition}
Let $A$ be a first-order formula.  We say $A$ is \emph{logically valid},
or just \idxem{valid} for short, and
write $\vDash A$, provided $\mathfrakA \vDash A [\sigma]$ holds
for all structures~$\mathfrakA$ and object assignments~$\sigma$.

If $A$ is a first-order sentence, we equivalently have that
$A$~is \idxem{valid}, written $\vDash A$ provided that
for all structures~$\mathfrakA$, we have $\mathfrakA \vDash A$.
\end{definition}


\begin{example}
Let $L$ contain the binary predicate~$P$ and the unary function~$f$.
Then
\begin{eqnarray*}
&& \vDash \forall x (x=x) \\
&& \vDash \forall x\, \exists y (x=y) \\
&& \vDash \forall x\, \forall y (x=y \land P(x,z) \limplies P(y,z)) \\
&& \vDash \forall x\, \forall y (f(x)=f(y)) \limplies \forall x (f(x)=f(f(x))) .
\end{eqnarray*}
All these examples are about properties of equality. Note that the first, second, and fourth formulas
are sentences, whereas the third one contains the free variable~$z$ and thus is not
a sentence.  One could equivalently write
\[
\vDash \forall z\, \forall x\, \forall y (x=y \land P(x,z) \limplies P(y,z)) .
\]
As we define next, this is called a ``\idxnm{generalization}'' of
$\forall x\, \forall y\, (x=y \land\penalty10000 P(x,z) \limplies\penalty10000  P(y,z))$.
\end{example}

\begin{definition}\label{def:generalization}
Suppose $A$ is a formula and $x_{i_1},\ldots, x_{i_k}$ is a sequence
of variables, with $k\ge 0$.  The formula
\begin{equation}\label{eq:generalizationDef}
\forall x_{i_1}\, \forall x_{i_2}\, \cdots\, \forall x_{i_k} A
\end{equation}
is called a \idxem{generalization} of~$A$. If the
variables $x_{i_1}, \ldots, x_{i_k}$ are distinct and enumerate
the variables that appear free in~$A$, then (\ref{eq:generalizationDef})
is called the \idxem{universal closure} of~$A$.\footnote{Strictly
speaking, it should be called {\emph a} universal closure of~$A$, since
there can be multiple ways to order the variables $x_{i_1},\ldots,x_{i_k}$.
However, it is more common to call it {\em the} universal closure. At any
rate, no matter how the variables are ordered, the universal closures are
all logically equivalent to each other. If necessary, the definition of
universal closure could be disambiguated by requiring $i_1<i_2 <\cdots < i_k$.}
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:genValidIff}
Let $A$ be a formula and $x_i$ be a variable.
Then $\vDash A$ holds if and only if
$\vDash \forall x_i A$. Therefore, if $B$ is a generalization of~$A$,
then $\vDash A$ holds if and only if $\vDash B$ holds.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $B$ be $\forall x_i\, A$.  Then $\vDash B$ holds if and only if
for all structures~$\mathfrakA$, and all $x_i$-variants~$\tau$ of all
object assignments~$\sigma$, we have $\mathfrakA \vDash A [\tau]$.
And $\vDash A$ holds if and only if, for all structures~$\mathfrakA$
and all objects assignments~$\tau$, we have $\mathfrakA \vDash A [\tau]$.
But these two ways of selecting~$\tau$ give the same possibilities
for~$\tau$. Therefore, $\vDash B$ is equivalent to $\vDash A$.

The second part of the theorem is proved by induction on the number~$k$
of quantifiers added to~$A$ in the generalization.
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:genValidIff} means that the question of
whether a formula~$A$ is valid can be reduced to the
question of whether the universal closure of~$A$ is
valid. The \idxnm{universal closure} of~$A$ is of course
a sentence. We will next discuss the notion of implication
between sentences, and only afterward define implication
between formulas.

\paragraph*{Logical implication from first-order sentences.}
Let $\Gamma$ be a set of sentences, and $A$~be either
a sentence or a general formula. We shall define
$\Gamma$ ``\idxnm{logically implies}''~$A$
whenever the truth of~$\Gamma$ implies the truth of~$A$.
This is denoted $\Gamma \vDash A$ using the same
notation as was used for tautological implication. However,
logical implication is a much more powerful notion than
tautological implication. We continue to work with
a fixed language~$L$. First, we define the crucial notion
of a ``\idxnm{model}'' of~$\Gamma$.

\begin{definition}\label{def:LogicalImplySent}
Let $\Gamma$ be a set of sentences and $\mathfrakA$
a structure. Then $\mathfrakA$ is called a \idxem{model} of~$\Gamma$,
written $\mathfrakA \vDash \Gamma$ if, for
every sentence $B\in \Gamma$, we have $\mathfrakA \vDash B$.

When this holds, we say that $\Gamma$~is \idxem{satisfied by}~$\mathfrakA$.
We also say that $\Gamma$~is \idxem{satisfiable}.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}
Let $\Gamma$ be a set of sentences and $A$~be a sentence.
Then $\Gamma$ \idxem{logically implies}~$A$ provided that
for every structure~$\mathfrakA$, if $\mathfrakA \vDash \Gamma$,
then $\mathfrakA \vDash A$.

More generally, suppose $\Gamma$ is a set of sentences
and $A$ is a formula. Then
Then $\Gamma$ \idxem{logically implies}~$A$ provided that
for every structure~$\mathfrakA$, if $\mathfrakA \vDash \Gamma$,
then $\mathfrakA \vDash A[\sigma]$ for every object assignment~$\sigma$.

We use the double turnstile\index{double turnstile@{double turnstile ($\vDash$)}}
notation $\Gamma \vDash A$ to denote that $\Gamma$ logically implies~$A$.
In this case, we also say that
$A$~is a \idxem{logical consequence} of~$\Gamma$.
\end{definition}

For the moment, the notation $\Gamma \vDash A$ is defined only for $\Gamma$
a set of {\em sentences}.  Definition~\ref{def:LogicalImplyFmla} below will 
extend the definition to allow $\Gamma$ to be a set of formulas.

Note that the double turnstile
sign\index{double turnstile@{double turnstile ($\vDash$)}},~$\vDash$ is being
used in two different (closely related) ways. The first way,
$\mathfrakA\vDash \Gamma$ or~$\mathfrakA \vDash A$, indicates what is
true in the single structure~$\mathfrakA$.
The second way, $\Gamma \vDash A$, indicates that $A$~follows
from~$\Gamma$ in all structures that satisfy~$\Gamma$.
That is, the first way is a statement about a single
structure~$\mathfrakA$; the second way is a statement about all possible
models of~$\Gamma$.  These two different usages are well-established but
potentially confusing. It is important to watch out for the
distinction between the two meanings.

Just as was done in propositional logic, it is common
to take liberties in notation by omitting set brackets, and writing things
like $A, B\vDash C$ and $\Gamma, A \vDash B$ instead
of $\{A,B\}\vDash C$ and $\Gamma \cup\{A\} \vDash B$.

\begin{definition}
Let $A$ and $B$ be sentences.  We say $A$ and~$B$ are
\emph{logically equivalent}\index{logically equivalent@{logically equivalent ($\tautEq$)}}
provided that both $A\vDash B$ and $B\vDash A$ hold. We write
$A \tautEq B$ to denote that $A$ and~$B$
are logically equivalent\index{logically equivalent@{logically equivalent ($\tautEq$)}}.
\end{definition}

We use the notation $\Gamma\nvDash A$ to indicate that $\Gamma$ does
not logically imply~$A$.

\begin{example}
Let $L$ contain the binary predicate~$P$ and the unary function~$f$.
Then
\begin{itemize}
\item[(a)] $\forall x\, \forall y\,P(x,y) \vDash P(x,f(x))$,
\item[(b)] $\forall x\, \forall y\,P(x,y) \vDash \forall x\, P(x,x)$,
\item[(c)] $\forall x\, \forall y\,P(x,y) \vDash \forall x\, P(x,f(x))$,
\item[(d)] $\forall x\, \forall y\, P(x,y) \tautEq \forall y\, \forall x\, P(x,y)$,
\item[(e)] $\exists y\, \forall x\, P(x,y) \vDash \forall x\, \exists y\, P(x,y)$, and
\item[(f)] $\forall x\, \exists y\, P(x,y) \nvDash \exists y\, \forall x\, P(x,y)$.
\end{itemize}
It is left to the reader to check that these logical implications
are correct. The non-implication of item~(f) will be discussed again in
Example~\ref{example:AEPEAP}.
\end{example}

Theorem~\ref{thm:genValidIff} also applies to logical implication
from a set of sentences:
\begin{theorem}\label{thm:genTautImpSent}
Suppose $\Gamma$ is a set of sentences, $A$ is a formula, and $\Gamma \vDash A$.
Let $B$ be a generalization of~$A$. Then $\Gamma \vDash B$.
\end{theorem}

The proof of this is essentially identical to the proof of
Theorem~\ref{thm:genValidIff}, and is left to the reader.

\begin{definition}
Let $\Gamma$ and $\Delta$ be sets of sentences. Then $\Gamma \vDash \Delta$ means
that $\Gamma \vDash A$ for every $A\in \Delta$. And, $\Gamma \tautEq \Delta$ means
that $\Gamma \vDash \Delta$ and $\Delta \vDash \Gamma$
\end{definition}

The same
notations can be used when $\Gamma$ and $\Delta$ are sets of formulas; this depends
on the next definitions.

\paragraph*{Logical implication from first-order formulas.}
Now we define what it means for $\Gamma$~to logically imply\index{logically implies}~$A$ 
when $\Gamma$ is a
set of formulas (instead of a set of sentences).
This definition is somewhat fraught because there
are two different conventions that might be adopted.
The definition we adopt is the most common one, and is:

\begin{definition}
Let $\Gamma$ be a set of formulas. Further let $\mathfrakA$ be
a structure and $\sigma$~be an object
assignment. Then the pair $(\mathfrakA,\sigma)$ \idxem{satisfies}~$\Gamma$,
written $\mathfrakA \vDash \Gamma[\sigma]$ if, for
every formula $B\in \Gamma$, we have $\mathfrakA \vDash B[\sigma]$.

When this holds, we say that $\Gamma$~is \idxem{satisfied by}
$(\mathfrakA,\sigma)$.
We also say that $\Gamma$~is \idxem{satisfiable}.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}\label{def:LogicalImplyFmla}
Let $\Gamma$ be a set of formulas and $A$~be a formula.
Then $\Gamma$~\idxem{logically implies}~$A$, written $\Gamma \vDash A$,
if and only if for every structure~$\mathfrakA$ and every object
assignment~$\sigma$, if $\mathfrakA \vDash \Gamma[\sigma]$
then $\mathfrakA \vDash A[\sigma]$.  In other words,
$\Gamma \vDash A$ holds provided that, for every
pair $(\mathfrakA,\sigma)$ satisfying~$\Gamma$,
we have $\mathfrakA \vDash A[\sigma]$.
When $\Gamma\vDash A$ holds, we also say that
$A$~is a \idxem{logical consequence} of~$\Gamma$.
\end{definition}

When $\Gamma$ is a set of sentences, there are two definitions
of logical implication ($\Gamma \vDash A$).  The first was given in
Definition~\ref{def:LogicalImplySent}; the second just given in
in Definition~\ref{def:LogicalImplyFmla}
since $\Gamma$ is also a set of formulas. (A sentence is always a
formula, but not necessarily vice-versa.)  It is easy to see that
the two definitions coincide for $\Gamma$ a set of sentences
since the truth of $\mathfrakA \vDash \Gamma [\sigma]$ does
not depend on~$\sigma$.

\begin{example} \label{example:logicImpls}
Let $P$~be a unary predicate symbol.
Some simple examples of logical implication and non-implication include:
\begin{itemizeLoose}
\item[(a)]  $x=y \vDash P(x) \liff P(y)$.

This holds, since if $\mathfrakA \vDash (x=y) [\sigma]$,
then $\sigma(x) = \sigma(y)$, so of course
$\sigma(x)\in\penalty10000 P^\mathfrakA$ if and only if $\sigma(y)\in P^\mathfrakA$.
\item[(b)] $P(x) \nvDash P(y)$.

This is because there are $(\mathfrakA, \sigma)$ such
that $\sigma(x) \neq \sigma(y)$, and
$\mathfrakA\vDash P(x)[\sigma]$ and $\mathfrakA\vDash \lnot P(y)[\sigma]$.
(See also Example~\ref{example:PxPy}
below.)
\item[(c)] $P(x) \nvDash \forall x \, P(x)$.

This holds for similar reasons as (b).
\item[(d)] $P(x) \vDash \exists x\, P(x)$.

If $\mathfrakA \vDash P(x) [\sigma]$, then the definition of truth implies
that $\mathfrakA \vDash \exists x\, P(x)[\sigma]$. (And, in fact, since $\exists x\, P(x)$
is a sentence,
we have $\mathfrakA \vDash \exists x\, P(x)$.)
\item[(e)] $x=y \vDash y=x$.

This follows from the definition of truth for the
formulas $x=y$ and $y=x$.
\end{itemizeLoose}
\end{example}

\begin{example}\label{example:prenexACI}
Here is a less simple example that will be useful later.  Suppose $A$ and~$C$ are
formulas and that the variable~$x$ does not appear free in~$C$.
Then
\begin{equation}
%\label{eq:prenexACI}
\forall x\,(C \limplies A) \tautEq C \limplies \forall x\, A .
\tag*{\hbox{(provided $x$ is not free in $C$)}}
\end{equation}
To prove this, let $\mathfrakA$ be a structure and $\sigma$~be an object assignment.
We must prove that $\mathfrakA\vDash \forall x\,(C \limplies A)[\sigma]$ holds
if and only if $\mathfrakA\vDash (C \limplies \forall x\, A)[\sigma]$ holds.

The proof splits into two cases. First, assume that
$\mathfrakA\nvDash C[\sigma]$. By the definition of
truth, $\mathfrakA\vDash (C\limplies \forall x\, A)[\sigma]$
holds.  Furthermore, since $x$ is not free in~$C$,
$\mathfrakA\nvDash C[\tau]$ for every $x$-variant~$\tau$
of~$\sigma$.  Therefore, $\mathfrakA\vDash (C \limplies A)[\tau]$
for every $x$-variant of~$\sigma$. Therefore,
once again by the definition of truth,
$\mathfrakA\vDash \forall x\,(C \limplies A)[\sigma]$ holds. That proves
the first case.

Second, assume $\mathfrakA\vDash C[\sigma]$. Since $x$ is not free~$C$,
$\mathfrakA\vDash C[\tau]$, for all $x$-variants~$\tau$ of~$\sigma$.
By the definition of truth, $\mathfrakA\vDash (C \limplies \forall x\, A)[\sigma]$ holds
if and only if $\mathfrakA\vDash (\forall x\, A)[\sigma]$. Similarly, for every $x$-variant~$\tau$
of~$\sigma$, $\mathfrakA\vDash (C \limplies A)[\tau]$ if and only if
$\mathfrakA\vDash A [\tau]$. Using the definition of truth twice, this means
that $\mathfrakA\vDash \forall x\,(C \limplies A)[\sigma]$ holds
if and only if $\mathfrakA\vDash \forall x\, A[\sigma]$. That completes
the second case. \hfill $\qed$

\medskip
This example is one of the results that will support the conversion
of formulas to prenex\index{prenex formula} form. For related results,
see Lemma~\ref{lem:prenexHelpers} later in this chapter.
\end{example}

The next theorem gives some simple observations about logical implication.
\begin{theorem}\label{thm:subsetFO}
Let $\Gamma$ and $\Pi$ be sets of formulas such that $\Gamma \subseteq \Pi$.
\begin{enumerate}
\item If $\Pi$ contains only sentences and $\mathfrakA \vDash \Pi$, then $\mathfrakA \vDash \Gamma$.
\item If $(\mathfrakA, \sigma)$ satisfies~$\Pi$, then it also satisfies~$\Gamma$.
\item If $\Pi$ is satisfiable, then $\Gamma$ is satisfiable.
\item If $\Gamma \vDash A$, then $\Pi \vDash A$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

Theorem~\ref{thm:subsetFO} is immediate from the definitions.

As mentioned earlier (on page~\pageref{page:forall_notexistsnot}), 
``$\forall x$'' has the same meaning as
``$\lnot \exists x \lnot$''. This is formalized by the next theorem; a slight
variation is proved later as Theorem~\ref{thm:negQnegBis}.
\begin{theorem}\label{thm:negQneg}
Let $A$ be a formula.
\begin{itemize}
\item[(a)] $\forall x\, A \tautEq \lnot \exists x \lnot A$.
\item[(b)] $\exists x\, A \tautEq \lnot \forall x \lnot A$.
\end{itemize}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We prove (b); the proof of~(a) is similar. We must show that
$\mathfrakA \vDash \exists x\, A[\sigma]$ holds if and only if
$\mathfrakA \vDash \lnot \forall x \lnot A[sigma]$ holds.  This is proved as
follows.
\begin{implicationsnarrow}
%\multicolumn{3}{l}{$\mathfrakA \vDash \lnot \forall x \lnot A[\sigma]$}  \\
$\mathfrakA \vDash \lnot \forall x \lnot A[\sigma]$
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $\mathfrakA \nvDash \forall x \lnot A[\sigma]$ \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & It is not true that $\mathfrakA \vDash \forall x \lnot A[\sigma]$ \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & It is not true that for all $x$-variants $\tau$ of $\sigma$, $\mathfrakA \vDash \lnot A[\tau]$ \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & For some $x$-variant~$\tau$ of $\sigma$, $\mathfrakA \nvDash \lnot A[\tau]$\\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & For some $x$-variant~$\tau$ of $\sigma$, $\mathfrakA \vDash A[\tau]$\\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $\mathfrakA \vDash \exists x\, A[\sigma]$. \qedhere
\end{implicationsnarrow}
\end{proof}

\paragraph*{The semantic deduction theorem.}\label{thm:deductionSemanticFO}
We now state the semantic version of the Deduction Theorem for
first-order logic. This is analogous to the Semantic Deduction
Theorem for propositional logic that was proved earlier as Theorem~\ref{thm:deductionSemanticProp}.
It is the {\em semantic} version of the actual Deduction Theorem,
which will be discussed in Section~\ref{sec:deductionInconsisFO}.
(The actual Deduction Theorem,
however, will apply to provability not to logical implication
and will require assuming that $A$ is a sentence.)

\begin{theorem}[Semantic Deduction Theorem]\label{thm:semanticDeductFO}
\index{Deduction Theorem!semantic}
Let $\Gamma$ be a set of formulas and $A$ and~$B$ be formulas.
Then $\Gamma \vDash A\limplies B$ holds if and only if $\Gamma,A \vDash B$
holds.
\end{theorem}

\noindent
Note that the theorem also holds for $\Gamma$ a set of sentences by the
remark after Definition~\ref{def:LogicalImplyFmla}.

\begin{proof}
By the definitions of truth and logical implication,
both $\Gamma \vDash A\limplies B$ and $\Gamma,A \vDash B$
are equivalent to the condition that for any structure $\mathfrakA$ and
object assignment~$\sigma$, if $\mathfrakA \vDash \Gamma[\sigma]$
and $\mathfrakA \vDash A [\sigma]$ then $\mathfrakA \vDash B[\sigma]$.
\end{proof}

\paragraph*{Duality between satisfiability and validity.}
Theorems \ref{thm:dualSatTaut} and~\ref{thm:ContradictionSemantic} established
a duality between tautological validity and satisfiability
for propositional formulas. The same kind of duality holds for
logical validity and satisfiability of first-order formulas.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:ContradictionSemanticFO}
Let $\Gamma$ be a set of formulas and let $A$ be a formula.
\begin{itemize}
\item[(a)] $\vDash A$ holds if and only if $\{\lnot A\}$ is unsatisfiable.
\item[(b)] $\Gamma \vDash A$ holds if and only if $\Gamma\cup \{\lnot A\}$ is unsatisfiable.
\end{itemize}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Part (a) is the special case of~(b) with $\Gamma = \varnothing$, so we need
only to prove part~(b).
Note that $\Gamma \vDash A$ means that for any pair $(\mathfrakA, \sigma)$,
if $\mathfrakA \vDash B[\sigma]$ for every $B\in \Gamma$ then $\mathfrakA \vDash A[\sigma]$.
Also, $\{\Gamma, \lnot A\}$ is unsatisfiable means that there is no pair $(\mathfrakA, \sigma)$
such that $\mathfrakA \vDash \lnot A[\sigma]$ and
such that $\mathfrakA \vDash B[\sigma]$ for all $B\in \Gamma$.
By the definition of truth for $\lnot A$, these two conditions are clearly equivalent.
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:ContradictionSemanticFO} is the semantic version
of the \idxnm{proof by contradiction} principles that will
be established later in Section~\ref{sec:deductionInconsisFO} for first-order
provability and inconsistency. One big difference, however, is that the
proof by contradiction principle of Section~\ref{sec:deductionInconsisFO} will require
that $A$ is a sentence.

\paragraph*{Tautologies in first-order logic.}
We have just defined the notions of logical validity
and logical implication. The adjective ``logical'' means that the
validity of the implication holds because of the combined meanings
of the propositional connectives ($\lnot,\land, \lor,\limplies,\liff$)
and the quantifiers ($\forall, \exists$).  We can also talk about
``tautological'' validity and ``tautological'' implication for
first-order formulas; loosely speaking, the adjective ``tautological''
means that the validity
or the implication holds just because of the meanings of the
propositional connectives. For example,
$\forall x P(x) \land \forall x Q(x)$ tautologically
implies $\forall x Q(x) \land \forall x P(x)$, just because of
the meaning of the connective~$\land$.

For the next definition, recall the definition of
substitution of formulas for propositional formulas
in Section~\ref{sec:substitutionProp}. The notation
$A(B_1,\ldots,B_k/p_1,\ldots,p_k)$ means the formula obtained by
replacing each occurrence
of~$p_i$ in~$A$ with~$B_i$.

\begin{definition}\label{def:tautologyFO}
Let $C$~be a propositional tautology, and suppose $C$ involves
only the propositional variables $p_1,\ldots, p_k$.
Let $B_1,\ldots, B_k$ be arbitrary first-order formulas.
Then $C(B_1,\ldots,B_k/p_1,\ldots,p_k)$ is called
a \idxem{tautology}.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}\label{def:tautImplyFO}
Let $\Gamma$ be a set $\{A_1,\ldots, A_k\}$ of first-order formulas
and $B$ be a first-order formula. Then $\Gamma$~\idxem{tautologically implies}~$B$
if
\[
A_1 \limplies A_2 \limplies \cdots \limplies A_k \limplies B
\]
is a tautology.

If $\Gamma$ is an infinite set, then $\Gamma$~tautologically implies~$A$
if some finite subset of~$\Gamma$ \idxem{tautologically implies}~$A$.\footnote{The
Compactness Theorem for propositional logic allows us to define tautological
implication for infinite sets~$\Gamma$ in this way.}\index{Compactness Theorem}
\end{definition}



\begin{example}\label{example:tautEquiv}
A few examples of tautological implication include
\begin{enumerateLoose}
\item $x=y \land y=z$ tautologically implies $y=z \land x=y$.
\item $x=y$ does not tautologically imply $y=x$.
\item $P(x) \lor Q(x)$ tautologically implies $Q(x) \lor P(x)$.
\item $\forall x\, (P(x)\lor Q(x))$ does not tautologically imply $\forall x\, (Q(x) \lor P(x))$.
\end{enumerateLoose}
In (b), the order of the
equality is reversed from $x=y$ to $y=x$; for this reason, it is
not tautologically implied. Of course, $x=y$ {\em logically} implies
$y=x$, but it does not {\em tautologically} imply it.

In (d), the subformula $P(x)\lor Q(x)$ is replaced by the
tautologically equivalent $Q(x)\lor P(x)$. However, the whole formula
$\forall x\, (P(x)\lor Q(x))$ is not tautologically equivalent to
$\forall x\, (Q(x) \lor P(x))$. This is because the tautological equivalence
is inside the scope of the quantifier~$\forall x$.

Of course, $\forall x\, (P(x)\lor Q(x))$ is {\em logically} equivalent to
$\forall x\, (Q(x) \lor P(x))$. This is a topic we will take up again
in Example~\ref{example:subEquivSubfmla}.
\end{example}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:tautImpliesEquiv}
Let $\Gamma$ be a (finite) set of first-order formulas, and $A$~be a first-order formula.
\begin{enumerate}
\item If $A$~is a tautology, then $\vDash A$.
\item If $\Gamma$ tautologically implies~$A$, then $\Gamma\vDash A$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
To prove~(a), suppose that $\mathfrakA$ is a structure and
$\sigma$~is an object assignment. We must show that $\mathfrakA\vDash A[\sigma]$.
Since $A$ is a tautology, $A$~is equal to
$C(B_1,\ldots,B_k/p_1,\ldots,p_k)$ for some propositional tautology~$C$
and first-order formulas $B_1,\ldots, B_k$.  Let $\varphi$~be the
(propositional) truth assignment defined by letting
\[
\varphi(p_i) ~=~ \left\{ \begin{array}{ll}
\True \quad & \hbox{if $\mathfrakA\vDash B_i[\sigma]$} \\
\False \quad & \hbox{if $\mathfrakA\nvDash B_i[\sigma]$} \\
\end{array}\right.
\]
We claim that for every subformula~$D$ of~$C$,
we have
\[
\varphi(D) = \True \qquad \hbox{if and only if} \qquad \mathfrakA\vDash D(B_1,\ldots,B_k/p_1,\ldots,p_k)[\sigma].
\]
This claim is proved by induction on the complexity of subformulas~$D$ of~$C$.
The base case is where $D$ is a single variable~$p_i$: the claim holds for these~$D$'s
by the definition of~$\varphi$.  The argument for the induction step breaks into
separate cases depending on whether the outermost connective of~$D$ is
a $\lnot$, $\lor$, $\land$, $\limplies$ or~$\liff$. But in all five cases, the
argument is trivial since the induction definition of truth for propositional
formulas exactly parallels the inductive definition of truth for first-order formulas
when the outermost connective is a propositional connective. We leave the details
of the induction step to the reader.

Taking $D$ to be the formula~$C$, the claim implies that $\varphi(C) = \True$
if and only if $\mathfrakA \vDash C[\sigma]$. Since $C$ is a tautology,
we certainly have $\varphi(C) = \True$. Therefore,  the claim gives $\mathfrakA \vDash C[\sigma]$.
That completes the proof of part~(a).

For part~(b), suppose $\Gamma$ contains the formulas $B_1,B_2,\ldots,B_k$
and
\[
B_1\limplies \cdots \limplies B_k \limplies A
\]
is a tautology.  By
part~(a), $\vDash B_1\limplies \cdots \limplies B_k \limplies A$.
So by the Semantic Deduction Theorem~\ref{thm:semanticDeductFO},
$B_1\ldots,B_k \vDash A$. Since $B_1,\ldots,B_k$ are in~$\Gamma$,
we thus have $\Gamma \vDash A$ as desired.
\end{proof}

\paragraph*{Substitution of logically equivalent subformulas.}
The next theorem states the meaning of a formula
does not change if a subformula is replaced
with a logically equivalent formula.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:subEquivSubfmla}
Let $A$ be a formula and $B$ be a subformula of~$A$. Let $C$ be a formula
that is logically equivalent to~$B$, i.e., $B \tautEq C$. Let $A^*$ be the formula obtained
by replacing its subformula~$B$ with~$C$. Then $A$ and~$A^*$ are logically
equivalent, i.e., $A \tautEq A^*$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{example}\label{example:subEquivSubfmla}
Example~\ref{example:tautEquiv} noted that $P(x)\lor Q(x)$
and $Q(x) \lor P(x)$ are tautologically equivalent; therefore
they are also logically equivalent (by Theorem~\ref{thm:tautImpliesEquiv}).
Thus,
$\forall x( P(x)\lor Q(x) )$ and $\forall x( Q(x)\lor P(x) )$
are logically equivalent.
\end{example}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:subEquivSubfmla}]
The proof will use induction on the complexity of~$A$, ignoring
the complexity of~$B$.  Suppose $D$ is a subformula of~$A$ that
has $B$ as a subformula; this includes the cases where $D$ is one of $A$ or~$B$.
Define $D^*$ to be the subformula that results after replacing
the subformula~$B$ in~$D$ with~$C$. On the other hand, suppose $D$
is a subformula of~$A$ that neither is a subformula of~$B$ nor
contains $B$ as a subformula; then let $D^*$ be~$D$.

We claim that $D \tautEq D^*$ for any subformula~$D$ of~$A$ that is
not a proper subformula of the subformula~$B$ in~$A$. This is proved by induction
on the complexity of the subformula~$D$. There are two base cases to consider.
The first base case is where $D$ is the same as~$B$, so $D^*$ is~$C$.
Thus $D\tautEq D^*$ since $B \tautEq C$.
The second base case is where $D^*$ is the same as~$D$; then of course
$D \tautEq D^*$.  In the induction steps,
$D$ is a subformula $\lnot D_1$ or $D_1 \circ D_2$ or
$\forall x_i\, D_1$ or $\exists x_i D_1$, and
$D^*$ is the corresponding subformula
$\lnot D_1^*$ or $D_1^* \circ D_2^*$ or
$\forall x_i\, D_1^*$ or $\exists x_i D_1^*$.
In the case where $D$ is $\lnot D_1$ and $D^*$~is $\lnot D_1^*$, the induction
hypothesis states that $D_1 \tautEq D_1^*$ and the definition
of truth gives $D \tautEq D^*$.
In the case where $D$ is $D_1 \circ D_2$ and $D^*$~is $D_1^* \circ D_2^*$,
there are two induction hypotheses, stating
that $D_1 \tautEq D_1^*$ and $D_2 \tautEq D_2^*$;
the definition of truth again gives $D \tautEq D^*$.

In the two remaining cases, $D$~is $\forall x_i\, D_1$ or $\exists x_i D_1$,
and $D^*$~is $\forall x_i\, D_1^*$ or $\exists x_i D_1^*$.
From the definition of truth, the truth value of $\mathfrakA \vDash D[\sigma]$
depends only on the truth values of $\mathfrakA \vDash D_1 [\tau]$,
where $\tau$ ranges over all
$x_i$-variants of~$\sigma$.  By the induction hypothesis,
the truth value of $\mathfrakA \vDash D_1 [\tau]$ is the same as
the truth value of $\mathfrakA \vDash D_1^* [\tau]$. Therefore,
$\mathfrakA \vDash D [\sigma]$ if and only if
$\mathfrakA \vDash D^* [\sigma]$. Since this holds for all structures~$\mathfrakA$
and object assignments~$\sigma$, we have proved $D \tautEq D^*$.
\end{proof}

As a simple corollary, Theorem~\ref{thm:negQneg} on quantifiers and
negations can be restated as:

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:negQnegBis}
Let $A$ be a formula.
\begin{itemize}
\item[(a)] $\lnot \forall x\, A \tautEq \exists x \lnot A$.
\item[(b)] $\lnot \exists x\, A \tautEq \forall x \lnot A$.
\end{itemize}
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Since $A$ and~$\lnot\lnot A$ are tautologically equivalent, and hence logically
equivalent, Theorem~\ref{thm:subEquivSubfmla} tells us
that $\lnot \forall x \, A \tautEq \lnot \forall x \, \lnot \lnot A$.
And by Theorem~\ref{thm:negQneg}(b), $\lnot \forall x \, \lnot \lnot A$
is logically equivalent to $\exists x \lnot A$.  That proves~(a);
the proof of~(b) is similar.
\end{proof}
