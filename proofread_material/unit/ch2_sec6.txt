\section{Soundness and Completeness Theorems for $\PL$}\label{sec:SoundCompletePL}

The Soundness and Completeness Theorems express the fact that $\PL$ is a good
proof system for propositional logic.  The \idxnm{Soundness Theorem} states that
$\PL$ is ``\idxnm{sound}'' in the sense that any $\PL$-theorem~$A$ is a tautology.
Furthermore, it states that if $A$ is provable from a set~$\Gamma$ of hypotheses, then
$\Gamma$ tautologically implies~$A$. This is a basic property for
any system of propositional logic, since we of course only want to
have proofs of formulas that are tautologically implied.

The \idxnm{Completeness Theorem} states the converse, namely that $\PL$ is ``\idxnm{complete}''.
This means firstly that if $A$ is
a tautology, then $A$ has a $\PL$-proof. And, secondly, that if
$A$ is a tautological consequence of~$\Gamma$, then $\Gamma \vdash A$.
In other words, $\PL$ with its four axiom schemes, PL1-PL4, and with the
sole rule of inference of Modus Ponens is strong enough to give
proofs of all tautologies. This is fairly remarkable!

We now state the Soundness and Completeness Theorems more carefully and
prove the Soundness Theorem. After that, Section~\ref{sec:completePL} will
present the more difficult proof of the Completeness Theorem.

\begin{theorem}[\idxnm{Soundness Theorem} for $\PL$]
\label{thm:soundPL} ~~ \ ~
\begin{itemize}
\item[\rm (a)] If $\Gamma$ is satisfiable, then $\Gamma$ is consistent.
\item[\rm (b)] If $\Gamma \vdash A$, then $\Gamma \vDash A$.
\end{itemize}
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[\idxnm{Completeness Theorem} for $\PL$]
\label{thm:completePL} ~~ \ ~
\begin{itemize}
\item[\rm (a)] If $\Gamma$ is consistent, then $\Gamma$ is satisfiable.
\item[\rm (b)] If $\Gamma \vDash A$, then $\Gamma \vdash A$.
\end{itemize}
\end{theorem}

Taken together, the Soundness and Completeness Theorems state that
satisfiability is equivalent to consistency. They also state that
$\vDash$~(tautological implication) is equivalent to $\vdash$~(provability).
In other words, $\Gamma\vdash A$ is equivalent to $\Gamma\vDash A$.
In the special case with $\Gamma$ the empty set, $\vdash A$ is equivalent
to $\vDash A$. In other words, $A$~has a proof in~$\PL$ if and only if
$A$~is a tautology.

The Completeness Theorem allows us to introduce a new \idxnm{derived rule} of
inference for $\PL$ called
Tautological Implication\index{Tautological Implication (TAUT)}. For this, suppose
$A_1,\ldots, A_k \vDash B$. Then the following Tautological Implication
(TAUT) is admissible as a \idxnm{derived rule} for $\PL$-proofs:
\begin{prooftree}
\LeftLabel{TAUT:}
\AxiomC{$A_1$} \AxiomC{$A_2$} \AxiomC{$\cdots$} \AxiomC{$A_k$}
\QuaternaryInfC{$B$}
\end{prooftree}
To prove that TAUT is an admissible inference rule when
$A_1,\ldots, A_k \vDash B$, note that
the Completeness Theorem implies that $A_1,\ldots, A_k \vdash B$.
So $k$ uses of the Deduction Theorem imply that
$\vdash A_1 \limplies A_2 \limplies \cdots \limplies A_k \limplies B$.
Then, if the hypotheses $A_1$, \ldots, $A_k$ have been derived, $k$~uses
of Modus Ponens allows $B$ to be derived.

\idxnm{Hypothetical Syllogism} and \idxnm{Modus Tollens} (and also
\idxnm{Modus Ponens}) are special cases of the Tautological Implication
rule. In fact, by the Soundness and Completeness Theorems,
the Tautological Implication\index{Tautological Implication (TAUT)}
rule is the strongest possible admissible rule
of inference for~$\PL$.

\bigskip

In both the Soundness and Completeness Theorems, part~(a) and part~(b) easily
imply each other.  Let's start by proving that part~(a) of the Soundness Theorem
implies part~(b).  For this, we note that
\begin{implications}
\multicolumn{3}{l}{$\Gamma \vdash A$} \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $\Gamma\cup\{ \lnot A \}$ is inconsistent & Proof by Contradiction, Theorem~\ref{thm:proofbyContra1} \\
& $\Rightarrow$ & $\Gamma\cup\{ \lnot A \}$ is unsatisfiable & Soundness Theorem~\ref{thm:soundPL}, part (a) \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $\Gamma\vDash A$ & Theorem~\ref{thm:ContradictionSemantic}
\end{implications}
That finishes the proof of (b) from~(a) for the Soundness Theorem.
For the \idxnm{Completeness Theorem}, we argue similarly:
\begin{implications}
\multicolumn{3}{l}{$\Gamma \vDash A$} \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $\Gamma\cup\{ \lnot A \}$ is unsatisfiable & Theorem~\ref{thm:ContradictionSemantic} \\
& $\Rightarrow$ & $\Gamma\cup\{ \lnot A \}$ is inconsistent & Completeness Theorem \ref{thm:completePL}, part (a) \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $\Gamma\vdash A$ & Proof by Contradiction, Theorem~\ref{thm:proofbyContra1}
\end{implications}
That finishes the proof that part~(a) of the Completeness Theorem implies part~(b).

We now prove the Soundness Theorem.
\begin{proof}[Proof of the Soundness Theorem for $\PL$]\index{Soundness Theorem}
Suppose $\Gamma$ is a set of formulas, and $\Gamma \vdash A$.
We shall prove that $\Gamma \vDash A$. (Essentially, we are
proving part~(b) of the Soundness Theorem, and will then use that
to prove part~(a).) Let
\[
A_1,\, A_2,\, \ldots, A_\ell
\]
be a $\PL$-proof of~$A$. Suppose $\varphi$ is a truth assignment
satisfying~$\Gamma$. We need to prove that $\varphi(A) = \True$.
For this, we will prove that $\varphi(A_i) = \True$ for
$i=1,\ldots,\ell$, by induction on~$i$. Since $A_\ell$ is~$A$, this will imply
that $\varphi(A) = \True$. 

There are two base cases for the proof by induction. If $A_i$ is
a $\PL$-axiom, then $A_i$ is a tautology, so of course $\varphi(A_i) = \True$.
If $A \in \Gamma$, then $\varphi(A_i) = \True$ since $\varphi$ satisfies~$\Gamma$.
For the induction step, suppose $A_i$ is inferred by Modus Ponens
from $A_j$ and~$A_k$ where $j,k<i$, and $A_k$~is equal to
$A_j\limplies A_i$. The two induction hypotheses are that
$\varphi(A_j) = \True$ and $\varphi(A_j{\limplies} A_i) = \True$.
By the definition of truth for the formula $A_j\limplies A_i$, we must
thus have $\varphi(A_i) = \True$.  That completes the proof by
induction.

Now we can prove part~(a) of the Soundness Theorem. Suppose $\Gamma$ is
inconsistent. Then $\Gamma \vdash A$ and $\Gamma \vdash \lnot A$. By
the previous two paragraphs, $\Gamma \vDash A$ and $\Gamma \vDash \lnot A$
for some formula~$A$.
Suppose $\varphi$ is a truth assignment satisfying~$\Gamma$. By
$\Gamma \vDash A$, $\varphi(A) = \True$. Similarly, by
$\Gamma \vDash \lnot A$, $\varphi(\lnot A) = \True$. This is impossible.
Therefore, there is no $\varphi$ that satisfies~$\Gamma$; i.e., $\Gamma$~is
unsatisfiable.  That proves part~(a), and finishes the proof
of the Soundness Theorem for~$\PL$.
\end{proof}
