\section{Four Theories of Arithmetic}\label{sec:fourTheoriesArith}

We now define three axiomatizations for arithmetic.
There are two quite weak theories, called $\TQ$ and~$\TR$. We
also define the quite strong theory $\PA$ of Peano Arithmetic.
These are all subtheories of~$\TTh {\mathcal N}$, so they
give partial axiomatizations of truth over the integers.\footnote{By ``integer'',
we continue to always mean ``nonnegative integer''.} This gives four theories
$\TR$, $\TQ$, $\PA$ and~$\TTh {\mathcal N}$, in order of increasing strength.

All four theories use the language $L_\PA = \{0, S, +, \cdot \}$.
As usual, we write $+$ and~$\cdot$ in infix notation. We often omit
parentheses when applying the successor function~$S$. For example
$S0$ and $SSx$ are shorthand notations for $S(0)$ and $S(S(x))$;
the intuition is that they denote $1$ and $x+2$.

\begin{definition}\index{Q@{$\TQ$}}
The theory~$\TQ$ is the theory with the following seven axioms
\begin{itemizeLoose}
\item[$\TQ_1$:] $\forall x\, \forall y\, ( Sx =Sy \limplies x=y )$
\item[$\TQ_2$:] $\forall x\, ( Sx \neq 0 )$
\item[$\TQ_3$:] $\forall x\, (x \neq 0 \limplies \exists y\, (Sy = x) )$
\item[$\TQ_4$:] $\forall x \, ( x+0 = x ) $
\item[$\TQ_5$:] $\forall x \, \forall y\, (x+Sy = S(x+y) )$
\item[$\TQ_6$:] $\forall x \, ( x \cdot 0 = 0 ) $
\item[$\TQ_7$:] $\forall x \, \forall y\, (x \cdot Sy = xy + x)$
\end{itemizeLoose}
\end{definition}
The first three axioms give properties of the \idxnm{successor} function~$S$.
Since $S$ is a function symbol, every object has a unique successor.
Axioms $\TQ_1$ and~$\TQ_3$ state that every nonzero element
has a unique \idxnm{predecessor}. Axiom~$\TQ_2$ states that
0~has no predecessor; this reflects the fact that we are axiomatizing
the nonnegative integers.

Axioms $\TQ_4$ and~$\TQ_5$ give a kind of characterization of addition $x+y$
based on induction on~$y$.
Axioms $\TQ_6$ and~$\TQ_7$ give a similar characterization of multiplication.
For instance, informally speaking, $\TQ_7$~states that $x\cdot (y+1) = xy + x$.

As we shall see, $\TQ$~is strong enough to represent all Turing decidable
sets and functions; nonetheless $\TQ$ is very weak. The much stronger
theory of Peano Arithmetic~($\PA$) is obtained by adding induction axioms
for every formula $A(x)$:
\begin{definition}\label{induction axiom}
Let $A=A(x)$ be a formula. The \idxnm{induction axiom} $\TInd_A$ is 
the universal closure of the statement
\[
A(0) \land \forall x\, (A(x) \limplies A(Sx))  \limplies \forall x\, A(x).
\]
\end{definition}
\begin{example}\label{example:Inds}
Let $A(x)$ be the formula $0+x = x$. The induction
axiom for~$A(x)$ is
\begin{description}
\item[$\TInd_A$:\hspace*{1em}] $0+0=0  \land \forall x\, (0+x=x \limplies 0+Sx = Sx)
               \limplies \forall x\,(0+x =x)$.
\end{description}
For an example where there are free variables other than~$x$,
let $B(x)$ be $x+\penalty10000 y =\penalty10000 y+\penalty10000 x$. Then the induction axiom
for~$B(x)$ is the sentence
\begin{description}
\item[$\TInd_B$:\hspace*{1em}] $\forall y\, [ 0+y = y+0 \land
       \forall x\, (x+y=y+x \limplies Sx+y = y+Sx)
        \limplies \forall x\, (x+y = y+x) ]$.
\end{description}
\end{example}
\begin{definition}
The theory $\PA$ of Peano Arithmetic is axiomatized with
the seven axioms of~$\TQ$ plus the induction
axioms $\TInd_A$ for every $L_\PA$-formula~$A$.
\end{definition}

\begin{example}\label{example:PAzeroxx}
We prove that the induction axioms enable $\PA$
to prove $\forall x\, (0+x = x)$.
Let $A$ be $0+x=x$, so that that
$\TInd_A$ is the formula displayed above in
Example~\ref{example:Inds}. It is obvious that $\PA \vdash 0=0$, since that is
an instance of an equality axiom.  Second, we
claim that $\PA$ proves $0+x=x \limplies 0+Sx =\penalty10000 Sx$.
By the refined Deduction Theorem~\ref{thm:refinedDeductionSyntacticFO}(b)
it will suffice to prove that $\PA, 0{+}x{=}x \vdash 0+Sx=Sx$
without using generalization on the variable~$x$. This is easily proved
by reasoning as follows:
\begin{threecolproof}
$0+Sx~=~$ & $S(0+x)$ & By axiom $\TQ_5$ \\
$=~$ & $Sx$ & By the induction hypothesis that $0+x=x$ .
\end{threecolproof}
We have shown that $\PA$ proves the hypotheses
of $\TInd_A$. Therefore, $\PA$~proves
its conclusion, $\forall x\, (0+x= x)$.
\end{example}

The theory $\TQ$ is too weak even to prove that
$0+x =x$. Exercise~\ref{exer:QzeroPlusx} asks for
a proof of this.  Therefore, $\TQ$ is a proper subtheory
of~$\PA$.

The next definition is needed
before we can define the third theory~$\TR$.
\begin{definition}
Let $n\ge 0$ be an integer. The notation $S^{(n)} t$ denotes the
term $S(S(\cdots S(S(t))\cdots ))$ where there are $n$~many
applications of~$S$.   For $n=0$, $S^{(n)}t$ is just the term~$t$.

The \idxem{numeral}~$\underline n$ is the term $S^{(n)}0$.
\end{definition}

Thus, $S^{(3)}x$ is the term $S(S(S(x)))$, and $S^{(4)}0$ is
the term $S(S(S(S(4))))$. These are often written
as $SSSx$ and $SSSS0$ for short.  The numerals $\underline 0$,
$\underline 1$, $\underline 2$ and~$\underline 3$ are equal to
the terms $0$, $S0$, $SS0$ and $SSS0$, etc. Of course, in
the standard model, these terms represent the integers $0,1,2,3,\ldots$.
It is important to note that the notations $S^{(n)} t$ and
$\underline n$ are used {\em only} if $n$~denotes a particular
integer.  That is to say, the terms $S^{(n)} t$ and
$\underline n$ do not involve~$n$ as a variable; instead, $n$~is hardcoded
in these terms via $n$~many appearances of the unary function symbol~$S$.

\begin{example}\label{ex:ThreePlusTwo}
The atomic formula $\underline 3 + \underline 2$ is the same
as $SSS0+SS0$. The theory~$\TQ$ can prove $\underline 3 + \underline 2 = \underline 5$
by reasoning as follows:
\begin{align*}
SSS0 + SS0 &~=~ S(SSS0+S0) && \hbox{by Axiom~$\TQ_5$} \\
&~=~ SS(SSS0+0) && \hbox{by Axiom~$\TQ_5$ again} \\
&~=~ SSSSS0 && \hbox{by Axiom $\TQ_4$}
\end{align*}
Similar reasoning shows that $\TQ$ can prove $\underline m + \underline n = \underline{m+n}$
for all $m,n \in \mathbb N$.
For this, see Theorem~\ref{thm:QimpliesR}.  % XXX CHECK IF BEST REFERECNE
\end{example}

\begin{notation}\label{notate:le}
For the rest of this chapter, the notation $s\le t$
denotes the formula $\exists x\, (x+s = t)$
where $x$ is a variable that does not appear in $s$ or~$t$.
The notation $s < t$
denotes the formula $s\le t \land s\neq t$,
namely the formula
$\exists x\, (x+\penalty10000 s =\penalty10000 t) \land\penalty10000  s\neq t$, where
again $x$~does not appear in $s$ or~$t$.
\end{notation}

We can view $\le$ as being added to the language~$\PA$
via an extension by definitions in the sense of
Definition~\ref{def:extensionByDefinition}. From
Theorem~\ref{thm:defPredSym}, it does not
change the power of a theory to add~$\le$ as a defined symbol.

The point of including~$\le$ as a defined symbol is that it
makes the axioms of~$\TR$ much more natural to state. In this regard, it is
important $\exists x\, (x+s= t)$ is used instead of
$\exists x\,(s+x=t)$ as the definition
of $s\le t$: this makes a difference because $\TQ$~cannot prove $x+s=s+x$.

Now that we have defined the weak theory~$\TQ$, we also define
the even weaker theory~$\TR$.

\begin{definition}\index{R@{$\TR$}}
The theory~$\TR$ uses the language $\{0, S, +, \cdot \}$
and has the following infinite set of axioms.
\begin{itemizeLoose}
\item[$\TR_{\textstyle\neq}$:] For each $n \neq m \in \mathbb N$, the
formula $\underline n \neq \underline m$ is
an axiom of~$\TR$.
\item[$\TR_{\textstyle+}$:] For each $n, m \in \mathbb N$, the formula
$\underline n + \underline m = \underline {n+m}$ is
an axiom of~$\TR$.
\item[$\TR_\bullet$:]  For each $n, m \in \mathbb N$, the formula
$\underline n \cdot \underline m = \underline {n\cdot m}$ is
an axiom of~$\TR$.
\item[$\TR_{\textstyle\le}$:] For each $n\in \mathbb N$,
the formula $\forall x\, (x\le \underline n \lor \underline n < x)$
is an axiom of~$\TR$.
\item[$\TR_{\textstyle\le}^\prime$:] For each $n\in \mathbb N$,
the formula
\[
\forall x \, [\, x\le \underline n \,\limplies\, x=\underline 0 \lor x = \underline 1 \lor
       \,\cdots\, \lor x=\underline {n{-}1} \lor x = \underline n \,]
\]
is an axiom of~$\TR$.
\end{itemizeLoose}
Recall that $r \le s$ is an abbreviation for $\exists x(x+r=s)$ and that
$r<s$ is an abbreviation for $r\le s \land r\neq s$.
\end{definition}

\begin{example}
With $n=3$ and $m=2$, the following are axioms of~$\TR$:

\vbox{
\[
SSS0 \neq SS0 \qquad SS0+SSS0=SSSSS0
\]
\[SS0 \cdot SSS0 = SSSSSS0
\qquad \forall x\,(x\le SSS0 \lor SSS0\le x)
\]
\[
\forall x\,(x\le SSS0 \limplies x=0 \lor x=S0 \lor x=SS0 \lor x=SSS0).
\]
}
\end{example}
The theory~$\TR$ is really very weak. Indeed, it does not
prove any of the axioms of~$\TQ$.  As a small example of something
that can be proved in~$\TR$, we have
\begin{theorem}\label{thm:RleAndlt}
\ ~ \hbox{\relax}
\begin{itemizeLoose}
\item[(a)] Let $m\le n \in \mathbb N$. Then $\TR \vdash \underline m \le \underline n$.
\item[(b)] Let $n\in \mathbb N$. $\TR \vdash
    \forall x\,(x\le \underline n \liff x=\underline 0 \lor x = \underline 1 \lor
       \cdots \lor x=\underline {n{-}1} \lor x = \underline n)$.
\item[(c)] Let $n\in \mathbb N$. $\TR \vdash
    \forall x\,(x < \underline n \liff x=\underline 0 \lor x = \underline 1 \lor
       \cdots \lor x=\underline {n{-}1})$.
\item[(d)]  Let $n\in \mathbb N$. $\TR \vdash \forall x\,( x<\underline n \lor x=\underline n \lor \underline n < x )$.
\end{itemizeLoose}
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
To prove part~(a), let $m\le n$ and set $p = n-m \ge 0$. By
the axiom~$\TR_{\textstyle+}$, the theory $\TR$~proves that
$\underline p + \underline m = \underline n$.
Therefore, by the definition of~$\le$ (see Notation~\ref{notate:le}),
$\TR$ proves $\underline m \le \underline n$.

The forward implication of~(b) is just Axiom~$\TR_{\textstyle\le}^\prime$. The reverse
implication is immediate from part~(a).

The forward implication of~(c) follows from part~(b)
and the definition of~$<$
in Notation~\ref{notate:le}. The reverse implication of~(c)
follows from the definition of~$<$, part~(b), and from
the fact that Axiom~$\TR_{\textstyle\neq}$ implies that
$\TR\vdash \underline  m \neq \underline n$ for all $m<n$.

Part~(d) follows directly from $\TR_{\textstyle{\le}}$
and parts (b) and~(c).
\end{proof}
