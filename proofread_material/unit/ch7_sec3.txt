\section{Representability}

To state results in full generality,
we let $T$ be a consistent theory in the language $L_\PA = \{0, S, +, \cdot\}$.
Generally, $T$ will contain~$\TQ$, or at least~$\TR$, as a subtheory.
However, it is not required that $T$ is a subtheory of~$\TTh {\mathcal N}$.
In other words, $T$~must be consistent but 
might contain sentences that are not true in~$\mathcal N$.

Informally, a relation~$R$ or function~$f$ is ``\idxnm{representable}'' in~$T$ if every
particular numeric instance of $R$ or~$f$ is provable in~$T$. (For this reason,
the terminology ``numeral-wise representable'' is sometimes used instead.)
This is formally defined as follows.

\begin{definition}\label{def:representRelation}
A $k$-ary relation~$R$ on~$\mathbb N$ is \idxem{representable} in~$T$
if there is a formula $A_R(x_1, x_2,\ldots,x_k)$ such
that for all $n_1,\ldots,n_k$ in~$\mathbb N$,
\begin{itemizeLoose}
\item[(a)] If $R(n_1,n_2, \ldots, n_k)$ holds,
then $T \vdash A_R(\underline{n_1},\underline{n_2},\ldots,\underline{n_k})$, and
\item[(b)] If $R(n_1,n_2, \ldots, n_k)$ is false,
then $T \vdash \lnot A_R(\underline{n_1},\underline{n_2},\ldots,\underline{n_k})$.
\end{itemizeLoose}
In this case, we say that $R$~is \emph{represented}\index{represent} by~$A_R$ in~$T$.
\end{definition}
Since $T$ is consistent, it follows immediately from these conditions
that $T$~proves $A_R(\underline{n_1},\underline{n_2},\ldots,\underline{n_k})$ iff
$R(n_1,n_2, \ldots, n_k)$ is true. Similarly,
$T$~proves $\lnot A_R(\underline{n_1},\underline{n_2},\ldots,\underline{n_k})$ iff
$R(n_1,n_2, \ldots, n_k)$ is false.

\begin{example}\label{example:representEqLt}
As two simple examples, we show that the binary relations $=$ and~$\le$
are representable in the theory~$\TR$.  The formula~$A_{=}(x,y)$ that
represents~$=$ is the formula $x=y$. To prove that $A_{=}(x,y)$ represents
the binary equality relation~$=$, we must show two things: (a)~for
all $n\in \mathbb N$, $R\vdash \underline n = \underline n$, and (b)~for
all $n \neq m$, $R\vdash \lnot \underline n = \underline m$.  The first item,~(a),
is just an instance of an equality axiom. The second item,~(b), is
just an $\TR_{\textstyle\neq}$ axiom.

Now let $A_\le(x,y)$ be the formula $x\le y$; in other words, $\exists z(z + x = y)$.
To show that the formula~$A_\le (x,y)$ represents~$\le$, we must
show two things: (a)~for all $m\le n$, $\TR$~proves
$\underline m \le \underline n$, and~(b)~for all $m>n$,
$\TR$~proves $\lnot \underline m \le \underline n$.
Item~(a) is the same as part~(a) of Theorem~\ref{thm:RleAndlt}.
To prove item~(b), let $m>n$. For each $m^\prime\le n$,
axiom~$R_{\neq}$ gives that $\TR$~proves $\underline m \neq \underline {m^\prime}$.
Therefore, part~(b) of Theorem~\ref{thm:RleAndlt} implies
that $\TR$~proves $\lnot \underline m \le \underline n$.
\end{example}

\begin{definition}
A $k$-ary function~$f$ on~$\mathbb N$ is \idxem{representable} in~$T$
if there is a formula $A_f(x_1, x_2,\ldots,x_k,y)$ such
that for all $n_1,\ldots,n_k$ in~$\mathbb N$, and for $m=f(n_1,n_2,\ldots, n_k)$,
the theory~$T$ proves
\begin{equation}\label{eq:representFunction}
\forall y\, [\, A_f(\underline{n_1},\underline{n_2},\ldots, \underline {n_k}, y)
               \liff y = \underline m \,] .
\end{equation}
In this case, we say that $f$~is \emph{represented}\index{represent} by~$A_f$ in~$T$.
\end{definition}

The next theorem gives an equivalent formulation
of representability for theories $T\supseteq \TR$.

\begin{theorem}
Suppose the theory~$T$ extends~$\TR$. A $k$-ary function~$f$
is represented in~$T$ by~$A_f$ if and only if the following three
conditions hold for all $n_1,\ldots,n_k$ in~$\mathbb N$
and $m=f(n_1,n_2,\ldots, n_k)$.
\begin{itemizeLoose}
\item[(a)] $T$ proves $A_f(\underline{n_1},\underline{n_2},\ldots, \underline {n_k}, \underline m)$.
\item[(b)]
    $T$ proves $\lnot A_f(\underline{n_1},\underline{n_2},\ldots, \underline {n_k}, \underline{m^\prime} )$
    for all $m^\prime \neq m$.
\item[(c)] $T$ proves $\forall x\, \forall y\, (A_f( \underline{n_1},\underline{n_2},\ldots, \underline {n_k}, x) \land
                     A_f( \underline{n_1},\underline{n_2},\ldots, \underline {n_k}, y) \limplies x=y )$.
\end{itemizeLoose}
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The formulas of (a) and~(c) taken together are logically equivalent to
the formula~(\ref{eq:representFunction}. Thus $A_f$ represents~$f$ if
and only if conditions (a) and~(c) hold. To complete the proof of the
theorem, we must show that, for $T \supseteq \TR$, if conditions (a) and~(c)
hold, then condition~(b) holds.

Suppose (a) and~(c) hold and $m^\prime \neq m$. By~(c),
$T$~proves
\[
\forall x\, \forall y\, (A_f( \underline{n_1},\underline{n_2},\ldots, \underline {n_k}, \underline{m}) \land
                     A_f( \underline{n_1},\underline{n_2},\ldots, \underline {n_k}, \underline{m^\prime})
                     \limplies \underline m = \underline{m^\prime} ) .
\]
Therefore, by (a) and by the fact that $T\vdash \underline m \neq \underline{m^\prime}$
from Axiom~$\TR_{\textstyle\neq}$, we get that $T$ proves
$\lnot A_f(\underline{n_1},\underline{n_2},\ldots, \underline {n_k}, \underline{m^\prime} )$.
That establishes~(b).
\end{proof}

\begin{example}
As three simple examples, the successor, addition, and multiplication
functions are representable in~$\TR$. The formula $A_S(x_1,y)$ that represents
the successor function $n \mapsto n+1$ is just the formula $S(x_1)=y$. To verify
this, we must show that for every $n\in \mathbb N$,
the theory~$\TR$ proves $S(\underline n) = \underline{n{+}1}$
and $\forall y\, (S(\underline n) = y \limplies y = \underline{n{+}1})$.
These both follow immediately by equality axioms (without needing any
axioms of~$\TR$) since $S(\underline n)$ and $\underline{n{+}1}$ are the same
term, namely the term formed from $n+1$ applications of the function symbol~$S$
to~$0$.

The formula $A_+(x_1,x_2,y)$ that represents the addition function
$\langle n_1, n_2 \rangle \mapsto\penalty10000  n_1+\penalty10000 n_2$ is just the formula
$x_1+x_2 = y$. To verify this, we must show that, for all $n_1, n_2\in \mathbb N$,
$\TR$ proves $\underline{n_1}+\underline{n_2} = \underline{n_1+n_2}$
and
$\forall y\, (\underline{n_1}+\underline{n_2} = y \limplies\penalty10000 y=\underline{n_1+ n_2})$.
The first formula is an $\TR_+$-axiom.  The second formula
follows from the first formula via an equality axiom.


The formula $A_\cdot(x_1,x_2,y)$ that represents the multiplication function
$\langle n_1, n_2 \rangle \mapsto\penalty10000  n_1\cdot\penalty10000  n_2$
is just the formula
$x_1\cdot x_2 = y$. This is verified by the same argument as was used
for the addition function.
\end{example}

\begin{theorem}
Suppose $T$ is a consistent theory in the language~$L_\PA$.
\begin{enumerate}
\item Suppose $R$ is a $k$-ary relation represented by~$A_R$ in~$T$. Then,
for all $n_1,\ldots, n_k \in \mathbb N$,
$T$~proves $A_R(\underline{n_1},\underline{n_2},\ldots,\underline{n_k})$
if and only if
$R(n_1,n_2, \ldots, n_k)$ is true. Similarly,
$T$~proves $\lnot A_R(\underline{n_1},\underline{n_2},\ldots,\underline{n_k})$
if and only if
$R(n_1,n_2, \ldots, n_k)$ is false.
\item Suppose $f$ is a $k$-ary function represented by $A_f$ in~$T$.
Then for all $n_1,\ldots,n_k$ and $m=f(n_1,\ldots, n_k)$,
$T$~proves $A_f(\underline{n_1},\ldots, \underline{n_k}, \underline m)$ and for all $m^\prime \neq m$,
$T$~proves $\lnot A_f(\underline{n_1},\ldots, \underline{n_k}, \underline{m^\prime})$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
This is immediate from the definitions of ``representability''.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:TextendsRrepresents}
If the theory~$T$ extends the theory~$\TR$, then every relation representable in~$\TR$
is representable in~$T$ and every function representable in~$\TR$ is
representable in~$T$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
This is also immediate from the definitions.
\end{proof}

We shall prove in the sequel that the representable relations
of the theory~$\TR$ are precisely the decidable relations and that the representable
functions of~$\TR$ are precisely the computable functions. It will follow
immediately that the same holds for any axiomatizable theory $T\supseteq \TR$.
For the moment,
we can prove one direction of the inclusions:

\begin{theorem}
Suppose $T$ is a consistent, axiomatizable theory and $T\supseteq R$.
\begin{itemizeLoose}
\item[(a)] Suppose $S$ is a representable relation of~$T$. Then $S$~is decidable.
\item[(b)] Suppose $f$ is a representable function of~$T$. Then $f$ is computable.
\end{itemizeLoose}
In particular, since $\TR$ is axiomatizable
and consistent, {\rm (a)} and~{\rm (b)} hold for $\TR$ itself.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By Theorem~\ref{thm:ceLogicalConseqs}, the
theory~$T$ is computably enumerable.
Let $A_S$ represent~$S$ in~$T$. To decide whether
$S(n_1,\ldots, n_k)$ holds, form the sentence
$A_S(\underline n_1, \ldots, \underline{n_k})$ and enumerate
the members of the theory~$T$ until one of the sentences
$A_S(\underline n_1, \ldots, \underline{n_k})$ or
$\lnot A_S(\underline n_1, \ldots, \underline{n_k})$ is enumerated. Since $A_S$
represents~$S$, one of these must be enumerated, and since $T$ is consistent, it indicates
whether or not $S(n_1,\ldots, n_k)$ holds.

For~(b), let $A_f$ represent~$f$ in~$T$. To compute the
value of $f(n_1,\ldots,n_l)$, enumerate the members of~$T$
until producing a formula of the form
$A_f(\underline{n_1},\underline{n_2},\ldots, \underline {n_k}, \underline m)$.
Since $A_f$ represents~$f$, such a formula must appear and then since $T$ is consistent,
$f(n_1,\ldots,n_k) =\penalty10000 m$.
\end{proof}

Note that the above theorems hold even if $T$ is not a subtheory
of~$\TTh {\mathcal N}$. In other words, the theorems hold even for
theories~$T$ that might contain sentences that are false for
the integers.
