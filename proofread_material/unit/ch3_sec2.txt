¥section{First-Order Syntax}¥label{sec:syntaxFO}

This section defines the syntax of first-order formulas. The definition goes
through a four-step process. First, we define the notion of a language‾$L$.
A language specifies which non-logical¥index{non-logical symbol} symbols may be
used in formulas. The symbols $¥lnot$, $¥land$, $¥lor$, $¥limplies$, $¥liff$,
$¥forall$, $¥exists$ and‾$=$ are considered to be ¥emph{logical symbols}¥index{logical symbol}
since they have a prescribed meaning in all settings. The non-logical¥index{non-logical symbol}
symbols however, can have different meanings in different settings. For example, $¥cdot$‾can
mean different things in different groups and yet another thing in the integers.
Once a language‾$L$ is fixed, we define the notions of $L$-terms,
atomic $L$-formulas, and finally $L$-formulas. Figure‾¥ref{fig:fmlaDefn}
shows examples of these four concepts.

First-order formulas are ¥emph{expressions}¥index{expression}, i.e.‾strings of
symbols, that are built from logical symbols, the equality sign, non-logical symbols,
variables, and parentheses.  The logical symbols include propositional
connectives, the existential quantifier‾$¥exists$, and the universal
quantifier‾$¥forall$. Variables‾$x_i$ are intended to range over a
domain of ``objects'' or ``individuals''.
The non-logical symbols are specified by a first-order language.
For example, for the theory of groups, we might take the
language to consist of $¥cdot$, $^{-1}$, $1$, and‾$=$.
Or for the theory of the integers, we might use the language
$0$, $S$, $+$, $¥cdot$ and‾$=$.  The specification of the
non-logical symbols and their attributes is called a language:¥footnote{A language
is sometimes called a ``signature''.}¥index{language}

¥begin{definition}
A ¥idxem{language}‾$L$ is a set of symbols along with information about
what kind of symbols they are and their arities. (The ``¥idxnm{arity}'' of a function
or predicate is the number of arguments it takes and must be a positive integer.) 
The following kinds of symbols can be constituents of a language.
¥begin{enumerateLoose}
¥item {¥bfseries ¥em Constant¥index{constant symbol} symbols}. These are intended to denote particular objects in
the domain of discourse. (A constant symbol has arity zero.)
¥item {¥bfseries ¥em Function symbols¥index{function symbol}}, along their arities.
¥item {¥bfseries ¥em Predicate symbols¥index{predicate symbol}}, along with their arities.
These are often called ``relation symbols''.
¥item {¥bfseries ¥em The equality sign‾$=$}¥index{equality sign ($=$)}. This is included
in a language as a special binary predicate symbol unless it is explicitly
excluded as a symbol.
¥end{enumerateLoose}
¥end{definition}

¥begin{example}¥label{example:languagePA}
The language of the theory of the integers¥index{integers, theory of}
described in the
previous section had the following constituents:
¥begin{enumerateLoose}
¥item The constant symbol‾$0$.
¥item The unary function symbol‾$S$ and the two binary
function symbols $+$ and‾$¥cdot$.
¥item Apart from equality, no predicate symbols.
¥item The binary predicate symbol for equality,‾$=$.
¥end{enumerateLoose}
This language is denoted $L_¥PA$. The subscript ``$¥PA$'' stands for
``¥idxnm{Peano Arithmetic}'', which will be defined later
in Section‾¥ref{sec:fourTheoriesArith}.

The predicates $¥le$ and $¥TPrime(-)$ were discussed
above. These are not part of‾$L_¥PA$. They can instead
be expressed by $L_¥PA$-formulas.
¥end{example}

Once a language $L$ has been fixed, we can define
the notion of a first-order formula over the
language‾$L$. For this, we shall define first ``terms'',
then ``atomic formulas'', and finally ``formulas''.
Terms and formulas are expressions, namely strings of symbols.
The symbols that may appear in an $L$-term or
an $L$-formula include:
¥begin{enumerateLoose}
¥item The symbols of‾$L$,
¥item The propositional connectives $¥lnot$, $¥lor$, $¥land$, $¥limplies$ and‾$¥liff$.
¥item The quantifier symbols $¥forall$ and‾$¥exists$.
¥item Variables $x_1, x_2, x_3, ¥ldots$.
¥item Parentheses and commas. Parentheses show precedence and delimit
the scope of functions and predicates; commas separate
the arguments of functions and predicates.
¥end{enumerateLoose}
Predicate symbols and quantifier symbols do not appear in terms.

¥begin{definition}
Let $L$ be a language. The ¥emph{$L$-terms}¥index{term} are inductively defined
by
¥begin{enumerateLoose}
¥item For $i¥ge 1$, the variable‾$x_i$ is a term.
¥item If $c$ is a constant symbol of‾$L$, then $c$ is a term.
¥item If $f$ is a $k$-ary function symbol of‾$L$ where $k¥ge 1$, and if
$t_1, t_2, ¥ldots, t_k$ are $L$-terms, then
¥[
f(t_1, t_2, ¥ldots, t_k)
¥]
is an $L$-term.¥footnote{The use of parentheses and commas
in the term $f(t_1, t_2, ¥ldots, t_k)$ is redundant. The redundancy comes
from the fact that
functions have a fixed, constant arity, and since we use prefix (Polish) notation for terms,
and there is a simple parsing algorithm that could uniquely recover the underlying formula
even if parentheses and commas were omitted. Nonetheless, in practice, parentheses and commas
make it much easier to read and comprehend terms, so we choose to include them.}
¥end{enumerateLoose}
¥end{definition}

As examples of infix notation,
$+(0,S(x_1))$ and $¥cdot(S(S(x_2)),+(S(x_3),S(x_4)))$ are
$L_¥PA$-terms. Their intended meanings, in more readable notation, are
$0+¥penalty 10000 (x_1+¥penalty 10000 1)$ and 
$(x_2+¥penalty 10000 2)¥cdot¥penalty 10000 ((x_3+¥penalty 10000 1)+¥penalty 10000 (x_4+¥penalty 10000 1))$.
These terms will denote
objects in the domain of individuals, depending on what objects the
variables‾$x_i$ are equal to.

¥begin{definition}
A term is ¥emph{closed}¥index{closed term} if it does not contain any variables. In other words,
a closed term is a term that is built from only constant symbols
and function symbols.
¥end{definition}

The simplest type of formulas is the atomic formulas.

¥begin{definition}
Let $L$ be a language. There are two ways of forming an
¥emph{atomic $L$-formula}¥index{atomic formula}.
¥begin{itemizeLoose}
¥item[(a)] If $P$ is a $k$-ary predicate symbol of‾$L$ and
$t_1,¥ldots,t_k$ are $L$-terms, then $P(t_1,¥ldots, t_k)$ is
an atomic $L$-formula.
¥item[(b)] If $L$ contains the symbol‾$=$¥index{equality sign ($=$)}
and $t_1$ and‾$t_2$
are $L$-terms, then $t_1 = t_2$ is an atomic $L$-formula.
¥end{itemizeLoose}
¥end{definition}

For example, $+(0,S(x_1)) = ¥cdot(S(S(x_2)),+(S(x_3),S(x_4)))$
is an atomic $L_¥PA$-formula.  If we add $¥le$ as a
binary predicate symbol, then
¥[
¥le (+(0,S(x_1)),¥,¥cdot(S(S(x_2)),+(S(x_3),S(x_4))) )
¥]
is an atomic $L_{¥PA}¥cup¥{¥le¥}$-formula.
These two formulas could be either true or false, depending on what
objects the variables‾$x_i$ are equal to, and on how the
symbols $¥le,¥, S,¥, +,¥, ¥cdot$ are interpreted.

Finally, we can define $L$-formulas.

¥begin{definition}
Let $L$ be a language. The $L$-formulas¥index{formula} are inductively defined
as follows.
¥nopagebreak
¥begin{itemizeLoose}
¥item[(a)] Any atomic $L$-formula is an $L$-formula.
¥item[(b)] If $A$ is an $L$-formula, then $¥lnot A$ is an $L$-formula.
¥item[(c)] If $A$ and‾$B$ are $L$-formulas, then $(A¥lor B)$, $(A¥land B)$,
$(A ¥limplies B)$ and $(A ¥liff B)$ are $L$-formulas.
¥item[(d)] If $A$ is an $L$-formula and $x_i$ is a variable,
then $¥forall x_i A$ and $¥exists x_i A$ are $L$-formulas.
¥end{itemizeLoose}
¥end{definition}
When the language $L$ is understood, we generally refer to $L$-terms and $L$-formulas
as just ``terms'' and ``formulas''.

¥paragraph*{Abbreviations for formulas.}
It is often convenient to use informal notations for formulas
that are easier to read but do not fully obey the above definition of formulas.
As an example, $¥TPrime(x)$ as defined in‾(¥ref{eq:xPrime}) does not
fit the formal definition for an $L_¥PA$-formula; instead it is meant to
be the readable transcription of an $L_¥PA$-formula. To write the actual formula,
we must add parentheses and
we need to replace $x,y,z$ with some choice of variables‾$x_i$. If we, say, use
$x_1, x_2, x_3$ for $x,y,z$, then the formula (¥ref{eq:xPrime}) becomes
¥begin{equation}¥label{eq:xPrimeFormula}
¥lnot x_1 = S(0) ¥land ¥forall x_2¥, (¥exists x_3 ¥,( x_2¥cdot x_3=x_1) ¥limplies (x_2=S(0) ¥lor x_2=x_1)).
¥end{equation}
The actual
formula, with all parentheses and in prefix notation, is
¥begin{equation}¥label{eq:xPrimeFormulaBis}
( ¥lnot  x_1 = S(0) ¥land ¥forall x_2¥, (¥exists x_3 ¥, ¥cdot(x_2, x_3) = x_1 ¥limplies (x_2 = S(0) ¥lor x_2=x_1))).
¥end{equation}
The formula (¥ref{eq:xPrime}) is an informal way of writing this.

This is an example of the conventions for writing formulas in more readable form,
similarly to what was done for propositional logic earlier in Section‾¥ref{sec:propFormulas}.
¥begin{itemizeDot}
¥item First of all, we often omit parentheses. The order of
precedence¥index{precedence of connectives} of operations is
similar to propositional logic:
¥begin{enumerateLoose}
¥item Negation $¥lnot$ and quantifiers $¥forall$ and $¥exists$ have the
highest precedence.
¥item $¥land$ and $¥lor$ have the second highest precedence.
¥item $¥limplies$ and $¥liff$ have the lowest precedence.
¥item Binary connectives of the same precedence associate from right to left.
¥end{enumerateLoose}
¥item Second, we often use variable names such as $x, y, z,¥ldots$ instead of $x_1, x_2, x_3, ¥ldots$.
¥item Third, when it helps with readability, we often use infix notation to write binary
functions (such as $+$ and‾$¥cdot$) and binary predicates
(such as $¥le$ and‾$<$). E.g., we write $s+t$ and $s¥le t$ as
abbreviations of $+(s,t)$ and ${¥le}(s,t)$.  We also use $s ¥neq t$¥index{inequality ($¥neq$)} as a
shorthand notation for $¥lnot s = t$.
¥item Fourth, we often add parentheses to make formulas more readable.
A common example is when writing quantifiers with formulas that use infix notation.
For instance, we might write $¥forall x (0¥le x)$ instead of
$¥forall x¥,0¥le x$, just to make the formula more readable.
In the same vein, we will generally write $¥forall x ( S(x) ¥neq x )$
instead of the more correct $¥forall x S(x) ¥neq x$.
¥item Other conventions are used in the literature. For instance,
parentheses are often used around quantifiers, to write $(¥forall x)$ and
$(¥exists x)$ instead of just $¥forall z$ and‾$¥exists x$. Sometimes
dots are used instead of parentheses; for example, by writing
$¥exists x. x=0 ¥lor S(x)=0$ instead of $¥exists x (x=0¥lor S(x)=0)$.
We will avoid these conventions in this book however.
¥end{itemizeDot}

The use of variables such as $x,y,z$ to informally denote $x_1, x_2, x_3$
brings up a technical complication. Namely, how did we decide to
use $x_1, x_2, x_3$ instead of other‾$x_i$'s? To illustrate this, consider
the following three formulas, all of which are versions of‾(¥ref{eq:xPrime}):
¥begin{eqnarray}
¥label{eq:xPrime123}
x_1¥neq S(0) ¥land ¥forall x_2¥, (¥exists x_3¥, (x_2¥cdot x_3 = x_1) ¥limplies x_2=S(0) ¥lor x_2=x_1)
¥¥
¥label{eq:xPrime156}
x_1¥neq S(0) ¥land ¥forall x_5¥, (¥exists x_6¥, (x_5¥cdot x_6 = x_1) ¥limplies x_5=S(0) ¥lor x_5=x_1)
¥¥
¥label{eq:xPrime456}
x_4¥neq S(0) ¥land ¥forall x_5¥, (¥exists x_6¥, (x_5¥cdot x_6 = x_4) ¥limplies x_5=S(0) ¥lor x_5=x_4)
¥end{eqnarray}
The first two formulas have used $x_1$ for the variable‾$x$, and thus assert that $x_1$ is prime.
The difference between these two formulas is that one uses $x_2, x_3$ for $y, z$ and
the other uses $x_5,x_6$ for‾$y,z$. But these are quantified variables, usually
called ``bound¥index{bound variable}'' variables.
Thus the formulas (¥ref{eq:xPrime123}) and‾(¥ref{eq:xPrime156}) have
the same meaning; they both say that $x_1$ is prime, and they both equally well
can serve as the formula $¥TPrime(x_1)$.

On the other hand, (¥ref{eq:xPrime456}) uses $x_4$ for‾$x$, and thus can serve
as the formula $¥TPrime(x_4)$ asserting that $x_4$ is prime, not that $x_1$ is prime.
Therefore, (¥ref{eq:xPrime456})‾is not
equivalent to the first two formulas! The variable‾$x$ in‾(¥ref{eq:xPrime}) is called a ``free¥index{free variable}'' variable.
Likewise, $x_1$ in (¥ref{eq:xPrime123}) and‾(¥ref{eq:xPrime156}), and $x_4$ in‾(¥ref{eq:xPrime456})
are ``free'' variables. These formulas assert that their ¥idxnm{free variable} is a prime.

This topic of free variables will be revisited in
Sections ¥ref{sec:truthFO} and‾¥ref{sec:substitution}. For now, we just
formally define what it means for an occurrence of a variable to be free or bound.
But first, consider the formula
¥[
¥forall x_1 ¥, ( x_1=0 ¥lor P(x_1)) ¥land x_1=x_1 .
¥]
as an example. The two occurrences of‾$x_1$ in the subformula $(x_1=0 ¥lor P(x_1))$
are bound¥index{bound variable} occurrences; they are ``¥idxnm{bound by}''
the quantifier $¥forall x_1$.  The two occurrences of $x_1$ in the
subformula $x_1 = x_1$ are free occurrences.  The symbol‾$x_1$ in
the quantifier‾$¥forall x_i$ is neither a free nor bound occurrence.

¥begin{definition}¥label{def:freeBound}
Let $A$ be a formula, and let $x_i$ be an occurrence of a variable in‾$A$
that does not immediately follow a $¥forall$ or‾$¥exists$ symbol.
The notions of $x_i$ being a ¥emph{free}¥index{free variable}
or ¥emph{bound}¥index{bound variable} are defined recursively
as follows. At the same time, for $x_i$ a bound occurrence, we define which
quantifier $x_i$ is ¥idxem{bound by}.
In all cases, the occurrence of‾$x_i$ is either free or bound, but not both.
¥begin{enumerate}
¥item If $A$ is an atomic formula, then the occurrence‾$x_i$ is free in‾$A$.
¥item If $A$ is $¥lnot B$ or $B¥circ C$ for $¥circ$ a propositional connective,
then the occurrence of‾$x_i$ is free or bound in‾$A$ according to whether the corresponding
occurrence in‾$B$ or in‾$C$ is free or bound. If the occurrence of‾$x_i$ is bound, then it is ¥emph{bound by}
the same quantifier in‾$A$ that it is bound by in $B$ or‾$C$.
¥item If $A$ is $¥exists x_i B$ or $¥forall x_i B$, then every occurrence of $x_i$ is
bound in‾$A$ (and is not free in‾$A$). 
If an occurrence of‾$x_i$is free in‾$B$, then we say that that
occurrence in‾$A$ is ¥emph{bound by} the quantifier $¥exists x_i$ or $¥forall x_i$ at the start of‾$A$.
Otherwise, the occurrence of‾$x_i$ is bound by the same quantifier in‾$A$ that it is bound by in‾$B$.
¥item If $j¥neq i$ and $A$ is $¥exists x_j B$ or $¥forall x_j B$,
then the occurrence of‾$x_i$ is free or bound in‾$A$ according to whether the corresponding
occurrence in‾$B$ is free or bound. If the occurrence of‾$x_i$ is bound, then it is ¥emph{bound by}
the same quantifier in‾$A$ that it is bound by in‾$B$.
¥end{enumerate}
¥end{definition}

It is also possible to give a characterization of what it means for
a variable to be free or bound in terms of the ``¥idxnm{scope}'' of a quantifier.
¥begin{definition}¥label{def:scopeQuantifier}
Let $¥forall x B$ or $¥exists x B$ be a subformula occurring in‾$A$.
The ¥idxem{scope} of the quantifier $¥forall x$ or $¥exists x$ is the
subformula‾$B$. (The scope does not include the quantifier itself.)
¥end{definition}

Then, an occurrence of‾$x_i$ that does not immediately follow
a quantifier symbol $¥forall$ or‾$¥exists$ is bound in‾$A$ if and only if it is in the
scope of a quantifier $¥exists x_i$ or $¥forall x_i$ in‾$A$.
It is ¥idxnm{bound by} the leading quantifier of $¥forall x_i B$ or $¥exists x_i B$
if and only if it is not within the ¥idxnm{scope} of
any other occurrence of $¥forall x_i$ or $¥exists x_i$
in the subformula‾$B$.

¥begin{example}¥label{example:boundAndFree}
Let $A$ be the formula
$¥exists x_2 ( x_2 + x_2 = x_1 ) ¥lor x_3=0$, which asserts that $x_1$ is even
or $x_3$ is equal to‾0. The occurrences of $x_1$ and‾$x_3$ are free in‾$A$.
The two occurrences of‾$x_2$ in the term $x_2+x_2$ are bound occurrences.
The formula $A$ might also be (informally) written as $A(x_1, x_3)$ to indicate
it is asserting properties of $x_1$ and‾$x_3$.

On the other hand, let $B$ be $¥exists x_3 ( x_3 + x_3 = x_1 ) ¥lor x_3=0$. In
this formula, there are two bound occurrences of‾$x_3$ and one free occurrence of‾$x_3$.
It still asserts, like‾$A$, that $x_1$‾is even or $x_3$ equals‾$0$. Here $x_3$ is
being used in two completely different, independent ways.
¥end{example}

¥begin{definition}¥label{def:sentenceFO}
A first-order formula with no free occurrences of variables is
called a ¥idxem{sentence}.
¥end{definition}

Sentences are also called ``closed formulas''.

For example, $¥forall x (0¥le x)$ is a sentence, whereas $0 ¥le x$
is not. The second formula asserts something about its free variable‾$x$,
namely that it is greater than or equal to zero. The first formula
does not have any free variables, so it asserts something about truth
in the domain of discourse.

¥paragraph*{Unique readability and induction on formulas.}
Similarly to the situation for propositional formulas, first-order terms
and first-order formulas satisfy the ¥idxem{unique readability} property.
Namely, every term can be {¥em uniquely} written in one of the forms
¥begin{enumerateLoose}
¥item A constant symbol‾$c$,
¥item A variable symbol‾$x_i$, or
¥item A compound term $f(t_1,¥ldots, t_k)$ 
for $f$ a function symbol and $t_1, ¥ldots, t_k$ terms.
¥end{enumerateLoose}
In addition, every atomic subformula can be uniquely written as $t_1 = t_2$ or
$P(t_1,¥ldots, t_k)$ where $P$ is a $k$-ary predicate and the‾$t_i$'s are terms.
Finally, every formula can be written uniquely in one of the form
¥begin{enumerateLoose}
¥item As an atomic formula,
¥item As $¥lnot B$, or $B ¥land C$, or $B ¥lor C$, or $B¥limplies C$, or $B ¥liff C$,
¥item As $¥forall x_i B$ or $¥exists x_i B$.
¥end{enumerateLoose}

The inductive definitions of terms and formulas mean that we
can use induction on the complexity of terms or formulas. The
unique readability properties mean that we can also give recursive
definitions of new functions based on the complexity of terms and
