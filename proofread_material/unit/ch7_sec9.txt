¥section{The Second Incompleteness Theorem}¥label{sec:SecondIncomplete}
¥index{Second Incompleteness Theorem|(}

The section proves the Second Incompleteness Theorem and some of its
consequences, including L¥"ob's Theorem. The proof uses the extensional
approach as discussed in Section‾¥ref{sec:IntensionalExtensional}. This means
that the theory‾$T$ needs to be 
substantially stronger than‾$¥TR$ so that it can prove
simple metamathematical assertions about proofs and theorems.

For the first part of this section, let $T$ be a fixed axiomatizable, consistent
theory‾$T$ extending‾$¥TR$. (Later in the section, we will need more assumptions
about‾$T$.)
A sentence‾$B$ is said to be ¥idxem{independent}
of‾$T$ if neither $B$ nor‾$¥lnot B$ is a consequence of‾$T$.
The First Incompleteness Theorem states that $T$‾is not complete.
However, the proofs of the First Incompleteness Theorem
in Section‾¥ref{sec:FirstIncompleteness} were proofs by contradiction,
and they did not give any example of a sentence‾$B$ which is
independent of‾$T$.  This section will address this
by providing examples of independent sentences.

The first example will be essentially the diagonal formula‾$D_A$
used in the proof of the First Incompleteness Theorem based
on self-reference. The second example will be a formula expressing
the consistency of‾$T$. Thus the second example will show that,
with appropriate conditions, a theory‾$T$ of arithmetic cannot prove
the self-consistency statement ``$T$‾is consistent''. This fact is
known as the ``Second Incompleteness Theorem''.

Recall that $¥TProof_T$ and $¥TPrf_T$ are decidable
relations with $¥TProof_T(w)$ expressing that $w$‾is
the G{¥"o}del number of a $T$-proof, and $¥TPrf_T(w,v)$
expressing that $w$‾is the G{¥"o}del number of a
$T$-proof of the formula with G{¥"o}del number‾$v$.
Since these predicates are decidable,
Theorem‾¥ref{thm:decidableAreRepresentable} tells us
they are representable in‾$¥TR$.  We write
$¥scProof_T$ and $¥scPrf_T$ to denote the formulas
which represent $¥TProof_T$ and $¥TPrf_T$ in‾$¥TR$.
So,
¥begin{center}
¥begin{tabular}{rcl}
$¥scProof_T(x_1)$ & represents & $¥TProof_T$ ¥¥
$¥scPrf_T(x_1,x_2)$ & represents & $¥TPrf_T$
¥end{tabular}
¥end{center}
The difference in fonts, as in ``$¥TPrf_T$'' versus ``$¥scPrf_T$'',
is just to stress that the former is a binary relation on‾$¥mathbb N$
where the latter is an $L_¥PA$-formula.

Since $¥TR$‾is a true theory and $¥scPrf_T$ represents $¥TPrf_T$ in‾$¥TR$,
it also defines $¥TPrf_T$ in‾$¥mathcal N$. Namely,
for all $n, m¥in ¥mathbb N$,
¥[
¥mathcal N ¥vDash ¥scPrf_T(n,m) ‾‾¥Leftrightarrow‾‾ ¥langle n,m ¥rangle ¥in ¥TPrf_T .
¥]

The formula $¥scThm_T(x)$ is defined to be the formula $¥exists y¥, ¥scPrf_T(y,x)$.
Since $¥scPrf_T$ defines the relation $¥TPrf_T$ in‾$¥mathcal N$, it follows
that $¥scThm_T(x)$ defines the set of theorems of‾$T$. Namely, for all $m ¥in ¥mathbb N$,

¥[
¥mathcal N ¥vDash ¥scThm_T(m) ‾‾¥Leftrightarrow‾‾
      ¥hbox{$m = ¥lgn A¥rgn$ for some‾$A$ such that $T¥vDash A$}.
¥]

Let $D$ be a formula obtained from the Diagonal
Theorem‾¥ref{thm:DiagTR}
such that¥footnote{Note
that this $D$ is very similar to the $D_A$ used in the
last proof of Section‾¥ref{sec:FirstIncompleteness} of the
First Incompleteness Theorem, since in that proof, $A$‾was presumed
to represent $¥overline{¥TThm_T}$. The difference is that the earlier
proof was by contradiction, so the existence of‾$A$ was based on the
(false) assumption that $¥TThm_T$ is decidable and hence
representable in‾$¥TR$. The definition of‾$D$
now uses just the fact that $¥TThm_T$ is {¥em definable} in‾$¥mathcal N$.}
¥begin{equation}¥label{eq:DiagDfmla}
¥TR ‾¥vdash‾ D ¥liff ¥lnot ¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn D ¥rgn}) .
¥end{equation}

¥begin{theorem}[¥hbox{Incompleteness Theorem --- explicit unprovable true formula}]
¥label{thm:SelfReferUnprovable}
Let $T$ be a consistent, axiomatizable extension of‾$¥TR$.
Suppose $D$ satisfies‾{¥rm(¥ref{eq:DiagDfmla})}.  Then $D$‾is true in‾$¥mathcal N$
and $T ¥nvdash D$.
¥end{theorem}

The formula $D$ says ``I am not provable
in‾$T$''.
The intuition for the proof of Theorem‾¥ref{thm:SelfReferUnprovable} is as follows.
First, for simplicity, let's assume that $T$ is a true theory.
If $D$‾is false, then $T$ does prove‾$D$. But this
would mean $T$‾proves a false formula, contradicting the assumption
that $T$ is a true theory. So $D$ must be true and hence not
provable in‾$T$.

To extend the intuition to the general case where we do not use the assumption
that $T$ is true, we argue as follows. First, if $T$ proves‾$D$, then $T$‾proves
``$T$‾does not prove‾$D$''; this is by the self-referential choice of‾$D$. On
the other hand, if $T$ proves‾$D$, there is a finite, explicit $T$-proof
of‾$D$ with some G{¥"o}del number‾$m$. Examining this $T$-proof shows that it is indeed
a $T$-proof; this can be done in‾$¥TR$ and hence in‾$T$, showing that $T$‾proves
``$T$ proves‾$D$''.  But $T$ is consistent, so it cannot prove both
``$T$‾does not prove‾$D$'' and ``$T$ proves‾$D$''. Thus $T$ cannot prove‾$D$.

The proof of Theorem ¥ref{thm:SelfReferUnprovable} makes this informal argument formal:

¥begin{proof}
From (¥ref{eq:DiagDfmla}), we have
¥[
T ¥vdash D ‾ ¥Leftrightarrow ‾ T¥vdash ¥lnot ¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn D ¥rgn}) .
¥]
On the other hand, we have
¥begin{implications}
¥multicolumn{3}{l}{$T¥vdash D$} ¥¥
& $¥Leftrightarrow$ & ${¥mathcal N} ¥vDash ¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn D ¥rgn})$ & By definition of $¥scThm_T$ ¥¥
& $¥Leftrightarrow$ & ${¥mathcal N} ¥vDash ¥scPrf_T(¥underline m, ¥underline{¥lgn D ¥rgn})$, for some $m¥in ¥mathbb N$ & By definition of $¥scThm_T$ ¥¥
& $¥Leftrightarrow$ & $¥TR ¥vdash ¥scPrf_T(¥underline m,¥underline{¥lgn D ¥rgn})$, for some $m¥in ¥mathbb N$ & $¥scPrf_T$ represents $¥TPrf_T$ ¥¥
& $¥Leftrightarrow$ & $¥TR ¥vdash ¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn D ¥rgn})$ & By definition of $¥scThm_T$ ¥¥
&&&‾‾‾ and since $¥TR$ is a true theory ¥¥
& $¥Rightarrow$ & $T ¥vdash ¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn D ¥rgn})$ & Since $T ¥supseteq ¥TR$
¥end{implications}

We shown that if $T¥vdash D$, then both $¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn D ¥rgn})$
and $¥lnot ¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn D ¥rgn})$ are consequences of‾$T$, contradicting
the consistency of‾$T$. Therefore $T¥nvdash D$.
¥end{proof}

Theorem‾¥ref{thm:SelfReferUnprovable} left open the possibility
that $T$ proves‾$¥lnot D$. This can be ruled out if $T$‾is
$¥omega$-consistent¥index{omega consistent@{$¥omega$-consistent}}:

¥begin{theorem}[¥hbox{Incompleteness Theorem --- explicit independent formula}]
¥label{thm:SelfReferIndependent}
Let $T$ be an $¥omega$-consistent, axiomatizable extension of‾$¥TR$.
Suppose $D$ satisfies‾{¥rm(¥ref{eq:DiagDfmla})}.  Then $D$‾is independent
of‾$T$.
¥end{theorem}
¥begin{proof}
From the previous theorem, $T¥nvdash D$, so we only need to show
that $T¥nvdash ¥lnot D$. Suppose that $T ¥vdash ¥lnot D$. By {¥rm(¥ref{eq:DiagDfmla})},
$T ¥vdash ¥exists x¥, ¥scPrf_T(x, ¥underline{ ¥lgn D ¥rgn })$.

On the other hand, by the previous theorem, there is no
$T$-proof of‾$D$, hence $¥TPrf_T(m, ¥lgn D ¥rgn)$ is false for
every $m¥in¥mathbb N$. Since $¥scPrf_T$ represents‾$¥TPrf_T$,
we have $T ¥vdash ¥lnot ¥scPrf_T(¥underline m, ¥underline{ ¥lgn D ¥rgn })$
for every‾$m$. This contradicts the $¥omega$-consistency of‾$T$.
¥end{proof}

Theorem‾¥ref{thm:SelfReferIndependent} gives
an independent sentence‾$D$. This works even for $T$ a very natural
and strong theory such as Peano Arithmetic (PA).
The problem is that $D$ is
a somewhat strange, self-referential, formula. As such, one might
object to the importance of‾$D$ as an independent sentence just
because it is self-referential. The Second Incompleteness Theorem
addresses this by giving an alternative formula $¥scCon_T$
which is independent of‾$T$ (after making some additional, but natural,
assumptions about‾$T$). $¥scCon_T$‾express the property that
``$T$‾is consistent''  so, informally, the
Second Incompleteness Theorem states that no arithmetic theory‾$T$ can
prove its own consistency (under suitable assumptions about‾$T$).


¥bigskip
¥paragraph*{The Second Incompleteness Theorem.}
The intuition
for the Second Incompleteness Theorem comes from the
observation that
Theorem‾¥ref{thm:SelfReferUnprovable} can be restated as follows:
¥begin{corollary}¥label{ConImpliesD}
Let $T$ be an axiomatizable extension of‾$¥TR$. Then,
¥[
¥hbox{If $T$ is consistent, then $D$‾is true in‾$¥mathcal N$.}
¥]
¥end{corollary}
We already know that $T$‾does not prove‾$D$.
From the corollary, we can make an intuitive leap
and conjecture that since $D$ is unprovable in‾$T$,
also the consistency
of‾$T$ is unprovable in‾$T$. Making this ``intuitive leap''
formal is exactly what is needed to prove the Second
Incompleteness Theorem.
As we shall see next, the notion of ``$T$‾is consistent'' can
be formalized as a sentence‾$¥scCon_T$. Then, with some additional
assumptions on‾$T$, we will be able to establish that $T$ proves
$¥scCon_T ¥limplies D$ and consequently that $T$‾does not
prove $¥scCon_T$.

¥begin{definition}
Let $T$ be an axiomatizable theory in the language‾$L_¥PA$. Then
$¥scCon_T$ is the sentence
¥[
¥lnot ¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn 0=1 ¥rgn}),
¥]
where we write ``0=1'' as a shorthand notation
for ``0=S(0)'' or ``0=¥underline 1''. In other words, 
$¥underline{¥lgn 0=1 ¥rgn}$ 
means the term $S^m 0$ where $m ¥in ¥mathbb N$ is the
G{¥"o}del number of the formula $0=S(0)$.

Similarly, $¥scCon_T$ is the sentence
$¥lnot ¥exists x ¥, ¥scPrf_T(x, ¥underline{¥lgn 0=1 ¥rgn})$.
¥end{definition}

We need some extra conditions for the proof of the
Second Incompleteness Theorem. The idea is that $T$ should not only
contain $¥TR$ but should also be strong enough to prove simple properties
about the formulas $¥scPrf_T$ and $¥scThm_T$. These are expressed by the
Hilbert-Bernays-L¥"ob conditions:

¥begin{definition}
Let $T¥supseteq R$ be an axiomatizable theory in the language‾$L_¥PA$. The
Hilbert-Bernays-L¥"ob conditions for‾$T$ state the following
hold for all $L_¥PA$-sentences $A$ and‾$B$:
¥begin{itemizeLoose}[labelwidth=¥widthof{$¥THBL_3$:},leftmargin=!]
¥item[$¥THBL_1$:] If $T ¥vdash A$, then $T ¥vdash ¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn A¥rgn})$.
¥item[$¥THBL_2$:] $T$ proves
    $¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn A¥rgn}) ¥limplies
         ¥scThm_T ( ¥underline{ ¥lgn ¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn A¥rgn}) ¥rgn } )$.
¥item[$¥THBL_3$:] $T$ proves
      $¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn A ¥rgn}) ¥land ¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn A¥limplies B ¥rgn}) ¥limplies
       ¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn B ¥rgn})$.
¥end{itemizeLoose}
¥end{definition}

It turns out that the Hilbert-Bernays-L¥"ob conditions are very natural
and hold for many sufficiently powerful theories‾$T$ of arithmetic,
including‾$¥PA$. For this, formulas such as $¥scPrf_T$ and $¥scThm_T$
must be formulated in a sufficiently straightforward and natural way.

It is not hard to see that the condition $¥THBL_1$ holds for
any axiomatizable theory‾$T$ that extends‾$¥TR$. At least, this holds if $¥scThm_T$ is
formalized as described earlier, using a formula $¥scPrf_T$ that represents
$¥TPrf_T$ in‾$¥TR$. The argument is just that if $T¥vdash A$, then there is
a particular $T$-proof, with some G{¥"o}del number‾$m$, and then
$¥TPrf_T(m, ¥lgn A ¥rgn)$ is true and hence
$T$‾can
prove that $¥scPrf_T(¥underline m, ¥underline{¥lgn A ¥rgn})$ holds.
From this, $T$‾can prove $¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn A¥rgn})$.

The condition $¥THBL_2$ can be viewed as a formalized
version of $¥THBL_1$. That is, $¥THBL_2$ states that
the implication of‾$¥THBL_1$ can be proved as a general principle
in‾$T$. The argument formalizes the fact that if $A$ has a $T$-proof‾$P$,
then there is a definable method to transform‾$P$ into a $T$-proof of
$¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn A ¥rgn})$. The difficulty is that
the $T$-proof‾$P$ is not known: it is just the (unknown) value of
the leading existential quantifier in $¥scThm_T$.
Carrying out the proof of‾$¥THBL_2$ in a theory such
as $¥PA$ can be detailed and lengthy; we do not present it here.

The condition $¥THBL_3$ is simpler. Informally, it states that
$T$ can prove that if $A$ has a $T$-proof‾$P_1$ and $A¥limplies B$
has a $T$-proof‾$P_2$, then there is a $T$-proof‾$P_3$ of‾$B$. The intuition
is that the $T$-proof of‾$B$ is formed by concatenating the $T$-proofs $P_1$
and‾$P_2$, and then appending the formula‾$B$ as derived by a
use of modus ponens. Viewed in this way, the condition $¥THBL_3$ is
almost a triviality, as it is just a formalized version of
Modus Ponens. However, it does depend on the theory‾$T$
being strong enough to define --- and prove properties of ---
predicates and functions that parse
and manipulate $T$-proofs.

We henceforth work with theories that satisfy the
Hilbert-Bernays-L¥"ob conditions.
One such theory is the
theory‾$¥PA$ of Peano Arithmetic. $¥PA$‾is a true theory, but
there are also non-true theories that satisfy the
Hilbert-Bernays-L¥"ob conditions, for instance the
theory $¥PA + ¥lnot ¥TCon_¥PA$.

¥begin{lemma}¥label{lem:ConProperties}
Suppose $T$‾is a consistent axiomatizable theory extending‾$¥TR$
that satisfies the Hilbert-Bernays-L¥"ob conditions. 
Let $A$‾be an $L_¥PA$-formula.
Then
¥begin{itemizeLoose}
¥item[(a)] $T$ proves $¥lnot ¥scCon_T ¥limplies ¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn A ¥rgn})$.
¥item[(b)] $T$ proves
¥[
¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn A ¥rgn}) ¥limplies ¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn ¥lnot A ¥rgn})
   ¥limplies ¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn ¥lnot ¥scCon_T ¥rgn}) .
¥]
¥end{itemizeLoose}
¥end{lemma}

Lemma‾¥ref{lem:ConProperties} shows the theory‾$T$ can
formalize enough metamathematics to prove that
$¥scCon_T$ properly formalizes the
statement that $T$ is consistent. Namely, part‾(a) states that if
$T$ is inconsistent, then every formula‾$A$ is a consequence of‾$T$.
And, part‾(b) states that if $T$‾proves directly contradictory
formulas, then $T$‾proves that $T$ is inconsistent.
Furthermore, these facts are provable by‾$T$.

¥begin{proof}
We prove‾(a) first. Since $T¥supseteq ¥TR$, $T ¥vdash 0 ¥neq 1$.
Therefore, $T ¥vdash 0=1 ¥limplies A$. By $¥THBL_1$,
$T$‾proves $¥scThm_T(¥underline{ ¥lgn 0=1 ¥limplies A ¥rgn })$.
By $¥THBL_3$, $T$‾proves
¥[
¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn 0=1 ¥rgn}) ¥limplies
    ¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn 0=1 ¥limplies A ¥rgn}) ¥limplies
    ¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn A¥rgn}).
¥]
$¥TCon_T$ is the same as
$¥lnot ¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn 0=1 ¥rgn})$. Part‾(a)
follows tautologically.

We now prove‾(b). By $¥THBL_1$, $T$ proves
$¥scThm_T(¥underline {¥lgn A ¥limplies ¥lnot A ¥limplies 0=1¥rgn})$.
By two uses of $¥THBL_3$,
¥[
T‾¥vdash‾¥scThm_T(¥underline {¥lgn A¥rgn}) ¥land
          ¥scThm_T(¥underline {¥lgn A ¥limplies ¥lnot A ¥limplies 0=1¥rgn})
         ¥limplies ¥scThm_T(¥underline {¥lgn ¥lnot A ¥limplies 0=1¥rgn})
¥]
and
¥[
T‾¥vdash‾¥scThm_T(¥underline {¥lgn ¥lnot A ¥rgn})
    ¥land ¥scThm_T(¥underline {¥lgn ¥lnot A ¥limplies 0=1¥rgn})
    ¥limplies ¥scThm_T(¥underline {¥lgn 0=1¥rgn}) .
¥]
Putting these together proves part‾(b).
¥end{proof}

¥begin{theorem}[Second Incompleteness Theorem]
Suppose $T$‾is a consistent axiomatizable theory extending‾$¥TR$
that satisfies the Hilbert-Bernays-L¥"ob conditions.
Then
¥[
T ¥nvdash ¥scCon_T.
¥]
¥end{theorem}

¥begin{proof}
Let $D$ be the unprovable self-referential
formula of‾(¥ref{eq:DiagDfmla}), chosen so that
$R ¥vdash D ¥liff ¥lnot ¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn D ¥rgn})$.
Theorem‾¥ref{thm:SelfReferUnprovable} established that $T¥nvdash¥penalty10000 D$.
We shall prove that $T$ proves $¥lnot D ¥limplies ¥lnot ¥scCon_T$.
From this, it follows that $T$‾proves $¥scCon_T ¥limplies D$ and therefore
$T ¥nvdash ¥scCon_T$.

Note that
¥begin{equation}¥label{eq:SIT_1}
T ‾¥vdash‾ ¥lnot D ¥limplies ¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn D ¥rgn})
¥end{equation}
by choice of‾$D$.
By $¥THBL_2$,
¥begin{equation}¥label{eq:SIT_2}
T ‾¥vdash‾ ¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn D ¥rgn}) ¥limplies
   ¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn ¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn D ¥rgn})¥rgn}).
¥end{equation}
By choice of $D$ again, $T$ proves $¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn D ¥rgn}) ¥limplies ¥lnot D$.
Hence by $¥THBL_1$,
¥begin{equation}¥label{eq:eqSIT_3}
T ‾¥vdash‾ ¥scThm(¥underline{¥lgn ¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn D ¥rgn}) ¥limplies ¥lnot D ¥rgn}) .
¥end{equation}
From $¥THBL_3$, (¥ref{eq:eqSIT_3}) gives that
¥begin{equation}¥label{eq:SIT_4}
T ‾¥vdash‾ ¥scThm(¥underline{¥lgn ¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn D ¥rgn})}) ¥limplies ¥scThm(¥underline{¥lgn¥lnot D ¥rgn}) .
¥end{equation}
From (¥ref{eq:SIT_1}), (¥ref{eq:SIT_2}) and (¥ref{eq:SIT_4}), we obtain
¥begin{equation}¥label{eq:SIT_5}
T ‾¥vdash‾ ¥lnot D ¥limplies ¥scThm_T(¥underline{¥lgn ¥lnot D ¥rgn}).
¥end{equation}
From (¥ref{eq:SIT_1}) and (¥ref{eq:SIT_5})
and by Lemma‾¥ref{lem:ConProperties}(b) with $A$‾equal to‾$D$
and $B$‾equal to $0=1$, we obtain
¥[
T ‾¥vdash‾ ¥lnot D ¥limplies ¥lnot ¥scCon_T
¥]
as desired. This completes the proof of the Second Incompleteness Theorem.
¥end{proof}

We have established that $T$ cannot prove $¥scCon_T$ --- at least assuming
that $T$ is a consistent, axiomatizable extension of‾$¥TR$. In this case, $¥scCon_T$
is a true statement that is not provable in‾$T$. Unlike the earlier
formula‾$D$ asserting its own unprovability, $¥scCon$‾is a natural statement,
with an intuitive meaning that does not depend on self-reference. As such, it is a
very satisfying example of the limitation of almost any useful strong
theory of arithmetic.

One might still complain that $¥scCon_T$ is metamathematical in nature, not
quite purely mathematical in the normal sense of mathematics. There
other statements that are independent of‾$¥PA$ that are more natural
in the sense that they do not explicitly involve metamathematical
concepts.  These other independent statements are beyond the scope
of the present text, but the interested reader can seek them out by
doing an internet search for the ``Paris-Harrington theorem'',
the ``hydra theorem'', the ``Goodstein theorem'', and related constructions.

¥medskip

The next obvious question is whether $¥scCon_T$ is always independent
of‾$T$. The answer is no. To see this, let $T$ be any
axiomatizable, consistent extension of‾$¥TR$. By the Second Incompleteness
Theorem, $T¥nvdash ¥scCon_T$. Therefore, if we let $T^¥prime$ be the
theory $T ¥cup ¥{¥lnot ¥scCon_T¥}$, it must be that $T^¥prime$ is
consistent. Clearly, $T^¥prime$ is also axiomatizable.
It is in addition reasonable to assume that
$T^¥prime ¥vdash ¥scCon_{T^¥prime} ¥limplies ¥scCon_T$
just because any $T$-proof is a $T^¥prime$-proof.¥footnote{Strictly
speaking, we need some extra assumptions. In particular, we
want $T$‾to prove $¥scThm_{T^¥prime}(¥lgn A ¥rgn)$ is
equivalent to $¥scThm_T(¥lgn ¥scCon_T ¥limplies A¥rgn)$.
This certainly holds for any straightforward way of
formalizing metamathematics in a powerful theory such as‾$¥PA$.
See also the comments after the statement of L¥"ob's Theorem.}¥label{footnote:Tprime}
Therefore
$T^¥prime ¥vdash ¥lnot ¥scCon_{T^¥prime}$ just because
$T^¥prime ¥vdash ¥scCon_{T^¥prime} ¥limplies ¥scCon_T$.

On the other hand, the extra condition of $¥omega$-consistency
is enough to ensure that $¥scCon_T$ is independent of‾$T$.
¥begin{theorem}
Suppose $T¥supseteq ¥TR$ is $¥omega$-consistent and axiomatiable
and satisfies the Hilbert-Bernays-L¥"ob conditions. Then
$¥scCon_T$ is independent of‾$T$.
¥end{theorem}

¥begin{proof}
We already proved that $T¥nvdash ¥scCon_T$. So suppose
that $T¥vdash ¥lnot¥scCon_T$; that is, that
$T¥vdash ¥exists y ¥, ¥scPrf_T(y,¥underline{ ¥lgn 0=1 ¥rgn })$.
Since $T$ is consistent and $T¥supseteq R$, and since
$¥scPrf_T$ represents $¥TPrf_T$, we have
that $T ¥vdash ¥lnot ¥scPrf_T(¥underline m, ¥underline{ ¥lgn 0=1 ¥rgn })$
for every $m ¥in ¥mathbb N$. This contradicts the
$¥omega$-consistency of‾$T$.
¥end{proof}
