\section{The Proof System \texorpdfstring{$\PL$}{PL}}\label{sec:PL}
\index{PL, proof system}

\nocite{Frege:Begriffsschrift}
\nocite{WhiteheadRussell:Principia}
\nocite{HilbertAckermmann:Grundzuge}

We now define the propositional proof system~$\PL$. This
is a so-called ``\idxnm{Hilbert-style}'' proof system.\footnote{
Hilbert-style systems are named after David Hilbert, one of the founding fathers of
mathematical logic, as this style of proof was used
in D.\ Hilbert and W.\ Ackermann's 1928 book
{\em Grundz{\"u}ge der theoretischen Logic}
({\em Foundations of Mathematical Logic}).
A system similar to a Hilbert-style system was already used
by A.N.~Whitehead and B.\ Russell in 1910 in their classic {\em Principia Mathematica}.
Even earlier, G.\ Frege used a precursor to Hilbert-style proof systems
in his 1879 {\em Begriffsschrift}. All three of these were
landmark developments in establishing the modern study of mathematical logic.

These particular four axioms can be found in Kleene~\cite{Kleene:metamathematics}. % XXX
%
%The Hilbert-style system~$\PL$ that we use is taken from Hodel~\cite{Hodel:MathLogic}, who
%named it~L.
}\label{footnote:HilbertStyle}

In the interests of making $\PL$ as simple as possible, $\PL$~uses
only $\{ \lnot, \limplies \}$-formulas.  Other connectives such
$\lor$, $\land$ and $\liff$ are abbreviations for equivalent formulas
that use only $\{\lnot, \limplies\}$.  Specifically,
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{r@{~~~~~is an abbreviation for~~~~~}l}
$A \lor B$ & $\lnot A \limplies B$ \\
$A \land B$ & $\lnot ( A \limplies \lnot B )$ \\
$A \liff B$ & $(A\limplies B) \land (B \limplies A)$
\end{tabular}
\end{center}


$\PL$-proofs start with axioms or other hypotheses, and infer new formulas
with Modus Ponens. Four types of axioms are permitted in $\PL$-proofs.

\paragraph*{$\PL$ axioms.}\index{PL, proof system!axioms}
The axioms of $\PL$ are all formulas of the forms:\footnote{The square
brackets in PL2 are the same as usual parentheses and are
used only to improve readability.}\index{square brackets}
\begin{description}[font=\bf]
\item[PL1:] $A \limplies (B \limplies A)$
\item[PL2:] $[A \limplies (B\limplies C)] \limplies [(A\limplies B) \limplies (A\limplies C)]$
\item[PL3:] $\lnot A \limplies (A \limplies B)$
\item[PL4:] $(\lnot A \limplies A) \limplies A$
\end{description}
where $A$, $B$ and $C$ may be any $\{\lnot, \limplies\}$-formulas.

\paragraph*{Modus Ponens.}\index{PL, proof system!Modus Ponens}\index{Modus Ponens}
The only inference rule for $\PL$ is Modus Ponens; namely, from
$A$ and $A\limplies\penalty10000 B$, it is permitted to infer~$B$.  This is denoted as
\begin{prooftree}
\AxiomC{$A$}
\AxiomC{$A\limplies B$}
\BinaryInfC{B}
\end{prooftree}
Here $A$ and $B$ may be any $\{\lnot,\limplies\}$-formulas.

\begin{definition}
Let $A$ be a $\{\lnot,\limplies\}$-formula.  A {\em $\PL$-proof}\index{PL, proof system!PL-proof@{$\PL$-proof}} of~$A$
is a sequence of $\{\lnot,\limplies\}$-formulas
\[
A_1, A_2, A_3, \ldots, A_\ell
\]
such that $A_\ell$ is~$A$ and such that each $A_i$ satisfies one
of the following conditions:
\begin{enumerateLoose}
\item $A_i$ is a $\PL$-axiom; or
\item $A_i$ is \emph{inferred by Modus Ponens}\index{Modus Ponens} from two
earlier formulas $A_j$ and~$A_k$ with $j,k<i$. Specifically we have
$A_k$ equal to $A_j \limplies A_i$, so the Modus Ponens inference
has the form
\begin{prooftree}
\AxiomC{$A_j$}
\AxiomC{$A_j \limplies A_i$}
\BinaryInfC{$A_i$}
\end{prooftree}
\end{enumerateLoose}
The final formula~$A_\ell$ is the \idxem{conclusion} of the $\PL$-proof
and is the formula that is proved.
\end{definition}

More generally, we can use a set~$\Gamma$ of formulas as additional hypotheses
that can be used freely in a $\PL$-proof.
\begin{definition}\label{def:PLproof}\index{PL, proof system!PL-proof@{$\PL$-proof}}
A \emph{$\PL$-proof of~$A$ from the hypotheses~$\Gamma$}
is a sequence of $\{\lnot,\limplies\}$-formulas
$A_1,A_2,\ldots, A_\ell$ such that $A_\ell$ is~$A$
and such that each $A_i$ satisfies one of the following conditions:
\begin{enumerateLoose}
\item $A_i$ is a $\PL$-axiom;
\item $A_i$ is in~$\Gamma$; or
\item $A_i$ is \emph{inferred by Modus Ponens} from two
earlier formulas $A_j$ and~$A_k$ with $j,k<i$.
\end{enumerateLoose}
\end{definition}

$\PL$-proofs are also sometimes called \emph{$\PL$-derivations}\index{derivation}.
We write $\vdash A$\index{single turnstile@{single turnstile ($\vdash$)}} to denote that $A$ has a $\PL$-proof; in this
case we say that $A$ is a \idxem{theorem}.
Similarly, we write $\Gamma \vdash A$\index{single turnstile@{single turnstile ($\vdash$)}} to denote that $A$ has
a $\PL$-proof from the hypotheses~$\Gamma$;
we then say that $A$ is a \emph{\idxnm{theorem} of~$\Gamma$}.
Note that it is permitted that
$\Gamma$ is infinite; however, a single $\PL$-proof can contain only finitely
many formulas, and thus can use only finitely many hypotheses from~$\Gamma$.

When $\Gamma$ is a finite set $\{B_1,\ldots, B_k\}$, we usually omit the set braces
and write just $B_1,\ldots, B_k \vdash A$.  We also abuse notation
by writing things like $\Gamma, A\vdash B$ instead of
$\Gamma\cup\{A\} \vdash B$.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:subsetHypsProp}
\ \hspace*{1em}
\begin{itemize}
\item[(a)] If $\Gamma \vdash A$ and $\Gamma^\prime \supset \Gamma$, then
$\Gamma^\prime \vdash A$.
\item[(b)] If $\Gamma \vdash A$, then there is a finite subset $\Gamma_0 \subseteq \Gamma$
such that $\Gamma_0 \vdash A$.
\end{itemize}
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Part~(a) is immediate from the definition of $\Gamma \vdash A$.
Part~(b) follows from the observation that a proof can contain only finitely
many formulas.
\end{proof}

To simplify notation, we assume that all formulas are $\{\lnot,\limplies\}$-formulas
for the rest of the chapter.

\begin{example}
There is a $\PL$-proof of $(\lnot A\limplies A)\limplies (\lnot A\limplies B)$, where $A$ and $B$
are any formulas. The $\PL$-proof consists of the following three formulas:
\begin{threecolproof}
1. & $\lnot A \limplies A \limplies B$ & Axiom PL3 \\
2. & $(\lnot A \limplies A \limplies B)\limplies (\lnot A \limplies A) \limplies (\lnot A\limplies B)$ & Axiom PL2 \\
3. & $(\lnot A \limplies A) \limplies (\lnot A\limplies B)$ & Modus Ponens, 1, 2 \\
\end{threecolproof}
The $\PL2$ axiom above is formed by letting $A$, $B$ and~$C$ in the definition of the
$\PL2$ axiom be the formulas $\lnot A$, $A$ and~$B$, respectively.
\end{example}

\begin{example}\label{example:AimpliesA}
There is a $\PL$-proof of $A\limplies A$, where $A$ is any formula. That is,
$\vdash A\limplies A$.  The $\PL$-proof consists of the following five formulas:
\begin{threecolproof}
1. & $(A \limplies ((A\limplies A) \limplies A)) \limplies (A\limplies (A\limplies A)) \limplies (A\limplies A)$
   & Axiom PL2 \\
2. & $A \limplies ((A\limplies A) \limplies A)$ & Axiom PL1 \\
3. & $(A\limplies (A\limplies A)) \limplies (A\limplies A)$ & Modus Ponens, 1, 2 \\
4. & $A\limplies (A\limplies A)$ & Axiom PL1 \\
5. & $A \limplies A$ & Modus Ponens, 3, 4
\end{threecolproof}
The $\PL2$ axiom above is formed by letting $B$ be the formula $A\limplies A$, and letting $C$ be the
formula~$A$. The first $\PL1$ axiom is obtained by letting $B$ be $A\limplies A$; the second
is formed by letting $B$ be~$A$.
\end{example}

\begin{example}\label{example:implyAorB}
For any $A$ and $B$, the formulas
$B \limplies (A \lor B)$ and $\lnot \lnot A \limplies\penalty10000 (A \lor\penalty 10000 B)$ have $\PL$-proofs.
To see this, recall that a formula $C\lor D$ is an abbreviation for
$\lnot C \limplies D$. Thus the two formulas are actually equal to
$B \limplies (\lnot A \limplies B)$ and $\lnot \lnot A \limplies (\lnot A \limplies B)$.
The first is an instance of the axiom~PL1; the second is an instance of the
axiom~PL3. Therefore, they each have a $\PL$-proof consisting of a single formula, namely an
axiom.
\end{example}

\begin{example} The formula $A\limplies B$ has a $\PL$-proof from the hypothesis~$B$.
That is, $B \vdash A \limplies B$.  The $\PL$-proof has three formulas:
\begin{twocolproof}
$B \limplies (A\limplies B)$ & Axiom PL1 \\
$B$ & Hypothesis \\
$A\limplies B$ & Modus Ponens
\end{twocolproof}
\end{example}

\begin{example}
Any formula~$A$ has a $\PL$-proof from itself as a hypothesis; that is, $A \vdash A$.  The
$\PL$-proof consists of just the single formula~$A$.
\end{example}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:PLclosedsubst}
The set of theorems (of $\PL$) is closed under \idxnm{substitution}. In other
words, if $\vdash A$, then $\vdash A(B/p_i)$ for any formulas $A$ and~$B$
and any variable~$p_i$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The set of axioms of $\PL$ is clearly closed under substitution. Also,
the valid Modus Ponens inferences are closed under substitution, since
\[
\hbox{%
\AxiomC{$D(B/p_i)$}
\AxiomC{$(D\limplies E)(B/p_i)$}
\BinaryInfC{$E(B/p_i)$}
\DisplayProof
\quad is the same as \quad
\AxiomC{$D(B/p_i)$}
\AxiomC{$D(B/p_i)\limplies E(B/p_i)$}
\BinaryInfC{$E(B/p_i)$}
\DisplayProof
}
\]
It follows that if
$A_1,\, A_2,\, A_3, \ldots,\, A_\ell$
is a $\PL$-proof, then so is
\[
A_1(B/p_i),\, A_2(B/p_i),\, A_3(B/p_i), \ldots,\, A_\ell(B/p_i) .
\]
This is proved by induction on the steps in the $\PL$-proof.
\end{proof}
