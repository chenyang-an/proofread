¥section{Propositional Languages}¥label{sec:adequate}

¥paragraph*{Languages and adequacy.}
A set‾$L$ of propositional connectives is called a
¥idxem{language}. So far, we have worked with
the language $L = ¥{ ¥lnot, ¥land, ¥lor, ¥limplies, ¥liff ¥}$;
however, we have already mentioned a few other connectives
such as $¥oplus$‾(parity), $¥top$‾(the constant‾$¥True$)
and‾$¥perp$‾(the constant‾$¥False$).

¥begin{definition}¥label{def:LformulaProp}
Let $L$ be a (propositional) language. An
$L$-formula is a propositional formula that
uses only propositional connectives from‾$L$.
¥end{definition}

So far in this chapter, ``formula'' has always meant
``$¥{¥lnot, ¥lor, ¥land, ¥limplies, ¥liff¥}$-formula''. 
Chapter‾¥ref{chap:PropProofs} will use
a different convention; there ``formula'' will mean
``$¥{¥lnot, ¥penalty10000 ¥limplies ¥penalty10000 ¥}$-formula''.

¥begin{definition}¥label{def:adequate}
A language‾$L$ is ¥idxem{adequate} if every Boolean
function is represented by some $L$-formula.
¥end{definition}

We have already proved a couple of languages are adequate:

¥begin{theorem}¥label{thm:adequateRecap}
The languages $¥{ ¥lnot, ¥lor, ¥land, ¥limplies, ¥liff¥}$
and $¥{ ¥lnot, ¥lor, ¥land¥}$ are both adequate.
¥end{theorem}

¥begin{proof}
The first part of the theorem was already stated
as Theorem‾¥ref{thm:adequateStd}. The second part is a consequence
of Theorem ¥ref{thm:adequateStdDNF} or‾¥ref{thm:adequateStdCNF}.
¥end{proof}

Note that if $L_1 ¥subseteq L_2$ and $L_1$‾is adequate,
then so is‾$L_2$. This is immediate from the fact
that every $L_1$-formula is an $L_2$-formula.

The last theorem can be improved to:
¥begin{theorem}¥label{thm:adequateRecapBis}
The languages $¥{ ¥lnot, ¥lor¥}$
and $¥{ ¥lnot, ¥land¥}$ are both adequate.
¥end{theorem}

¥begin{proof}
Let $f$ be a Boolean function. We show that
$f$ is represented by some $¥{¥lnot,¥lor¥}$-formula.
By Theorem‾¥ref{thm:adequateStdCNF}, $f$‾is represented
by a CNF formula‾$A$, which is a formula
of the form $C_1 ¥land C_2 ¥land ¥cdots ¥land C_k$
with the $C_i$'s all clauses and hence all $¥{¥lnot, ¥lor¥}$-formulas.
Using De Morgan's law¥index{De Morgan's law}, $A$‾is tautologically equivalent to
$¥lnot ( ¥lnot C_1 ¥lor ¥cdots ¥lor ¥lnot C_k )$. This
is a $¥{¥lnot , ¥lor¥}$-formula which represents‾$f$.

The fact that $f$ is also represented by a $¥{¥lnot, ¥land¥}$-formula
is proved similarly.
¥end{proof}

¥begin{theorem}¥label{thm:notImpliesAdequate}
The language $¥{¥lnot, ¥limplies¥}$ is adequate.
¥end{theorem}

¥begin{proof}
Since $¥{¥lnot, ¥lor¥}$ is adequate, it suffices to show
that every $¥{¥lnot, ¥lor¥}$-formula‾$A$ is tautologically
equivalent to a $¥{¥lnot, ¥limplies ¥}$-formula. For this,
we use the fact that $B ¥lor C$ is tautologically equivalent
to $¥lnot B ¥limplies C$ for any $B$ and‾$C$.

Specifically, transform the formula‾$A$ by repeatedly choosing a subformula
of the form $(B¥lor C)$
and replacing it with $(¥lnot B ¥limplies C)$. Each such replacement
removes one $¥lor$‾connective and adds a $¥lnot$‾connective
and a $¥limplies$‾connective.
Thus, the procedure ends with a $¥{¥lnot, ¥limplies ¥}$-formula
that is tautologically equivalent to‾$A$.¥footnote{See Corollary ¥ref{coro:substituteTaut}.}
¥end{proof}

Now we give examples of a couple of languages that are not adequate.

¥begin{theorem}¥label{thm:notAdequate2}
‾‾ ¥
¥begin{itemize}
¥item[(a)] $¥{¥lnot¥}$ is not adequate.
¥item[(b)] $¥{¥land, ¥lor ¥}$ is not adequate.
¥end{itemize}
¥end{theorem}

¥begin{proof}
For‾(a), note that a $¥{ ¥lnot ¥}$-formula‾$A$
must be of the form $¥lnot ¥cdots ¥lnot p_i$,
with zero or more $¥lnot$'s applied to a variable.
Since only one variable appears in‾$A$, it cannot
be tautologically equivalent to a formula
such as $p_1¥land p_2$ that depends on more than
one variable. Hence, no $¥{¥lnot¥}$-formula
can represent $f_{p_1¥land p_2}$ for instance.

For‾(b), let $A$‾be any $¥{¥land, ¥lor¥}$-formula.
We claim that if $¥varphi(p_i) =¥True$ for all‾$i$, then $¥varphi(A)=¥True$.
The claim implies that $A$‾cannot represent $f_{¥lnot p_1}$, since
$f_{¥lnot p_1}(¥True) = ¥False$.

Fix $¥varphi$ to be the assignment such that $¥varphi(p_i) = ¥True$ for
all‾$i$.
We need to prove the claim that for any $¥{¥land, ¥lor¥}$-formula‾$A$,
$¥varphi(A) = ¥True$. The proof is by induction on the complexity of‾$A$.
¥begin{description}
¥item[Base case:] $A$‾is a variable‾$p_i$. Then $¥varphi(A) = ¥True$
by the choice of‾$¥varphi$.
¥item[Induction step:] $A$‾is $(B¥lor C)$ or $(B¥land C)$.
There are two induction hypotheses, one for $B$ and one for‾$C$: they
state that $¥varphi(B) = ¥True$ and $¥varphi(C) = ¥True$. Therefore,
by the definitions of truth for $¥lor$ and for $¥land$, we must
have $¥varphi(A) = ¥True$.
¥end{description}
That completes the proof of the claim, and of part‾(b) of the theorem.
¥end{proof}

¥paragraph*{Other propositional connectives.}
There are many other propositional connectives that
can be used in addition to the standard ones
of $¥lnot$, $¥lor$, $¥land$, $¥limplies$, and‾$¥liff$.

The parity¥index{parity@{parity ($¥oplus$)}} connective,‾$¥oplus$, is defined so that
¥[
¥varphi(A ¥oplus B) ‾=‾ ¥left¥{ ¥begin{array}{ll}
¥True ‾‾‾ & ¥hbox{if $¥varphi(A) ¥neq ¥varphi(B)$} ¥¥
¥False ‾‾‾ & ¥hbox{if $¥varphi(A) = ¥varphi(B)$} ¥¥
¥end{array}¥right.
¥]
The notation‾``$¥oplus$'' reflects the fact that
when $¥True$ and‾$¥False$ are identified with $1$ and‾$0$ (respectively),
then $¥oplus$ is the same as addition modulo two.
Another name for the parity connective‾$¥oplus$ is
``¥idxnm{exclusive or}'', since $¥varphi(A ¥oplus B)$ is
true if and only if exactly one of $¥varphi(A)$ or‾$¥varphi(B)$ is true.
Exercise‾¥ref{exer:oplusLiff} asks you to show that
$A¥oplus B ¥tautEq ¥lnot(A ¥liff B)$.

Another very interesting connective is the ¥emph{nand}¥index{nand@{nand ($¥vert$)}} connective,
denoted‾``$|$''. This is also called the ¥idxem{Sheffer stroke} after its
discoverer H.¥ Sheffer.  ``Nand'' is short for ``not-and''.
As the name indicates, $p | q$ has
truth value equal to the negation of the conjunction of $p$ and‾$q$, so that
for all‾$¥varphi$,
¥[
¥varphi(A | B) ‾=‾ ¥varphi(¥lnot (A ¥land B)).
¥]
A remarkable aspect of the $|$ connective is that it is adequate all
by itself:
¥begin{theorem}¥label{thm:nandAdequate}
$¥{ | ¥}$ is adequate.
¥end{theorem}
¥begin{proof}
It is easy to check that $¥lnot A$ is tautologically equivalent
to $A | A$. Furthermore, $A¥land B$ is tautologically equivalent
to $¥lnot ( A | B)$, and hence to $(A|B)|(A|B)$.  Thus, any
$¥{¥lnot, ¥land¥}$-formula can be converted to a tautologically
equivalent $¥{|¥}$-formula by repeatedly replacing subformulas
of the form $¥lnot A$ with $(A|A)$ and subformulas of the form $(A ¥land B)$
with $((A|B)|(A|B))$.

Theorem‾¥ref{thm:adequateRecapBis} showed that $¥{¥lnot, ¥land¥}$ is adequate;
therefore $¥{ | ¥}$ is also adequate.
¥end{proof}

Exercise‾¥ref{exer:nor} defines the dual ¥emph{nor}¥index{nor@{nor ($¥downarrow$)}} connective,‾$¥downarrow$,
and asks for a proof that $¥{¥downarrow¥}$ is adequate.

Two very simple connectives are the constants
$¥top$¥index{true, nullary connective@{true, nullary connective ($¥top$)}} and
$¥perp$¥index{false, nullary connective@{false, nullary connective ($¥perp$)}}.
These are 0-ary or ``¥idxnm{nullary}'' connectives that do
not take any arguments. Their truth values are defined
by $¥varphi(¥top) = ¥True$ and $¥varphi(¥perp) = ¥False$.
¥begin{theorem}¥label{thm:topLimplies}
$¥{¥perp, ¥limplies ¥}$ is adequate.
¥end{theorem}

¥begin{proof}
Any negated formula $¥lnot A$ is tautologically equivalent to
$A ¥limplies ¥perp$. Since $¥{¥lnot,¥limplies¥}$ is adequate
(by Theorem‾¥ref{thm:notImpliesAdequate}), it follows that
$¥{¥perp, ¥limplies¥}$ is adequate.
¥end{proof}

We have so far introduced examples of 0-ary, 1-ary, and 2-ary connectives.
It is also possible to have $k$-ary connectives for $k>2$. One example
is the 3-ary connective $¥TCase(¥cdot, ¥cdot, ¥cdot)$¥index{case@{$¥TCase$}}; this
can also be called the ``if-then-else'' connective. The truth
value of $¥TCase(A, B, C)$ is defined by
¥[
¥varphi(¥TCase(A, B, C)) ‾=‾ ¥left¥{ ¥begin{array}{ll}
¥varphi(B) ‾‾‾ & ¥hbox{if $¥varphi(A) = ¥True$} ¥¥
¥varphi(C) ‾‾‾ & ¥hbox{if $¥varphi(A) = ¥False$.}
¥end{array}¥right.
¥]
One can think of $¥TCase(A,B,C)$ as meaning the
same as ``If $A$ then $B$ else $C$''.
It is easy to check that $¥TCase(p,q,r)$ is tautologically
equivalent to the DNF formula $(p¥land q) ¥lor (¥lnot p ¥land r)$.
In view of the equivalence with ``If‾$A$ then‾$B$ else‾$C$'',
it is also tautologically equivalent to $(p¥limplies q) ¥land (¥lnot p ¥limplies¥penalty10000 r)$.
Hence it is tautologically equivalent to the CNF formula
$(¥lnot p ¥lor q) ¥land (p ¥lor r)$.

Exercise‾¥ref{exer:caseAdequate} asks you to prove that
$¥{¥lnot, ¥TCase ¥}$ is adequate but
$¥{¥TCase¥}$ is not.

An example of a $k$-ary connective for arbitrary fixed $k¥ge 1$
is the ¥idxnm{majority} connective $¥TMaj^k$.
The semantics for majority connectives is defined by
letting $¥TMaj^k(A_1,¥ldots, A_k)$ be
true exactly when the number of true‾$A_i$'s is $¥ge k/2$.
Exercise‾¥ref{exer:majorityAdequate} deals with the adequacy of
$¥TMaj^k$ together with‾$¥lnot$.
