\section{Undecidability}\label{sec:UndecidableInformal}


This section discusses methods for showing that functions
are {\bfseries \emph{not}} computable or relations
are {\bfseries \emph{not}} decidable. It is hard to do this
with only an informal definition of the notion of algorithm
since the informal definition does not put any apparent
limits on the power of algorithms.  However, once we
give formal, mathematically rigorous, definitions of algorithms, then we can
prove theorems about what is not computable.

Nonetheless, by exploiting the full power of the Church-Turing thesis,
we can prove results about non-computability and non-decidability
in a very general way.  The key technical result will establish
the non-computability of the ``\idxnm{halting problem}''.

The Church-Turing thesis provides the link between the informal
 and formal definitions of ``algorithm''. In fact,
even before giving a mathematical definition of algorithms, notably
in terms of Turing machines, we can exploit the Church-Turing
thesis to prove results about non-computability.  Our proofs will
be completely rigorous, but will assume some properties
about the formal definitions of algorithms that will be provided
later, notably when Turing machines are defined.

For simplicity, and concreteness, we let $\Gamma = \{0, 1\}$,
and restrict our attention
to algorithms that accept strings from~$\Gamma^*$ as input
and output strings from~$\Gamma^*$ (if they generate outputs).%
\footnote{This can be done without loss of generality, since a larger alphabet~$\Sigma^*$
can be accommodated by representing distinct symbols from~$\Sigma$ by
distinct finite length codewords from~$\Gamma^* = \{0,1 \}^*$.}
The Church-Turing thesis implicitly includes the following assumptions:
\begin{description}
\item[A unified representation scheme for all algorithms:]
\index{unified representation of algorithms}%
There is a single set of ``instruction types'' that suffices to
describe an arbitrary algorithm. The informal notion of algorithm
required that any algorithm be described by a finite set of
unambiguous instructions. The further assumption that there is
a unified representation scheme means that there are only
finitely many types of instructions. Any particular algorithm
can be defined by combining instructions appropriately.

Concomitantly, any algorithm can be described by a
finite string, over the alphabet~$\Gamma$,
that encodes the instructions for
the algorithm. This is similar to the way that arbitrary first-order
$L$-formulas can be described by strings over~$\Sigma^{\Tfo-L+}$.
If $M$ is an algorithm, we write $\lgn M \rgn$ to denote
a string that encodes the unambiguous instructions for~$M$.
The string~$\lgn M \rgn$ is called the
``G{\"o}del number\index{Godel number@{G{\"o}del number}}'' of~$M$.\footnote{The
terminology ``G{\"o}del number'' is a bit inaccurate at the moment, since
$\lgn M \rgn$ is a string in~$\Gamma^*$, not a number. The terminology
reflects the historical fact that the first use of G{\"o}del numbers
was based on integers, not strings.
Chapter~\ref{chap:ArithmeticIncompleteness} will use integers instead of
members of~$\Gamma^*$ to encode the description of a Turing
machine; however, for the time being, our G{\"o}del numbers
are strings from $\{0,1\}^*$.}

\item[A universal algorithm.]\index{universal algorithm}
A universal algorithm is an
algorithm that can simulate any other algorithm. Specifically,
there is a universal algorithm~$U(v, w)$ that takes two inputs:
The string~$v$ is supposed to equal $\lgn M \rgn$ encoding
the finite set of instructions for some algorithm~$M$.
The string $w$ is intended to be the input to~$M$.

This universal algorithm~$U$, when given inputs $v =\lgn M \rgn$
and~$w$, can simulate, in a step-by-step fashion,
the action of the algorithm~$M$
on input~$w$. As it
simulates $M(w)$, the algorithm~$U$ can detect things 
such as whether $M$ has accepted or rejected, whether $M$ has
halted or is still running, and whether the algorithm has just
finished outputting a string.

In other words, the universal algorithm is able to parse the instructions
encoded by the string $v = \lgn M \rgn$, and then act upon those instructions
to simulate the actions of the algorithm~$M$. A universal algorithm
is possible only because of the assumption of a unified representation
for algorithms.

\item[Malleability of algorithms.]\index{malleability}
It is implicit in the notion of a universal algorithm
that a string~$\lgn M \rgn$ encoding a set of instructions
can be parsed and even modified to form a different algorithm.
For example, given the G{\"o}del number~$\lgn M \rgn$
of an algorithm~$M$, one can construct
the G{\"o}del number~$\lgn N \rgn$
of an algorithm~$N$ that acts exactly like~$M$
except that $N$~will accept if $M$ rejects, and $N$~will reject
if $M$ accepts. Furthermore, this mapping $\lgn M \rgn \mapsto \lgn N \rgn$
is a computable function.

For a second example, suppose $\lgn M \rgn$ is the
G{\"o}del number of an algorithm~$M$
that accepts a pair of inputs $\langle v, w \rangle$. Then
for any particular value of~$v$, we can form the
G{\"o}del number~$\lgn M _ v \rgn$
of an algorithm~$M_v$ such that $M_v$~takes a single input~$w$
and acts exactly like $M$ on input $\langle v, w\rangle$.
In other words, $M_v$ is the same as~$M$, but with $v$ set to
a fixed value.
The malleability condition asserts that the map
$\langle \lgn M \rgn, v \rangle \mapsto \lgn M_v \rgn$ is computable.

As a third example, suppose $\lgn M_1 \rgn$ and $\lgn M_2 \rgn$
are G{\"o}del numbers for algorithms that compute unary functions
$f_1$ and~$f_2$. Then an algorithm~$N$ can be constructed that
computes the composition $f_2\circ f_1$ by letting $N$~first
run~$M_1$ and use the output of~$M_1$ as the input to~$M_2$.
The mapping $\langle \rgn M_1, M_2 \rangle \mapsto \lgn N \rgn$
can be a computable function.
\end{description}

These three assumptions of a unified representation for algorithmic
instructions, a universal algorithm, and the malleability of
algorithms may sound very strong, but
they should not be particularly surprising. First, given our intuition
that modern-day computers can carry out arbitrary algorithms\footnote{Putting
aside considerations of efficiency and the limitations of time and space.},
we see immediately that instructions can be presented from some finite
instruction set of the type used by computers.  The G{\"o}del number
of an algorithm is just the source code for the algorithm.
That is all that is meant by the existence of a
uniform representation.  Second, the fact that compilers and interpreters
can handle arbitrary programs means that they are, in effect, universal
algorithms.  Third, the malleability of algorithms just means that
the instructions for an algorithm (as encoded by its G{\"o}del number or
its source code) may be locally modified to change
the behavior of the program.
In particular, the malleability of algorithms
allows us to modify the inputs to a program or the output behavior of
a program and to compose two programs.  It is important
to note that these kinds of operations only modify the
input/output conventions for programs; they do not require
understanding anything at all about the inner workings of
programs.

The Church-Turing thesis states that arbitrary algorithms
can be implemented via Turing machines.  This gives
another justification for the assumptions of
a unified representation for algorithmic
instructions, a universal algorithm, and malleability.
Turing machines act like modern-day computers,
except with a highly restricted set of possible
instructions. Furthermore, Turing's original paper
describes a \idxnm{universal Turing machine}, which is
just a universal algorithm specialized for Turing machines.

As a philosophical side remark, it is worth mentioning
that the existence of a universal algorithm is a rather
striking fact, even an {\em a priori} unexpected fact. If one
did not know the Church-Turing thesis, it could certainly seem
plausible that there is a hierarchy of possible ``instruction sets''
for algorithms, with the instruction sets increasing in computational
strength as one goes up the hierarchy. That is to say, one might
envision a hierarchy of increasingly strong
programming languages $C_1$, $C_2$, $C_3$, etc.,
such that each programming language~$C_{i+1}$ can express more algorithms
than the programming language~$C_i$. In this case, there could be no
universal algorithm, since it could not be written in any
particular programming language~$C_i$. This scenario
is precluded by the Church-Turing thesis!

The rest of this section will prove undecidability results.
The assumption of a unified representation for algorithms
is crucial for the results below.
However, the full strength of the
\idxnm{malleability} assumption will be used only for the final
result about the halting problem ($\THalt_0$).
The assumption that there is a universal algorithm
will not be used at all.

\subsection{Undecidability via cardinality}

The ``unified representation\index{unified representation of algorithms}'' assumption
that every algorithm~$M$ can be described
by some string~$\lgn M \rgn$ (over a fixed language~$\Gamma$)
immediately
implies that there are only countably many algorithms,
just because there are only countably many possible strings
in~$\Gamma^*$. However, there are uncountably many
subsets of $\mathbb N$, and likewise, uncountably many subsets
of~$\Sigma^*$ for any alphabet~$\Sigma^*$. In other words,
there are more subsets of~$\Sigma^*$ than there are algorithms~$M$.
This immediately gives the following theorem:
\begin{theorem}\label{thm:Cantors}
For any alphabet~$\Sigma$, there is a subset of~$\Sigma^*$ which
is not decidable. Likewise, there is a relation on~$\mathbb N$ which
is not decidable.
\end{theorem}

Proving this theorem using the countable/uncountable distinction
is unfortunately not particularly illuminating, since it gives
no idea what particular set is not decidable. We will improve
on this below by defining the ``\idxnm{halting problem}'' and
proving it is not decidable. That will give a constructive definition
of a set that is not decidable. It will also serve as the basis
for proving many other sets to not be decidable.

First, however, it is interesting to review the proof
that there are uncountably many subsets of~$\mathbb N$.  (Essentially
the same proof shows that there are uncountably many subsets
of~$\Sigma^*$.)  This theorem was originally proved by Cantor
in 1891 to prove the uncountability
of the set of real numbers; its proof hinges
on what is called the Cantor \idxnm{diagonal argument}.
We will use the diagonal argument again for the halting problem.
\begin{theorem}[Cantor]\label{thm:PowerNuncountable}
There are uncountably many subsets of~$\mathbb N$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The proof is by contradiction.
Suppose there are countably many subsets of~$\mathbb N$.
Thus all the subsets of~$\mathbb N$ can be enumerated
in an infinite sequence $X_0, X_1, X_2, \ldots$.
That is, each $X_i$ is a subset of~$\mathbb N$, and every
subset of~$\mathbb N$ is equal to (at least) one of the sets~$X_i$.

Define a subset~$Y$ of~$\mathbb N$ by specifying, for all~$i\in \mathbb N$, that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:CantorDiagonalSet}
i \in Y ~\Leftrightarrow~ i \notin X_i.
\end{equation}
We claim that it is impossible that $Y=X_i$ for any fixed~$i$.
The reason is that, if $Y = X_i$, then $i \in Y$ if and only if
$i \notin X_i$, and the latter is the same as $i \notin Y$.
And, having $i\in Y $ if and only if $i \notin Y$ is impossible.
On the other hand, $Y$~is definitely a subset of~$\mathbb N$, so
by the choice of the~$X_i$'s, the set~$Y$ is equal to some~$X_i$.

This is a contradiction. Thus, we have proved that it is
impossible that the set of subsets of~$\mathbb N$ is
countable.
\end{proof}

The proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:PowerNuncountable}
is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:PowerNuncountableThm}.
The figure makes it evident why this proof is called
a \idxnm{diagonal argument}.
\begin{figure}
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{c|cccccc}
& 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 & $\cdots$ \\
\hline
$X_0\tallerMore$ & {\bf 0} & 1 & 1 & 1& 0 & $\cdots$ \\[0.1ex]
$X_1$ & 1 & {\bf 1} & 0 & 1 & 1 & $\cdots$ \\[0.1ex]
$X_2$ & 0 & 0 & {\bf 1} & 1 & 1 & $\cdots$ \\[0.1ex]
$X_3$ & 1 & 0 & 1 & {\bf 0} & 0& $\cdots$ \\[0.1ex]
$X_4$ & 1 & 1 & 0 & 0 & {\bf 1} & $\cdots$ \\[0.1ex]
$\vdots$ & $\vdots$ & $\vdots$ & $\vdots$ & $\vdots$ & $\vdots$ & $\ddots$ \\[0.1ex]
$Y$ & {\bf 1} & {\bf 0} & {\bf 0} & {\bf 1} & {\bf 0} & $\cdots$
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
\caption{An example of how the Cantor diagonal argument works.
Each $X_i$ is a subset of~$\mathbb N$. The entry in the
row~$X_i$ and column~$j$ is equal to~1 if $j\in X_i$ and equal to~$0$
otherwise. In other words, it is the value of $\chi_{X_i}(j)$.
The set~$Y$ is defined by Equation~\ref{eq:CantorDiagonalSet}
by letting the value for $\chi_Y(i) = 1-\chi(X_i)$. In other words,
$Y$~is defined by flipping the values (shown in boldface) on the
diagonal.  Therefore, the set~$Y$ cannot be equal to any~$X_i$.}
\label{fig:PowerNuncountableThm}
\end{figure}


\subsection{Undecidability via the halting problem}

We now give an example of a particular, concrete problem
set that is not decidable, namely the \idxnm{halting problem}.
The proof that the halting
problem is undecidable will exploit the diagonal argument
used in the proof of Cantor's Theorem~\ref{thm:Cantors}. Now, however,
we must diagonalize against all decidable sets.

By the assumption of uniform representations for algorithms,
every algorithm~$M$ has a representation~$\lgn M \rgn$ which
is a string over a (fixed) alphabet~$\Gamma$.  The string~$\lgn M \rgn$
is often called the
``G{\"o}del number\index{Godel number@{G{\"o}del number}}'' of~$M$.
We assume without loss of generality that {\em any} string
in~$\Gamma$ can be viewed a G{\"o}del number for an algorithm.
This can be done without loss of generality since any
$w \in \Gamma^*$ that does not encode a syntactically correct
set of instructions for an algorithm can be treated as being
a G{\"o}del number for a fixed trivial algorithm, say the algorithm
that ignores its input and immediately rejects and halts.

We define three versions of the halting problem. Recall that
~$\Gamma = \{ 0,1 \}$ is being used both as the alphabet
for inputs to algorithms and as the alphabet used to encode
G{\"o}del numbers of Turing machines. On any given input~$w\in \Gamma^*$,
an algorithm will either eventually halt (with an output or with an
accept/reject decision) or will run forever. We write $M(w)$
for the action of the algorithm~$M$ when given the input~$w$.

\begin{definition}
The \emph{\idxnm{halting problem} with input} is
the binary relation
\[
\THalt_1 ~=~ \{ \langle \lgn M \rgn, w \rangle : \hbox{$M(w)$ halts} \}.\footnote{%
A more correct, but overly pedantic way to define $\THalt_1$ would
be that it equals
\[
\{ \langle v, w \rangle : \hbox{$v$ is a G{\"o}del number for an algorithm~$M$ and $M(w)$ halts} \}.
\]
} % End of footnote
\]
The \idxem{self-halting problem} is the unary relation defined by
\[
\THalt_\TSelf ~=~ \{ \lgn M \rgn : \hbox{$M ( \lgn M \rgn )$ halts} \} .
\]
The \emph{halting problem} is the unary relation defined by
\[
\THalt_0 ~=~ \{ \lgn M \rgn : \hbox{$M ( \epsilon )$ halts} \} .
\]
\end{definition}
Note that something somewhat self-referential is happening in the
definition $\THalt_\TSelf$.  We have defined that $\lgn M \rgn$ is
``self-halting'', namely is in $\THalt_\TSelf$, if and only if
the algorithm~$M$ eventually halts when it
is run with its input equal to its own
G\"del number~$\lgn M \rgn$. There is nothing circular
or otherwise problematic about this definition. And, certainly,
the other two halting problems are defined straightforwardly enough
without any self-referential aspects.

We will use the diagonal method to prove that the self-halting problem
is undecidable. The undecidability of the self-halting problem
can then be used to prove the undecidability of $\THalt_1$ and $\THalt_0$.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:selfHaltUndecidable}
The self-halting problem $\THalt_\TSelf$ is undecidable.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Suppose, for the sake of a contradiction, that there
is some algorithm~$M$ that decides
the self-halting problem $\THalt_\TSelf$.  Modify~$M$
to form a new algorithm~$N$ by letting $N(w)$
run identically like~$M(w)$ until $M(w)$ halts.
If $M(w)$~is about to reject, $N(w)$ halts (and, say, accepts).
If $M(w)$~is about to accept, $N(w)$ enters an infinite loop
and never halts. In other words, $N$~is the following algorithm:
\begin{tabbing}
\AlgFirstIn\=\Algin\=\Algin\=\Algin\=\Algin\=\kill
\>\underline{Assumption:} $M$ decides $\THalt_\TSelf$. \\
\>\underline{Input:} $w \in \Gamma^*$ \\
\>\underline{Algorithm:} \\
\>Run $M$ on input~$w$ until it halts. \\
\>If $M(w)$ rejects, \\
\>\>Accept and halt. \\
\>If $M(w)$ accepts, \\
\> \> Enter an infinite loop and never halt.
\end{tabbing}
Since $N$ is an algorithm, it of course must have a
G{\"o}del number~$\lgn N \rgn$.
Taking the input~$w$ to~$N$ to be equal to $\lgn N \rgn$, we have
\begin{implicationsnarrow}
$N(\lgn N \rgn)$ halts
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $M(\lgn N \rgn)$ rejects \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $\lgn N \rgn \notin \THalt_\TSelf$
     & Since $M$ decides $\THalt_\TSelf$ \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $N(\lgn N \rgn)$ does not halt
     & By the definition of $\THalt_\TSelf$
\end{implicationsnarrow}
This gives that $N(\lgn N \rgn)$ halts if and only if it does not
halt, which is, of course, a contradiction.
Therefore, there cannot be an algorithm~$M$ that decides
the self-halting problem $\THalt_\TSelf$.
\end{proof}

Figure~\ref{fig:selfHaltUndecidableThm} gives a more
pictorial version of the proof,
and illustrates how it is a diagonal argument.

The enumeration order is unimportant, but for the sake of
consistency with earlier constructions, they can be taken
in order of increasing length, lexicographically ordering
strings of a given length. Each $v_i$ is the G{\"o}del number
$v_i = \lgn M_i \rgn$ of an algorithm~$M_i$.  The entry in the

In the final row~$Y$, the column~$v_i$ entry indicates

The undecidability of the self-halting problem can be used
to prove the undecidability of the other two halting problems.
The halting problem with input, $\THalt_1$, is a
generalization of the self-halting problem, so its undecidability
follows immediately.
\begin{theorem}\label{thm:Halt1Undecidable}
$\THalt_1$ is undecidable.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Suppose that $\THalt_1$ is decided by an algorithm~$M$.
Then the following algorithm~$N$ decides $\THalt_\TSelf$:

\vbox{
\begin{tabbing}
\AlgFirstIn\=\Algin\=\Algin\=\Algin\=\Algin\=\kill
\>\underline{Assumption:} $M$ decides $\THalt_1$. \\
\>\underline{Input:} $w \in \Gamma^*$ \\
\>\underline{Algorithm:} \\
\>Run $M(w,w)$ until it halts. \\
\>If $M(w,w)$ accepts, accept and halt. \\
\>If $M(w,w)$ rejects, reject and halt.
\end{tabbing}
}

The point is that the input~$w$ is equal to a G{\"o}del number~$\lgn M_w\rgn$
of an algorithm~$M_w$, and that $\lgn M_w \rgn \in \THalt_\TSelf$ if
and only if $\langle \lgn M_w \rgn, \lgn M_w \rgn \rangle \in \THalt_1$,
since these both mean ``$M_w(\lgn M_w \rgn)$ halts''.

Thus an algorithm~$M$ that decides $\THalt_1$ gives immediately an
algorithm that decides $\TSelf_\THalt$.  But there is no algorithm
that decides $\TSelf_\THalt$, so $\THalt_1$ must be undecidable.
\end{proof}

The undecidability of the $\THalt_\TSelf$ and $\THalt_1$
were proved without using the assumption of a universal
algorithm, and without using the full strength
of the \idxnm{malleability} assumption. In fact, none of the algorithms
discussed yet in this chapter have an input
which is used as a G{\"o}del number of an algorithm.
For example, the two last
proofs created an algorithm~$N$ assuming the existence of
some particular, concrete algorithm~$M$. Those algorithms
were formulated directly in terms of a fixed~$M$; what they
did {\em not} was
take $\lgn M \rgn$ as an input and use this to create~$\lgn N\rgn$.

The next theorem will need to use the full strength
of the \idxnm{malleability}
assumption. Specifically, the proof will use an
algorithm that takes the G{\"o}del number $\lgn M \rgn$
of an algorithm, and modifies~$\lgn M \rgn$ to the obtain a
G{\"o}del number~$\lgn N \rgn$ of a related algorithm.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:Halt0Undecidable}
The halting problem $\THalt_0$ is undecidable.
\end{theorem}

Our proof will use the malleability assumption, but
does not need to use the universal algorithm.
We use two assumptions about the \idxnm{malleability} of the algorithms.
\begin{itemizeDot}
\item
The first assumption is that there is an algorithm, which given a string~$w$ as
input, produces the G{\"o}del number of an algorithm~$M_w$ that
computes the constant function which equals~$w$ for all inputs. To be
precise, let $g_w$~denote the unary function such that $g_w(v) = w$
for all~$v$. We claim that there is a computable function~$f$
such that, for all~$w$,  the value of~$f(w)$ is equal to
the G{\"o}del number $\lgn M_w \rgn$
of an algorithm~$M_w$ which computes
the constant function~$g_w$.

To state this assumption in terms of computer programs,
this is saying there is an effective procedure which, given any~$w$,
generates the source
code for a program that ignores its input and just outputs~$w$.
This program might consist primarily of a print statement
that outputs the string~$w$.
\item The second assumption is that there is a
computable binary function~$f^\prime$
such that the following holds:
Whenever $M_1$~is an
algorithm that (partial) computes a unary function~$h$, and $M_2$~is
an algorithm that takes a single input~$w$, then
$f^\prime(\lgn M_1\rgn, \lgn M_2 \rgn)$ is equal to
the G{\"o}del number~$\lgn M^\prime \rgn$ of an algorithm~$M^\prime$
that, on input~$w\in \Sigma^*$, acts by first computing $v = h(w)$
and then runs $M_2(v)$.

In particular, whenever $M_1$~is an
algorithm that computes a unary function~$h$, and $M_2$~is
an algorithm that decides a unary relation~$R$, then
$f^\prime(\lgn M_1\rgn, \lgn M_2 \rgn)$ is equal to
the G{\"o}del number~$\lgn M^\prime \rgn$ of an algorithm~$M^\prime$
that decides the unary relation $R\circ h = \{ w : h(w) \in R \}$.

The intuition is that $M^\prime(w)$~acts by first running the
algorithm~$M_1$, and then running~$M_2$
using the output from~$M_1$ as the input
to~$M_2$. To state this in terms of computer programs, it means
just means that the source code for the programs $M_1$ and~$M_2$
can be combined to form the source code for $M^\prime$
by letting the output of~$M_1$ be the input to~$M_2$ and then letting
the resulting output of~$M_2$ be the final output of~$M^\prime$.
\end{itemizeDot}

\begin{proof}
We prove Theorem~\ref{thm:Halt0Undecidable} by contradiction.
Suppose that $\THalt_0$ is decided by an algorithm~$N$.
This means that $N(\lgn M \rgn)$ correctly decides whether
$M(\epsilon)$ halts,
for all inputs~$\lgn M \rgn$. Consider the following algorithm~$N^\prime$:

\vbox{
\begin{tabbing}
\AlgFirstIn\=\Algin\=\Algin\=\Algin\=\Algin\=\kill
\>\underline{Assumption:} $N$ decides $\THalt_0$, and $f, f^\prime$ are as above.\\
\>\underline{Input:} $\lgn M \rgn \in \Sigma^*$ \\
\>\underline{Algorithm $N^\prime$:} \\
\>Run $N$ on input $f^\prime(f(\lgn M \rgn),\lgn M \rgn)$. \\
\>If $N$ accepts, then accept. \\
\>If $N$ rejects, then reject.
\end{tabbing}
}
\noindent
To understand this, note that $f(\lgn M \rgn)$ is
the G{\"o}del number of an algorithm that outputs~$\lgn M \rgn$.
Thus $f^\prime(f(\lgn M \rgn),\lgn M \rgn)$ equals the G{\"o}del number
of an algorithm~$M^\prime$ that, for any input~$w$,
running $M^\prime$ on input~$w$ yields the same result
as running~$M$ on input~$\lgn M \rgn$.
We have:
\begin{implicationsnarrow}
$N^\prime(\lgn M \rgn )$ accepts
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $N(f^\prime(f(\lgn M \rgn),\lgn M \rgn))$ accepts \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $f^\prime(f(\lgn M \rgn),\lgn M \rgn)\in \THalt_0$ \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $\lgn M^\prime\rgn\in \THalt_0 $ \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $M^\prime(\epsilon)$ halts \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $M(\lgn M \rgn )$ halts \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $\lgn M \rgn \in \THalt_\TSelf$
\end{implicationsnarrow}
Since $N^\prime$~accepts its input~$\lgn M \rgn$ if and only
if $\lgn M \rgn \in \THalt_\TSelf$, the set $\THalt_\TSelf$
is decided by~$N^\prime$. But this contradicts
Theorem~\ref{thm:selfHaltUndecidable} that $\THalt_\TSelf$ is undecidable.
Therefore, $\THalt_0$ is not decidable.
\end{proof}

The above proof implicitly used the notion of a ``\idxnm{many-one reduction}''
from $\THalt_\TSelf$ to $\THalt_0$:
\begin{definition}
Let $R$ and~$S$ be unary relations on~$\Sigma^*$ (or on~$\mathbb N$).
A \idxem{many-one reduction} from~$R$ to~$S$ is a computable
function $f: \Sigma^* \rightarrow \Sigma^*$ (or $f:\mathbb N \rightarrow \mathbb N$)
such that for all $w \in \Sigma^*$  (or, all $w\in \mathbb N$),
we have $w \in R$ if and only if $f(w)\in S$.

If there is a many-one reduction from $R$ to~$S$, then
$R$~is said to be \idxem{many-one reducible} to~$S$.
\end{definition}
The point of a many-reduction from $R$ to~$S$ is that deciding
(or semideciding)~$R$ can be reduced to the problem of 
of deciding (or semideciding)~$S$.

In the above proof, letting $g(w) = f^\prime (f(w), w)$, the proof argument
showed that $g$~is a many-one reduction from $\THalt_\TSelf$ to $\THalt_0$.
This was used to show that $\THalt_0$ is undecidable since $\THalt_\TSelf$
is undecidable. The general theorem this is based on is:
\begin{theorem}
Suppose $R$ and $S$ are unary relations and that $f$~is a many-one reduction
from $R$ to~$S$. If $S$~is decidable, then $R$ is decidable. 
If $S$~is semidecidable, then $R$ is semidecidable. Thus if $S$ is c.e.,
$R$ is c.e.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The proof of this is simple: To decide whether $w\in R$, compute $f(w)$
and decide whether $f(w)\in S$.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}
The proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:Halt1Undecidable} showed that
$\THalt_1$ is undecidable by observing that
the function $w \mapsto \langle w, w\rangle$ is
a many-one reduction from $\THalt_\TSelf$ to~$\THalt_1$.
Likewise, the proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:Halt0Undecidable}
showing $\THalt_0$ is undecidable used a many-one reduction
from $\THalt_\TSelf$ to $\THalt_0$.
\end{example}

\subsection{Self-referential algorithms}\label{sec:SelfReferAlg}

A key step in the above proof that $\THalt_0$ is undecidable
was to construct an algorithm~$M^\prime$ such that
for any input~$w$, running $M^\prime(w)$ has the same effect as
running $M(\lgn M \rgn)$. This means that $M^\prime$ has the
effect of ``running $M$ on itself'', where the ``itself'' part is referring
to~$\lgn M \rgn$. This construction can be sharpened to create an
algorithm~$D_M$ that computes $M(\lgn D_M \rgn)$. This can be
phrased informally to say that ``$D_M$ is running M on itself'', where
the ``itself'' part refers~$\lgn D_M\rgn$. Loosely speaking, $D_M$~is
doing something with its own G{\"o}del number, namely running~$M$ on it.
For this reason, $D_M$ is an example of a ``\idxnm{self-referential}''
algorithm, because it acts on its own G{\"o}del number.
(The letter ``$D$'' stands for ``diagonal''
since $D_M$ can be used in diagonal arguments to prove something
is not computable.)

\begin{example}\label{example:quineProgram}
Let $M$ be the algorithm that computes the
identity function, namely $M(w)$ outputs~$w$.
Then $D_M$ is an algorithm with G{\"o}del number~$\lgn D_M\rgn$
such that, for any input~$w$, $D_M(w)$ outputs~$\lgn D_M\rgn$.
(The input~$w$ to $D_M$ is ignored.)

Note that $M(\lgn D_M\rgn)$ and $D_M(w)$ produce the identical output,
namely~$\lgn D_M\rgn$.
\end{example}

We still need to prove that diagonal algorithms~$D_M$ always exist.
The existence of an algorithm~$D_M$ as in the example that outputs its own
G{\"o}del number may seem a little
counterintuitive (or maybe even {\em very} counterintuitive). 
If algorithms are written as C~programs for instance,
this means there is a C~program that outputs its own source code.
Such a program is called a ``\idxnm{quine}'' or a ``\idxnm{self-printing} program''.
Quines and self-printing programs are indeed possible. In fact,
they are possible for essentially any computer language, not just for C programs.
The reader is encouraged to search the internet for examples.

We'll show that the existence of self-referential algorithms follows
from the \idxnm{malleability} assumptions.  For this,
we formally state and prove the existence of
a self-referential algorithm~$D_M$:

\begin{theorem}[Diagonal Theorem for Algorithms]
\label{thm:Diag}\index{diagonal theorem}
Let $M$ be an algorithm that takes a single input $w\in\Sigma^*$.
There is an algorithm~$D_M$ with G{\"o}del number~$\lgn D_M\rgn$
such that, for any input~$w$, running $D_M(w)$ produces
the same result as running $M(\lgn D_M\rgn)$.
\end{theorem}

Note that the input~$w$ to~$D_M$ is ignored. The only reason for having
the input~$w$ is that by convention any function must take at least one input.

\begin{proof}
Let $f$ and $f^\prime$ be the computable functions
described in items (a) and~(b) immediately
following the statement of Theorem~\ref{thm:Halt0Undecidable}.
Thus $f(w)$ produces the G{\"o}del number $\lgn M_w\rgn$
of an algorithm~$M_w$ that ignores its input and outputs~$w$.
And $f^\prime(\lgn M_1\rgn , \lgn M_2 \rgn )$ is equal to
the G{\"o}del number of an algorithm that has the same effect
as running $M_2$ on the output (if any) of~$M_1$. For the present
proofs, the important points are
that $f^\prime(f(\lgn M_1 \rgn), \lgn M_2 \rgn)$ is the G{\"o}del
number of a Turing machine that does the same thing
(halts, rejects, accepts, and/or outputs something) as
$M_2(\lgn M_1\rgn)$, and that
the mapping
$\langle \lgn M_1 \rgn, \lgn M_2 \rgn \rangle \mapsto f^\prime(f(\lgn M_1 \rgn), \lgn M_2 \rgn)$
is computable.

Form an algorithm~$E_M$ so that $E_M(w)$ runs $M(f^\prime(f(w), w))$.
Since any $w$ is a G{\"o}del number~$\lgn N \rgn$ of an algorithm~$N$,
this is the same as $E_M(\lgn N \rgn) = M(f^\prime(f(\lgn N \rgn), \lgn N \rgn))$.
By construction,
$E_M(\lgn N \rgn)$ always yields the same result as
running $M$ on the G{\"o}del number of an algorithm that ignores
its input and computes
$N(\lgn N \rgn)$.\footnote{The names of the variables $M$ and~$N$
of variables are swapped here, but $E_N$ is the same as the algorithm~$N^\prime$
of the proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:Halt0Undecidable} except that
now running $M$ might have possibly outcomes other than accept and reject.}

Define $D_M$ to be the algorithm with G{\"o}del number
$\lgn D_M \rgn$ equal to $f^\prime(f(\lgn E_M \rgn ),\lgn E_M \rgn)$.
The algorithm~$D_M$ acts by first forming $\lgn E_M\rgn$ and
then running $E_M(\lgn E_M\rgn)$. The action of $E_M(\lgn E_M \rgn)$
is to form $f^\prime(f(\lgn E_M \rgn ),\lgn E_M \rgn)$ and run
$M$ with input $f^\prime(f(\lgn E_M \rgn ),\lgn E_M \rgn)$.
To summarize, the algorithm~$D_M$ acts as follows:
\begin{tabbing}
\AlgFirstIn\=\Algin\=\Algin\=\Algin\=\Algin\=\kill
\>\underline{Assumption:} $M$ is an algorithm that takes one input. \\
\>\underline{Input:} None (or, ignored) \\
\>\underline{Algorithm $D_M$:} \\
\>Run $M$ on input $f^\prime(f(\lgn E_M \rgn ),\lgn E_M \rgn)$. \\
\>(The result of $M$ is used as the result of~$D_M$.)
\end{tabbing}
We chose $D_M$ so that $\lgn D_M\rgn$ is equal to
$f^\prime(f(\lgn E_M \rgn ),\lgn E_M \rgn)$. Thus, the effect of
$D_M$~is the same as $M(\lgn D_M \rgn)$. This proves the Diagonal Theorem.
\end{proof}

The Diagonal Theorem can be used to give another proof
of the undecidability of $\THalt_0$ that does not require
using the fact that $\THalt_\TSelf$ is undecidable.

\begin{proof}[Second proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:Halt0Undecidable}]
The proof is again by contradiction. Suppose $\THalt_0$ is
decided by an algorithm~$N$. Note that $N(w)$ always halts and either
accepts or rejects. Modify $N$ to form an
algorithm~$N^\prime$ so that if $N(w)$~rejects, then $N^\prime(w)$ halts,
and if $N(w)$~accepts, $N^\prime$~enters an infinite loop
and never halts. Namely:
\begin{tabbing}
\AlgFirstIn\=\Algin\=\Algin\=\Algin\=\Algin\=\kill
\>\underline{Assumption:} $N$ decides $\THalt_0$.\\
\>\underline{Input:} $w \in \Sigma^*$ \\
\>\underline{Algorithm $N^\prime$:} \\
\>Run $N$ on input $w$. \\
\>If $N(w)$ rejects, then halt. \\
\>If $N(w)$ accepts, then enter an infinite loop and never halt.
\end{tabbing}
Form the algorithm~$D_{N^\prime}$ as in the Diagonal Theorem.
Then,
\begin{implicationsnarrow}
$D_{N^\prime}(\epsilon)$ halts
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $N^\prime(\lgn D_{N^\prime}\rgn)$ halts \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $N(\lgn D_{N^\prime}\rgn)$ rejects \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $D_{N^\prime}(\epsilon)$ does not halt (since $N$ decides $\THalt_0$)
\end{implicationsnarrow}
This is a contradiction and proves the theorem.
\end{proof}

See Exercise~\ref{exer:DiagParameter} for another version
of the Diagonal Theorem that allows an additional input~$v$ as
a side parameter.

\subsection{Computably inseparable c.e.\ sets}

For another application of the Diagonal Theorem,
we prove the existence of ``\idxnm{computably inseparable}'' pairs
of computably enumerable sets.
\begin{definition}
Let $X$ and~$Y$ be disjoint subsets of $\Sigma^*$.
The sets $X$ and~$Y$ said
to be \idxem{computably separable} if there is
a decidable set~$Z$ such that $X \subseteq Z$ and
$Y \cap Z = \varnothing$. Otherwise, they are
\idxem{computably inseparable}
\end{definition}
The separation conditions can also be written as
$X \subseteq Z$ and $Y \subseteq \overline Z$.
Note that if $X$ and~$Y$ are disjoint and computably inseparable,
then neither one is decidable.  Otherwise,
we could take one of $Z = X$ or $Z =\overline Y$ as the
decidable separating set.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:AcceptRejectInsep}
There are computably enumerable sets $X$ and~$Y$
which are computably inseparable.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Define $\TAccept_0$ to be the set of G{\"o}del numbers~$\lgn M \rgn$
such that $M(\epsilon)$ eventually accepts. Similarly, define
$\TReject_0$ to be the set of G{\"o}del numbers~$\lgn M \rgn$
such that $M(\epsilon)$ eventually rejects.

We claim that $\TAccept_0$ and $\TReject_0$ are c.e.
For this, it is enough to show they are semidecidable.
We omit the proof that they are semidecidable
here: Exercise~\ref{exer:CEonly}(b) asks you to supply it.

Now we show that $\TAccept_0$ and $\TReject_0$ are
computably inseparable. Suppose, for the sake of a contradiction,
that there is a decidable set~$Z$ such that
$\TAccept_0 \subseteq Z$ and $\TReject_0 \subseteq \overline Z$.
The complement~$\overline Z$ is also decidable, by some algorithm~$N$.
Let $D_N$ be the self-referential algorithm
from the Diagonal Theorem~\ref{thm:Diag}.  Then
\begin{implicationsnarrow}
$D_N(\epsilon)$ accepts
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $ N(\lgn D_N \rgn)$ accepts & by choice of $D_N$ \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $ \lgn D_N \rgn \notin Z$ & by choice of $N$ \\
& $\Rightarrow$ & $ \lgn D_N(\epsilon) \rgn$ does not accept & by choice of $Z$
\end{implicationsnarrow}
Therefore $D_N(\epsilon)$ does not accept. But also,
\begin{implicationsnarrow}
$D_N$ rejects
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $ N(\lgn D_N \rgn)$ rejects \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $ \lgn D_N \rgn \in Z$ \\
& $\Rightarrow$ & $ \lgn D_N(\epsilon) \rgn$ does not reject
\end{implicationsnarrow}
Thus $D_N(\epsilon)$ does not reject.
Now we have a contradiction: 
The algorithm~$N$ deciding~$\overline Z$ has to
halt on all inputs, so $D_N(\epsilon)$ must halt
and either accept or reject.  Therefore, no
decidable separating set~$Z$ can exist.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Rice's Theorem}\index{Rice's Theorem|(}

Rice's Theorem states that
for any non-trivial property~$P$ of computably enumerable
sets, it is not decidable whether a given algorithm
enumerates a member of~$P$. In other words, there is no
decision procedure, which given a G{\"o}del number~$\lgn M\rgn$,
decides whether the set enumerated by~$M$ satisfies the property~$P$.
Informally, this means there is no
general way to examine the source code
of a program (the G{\"o}del number of an algorithm) and determine
reliably what the program does. This can be viewed as a theoretical
barrier for things such as checking the correctness of programs,
or checking that a program is virus-free, etc.

Let's make this precise with a couple of definitions and a theorem.

\begin{definition}
Let $P$ be a collection of c.e.\ subsets of~$\mathbb N$.
We call $P$ a \idxem{non-trivial} property of c.e.\ sets if $P$ is nonempty
and does not contain all c.e.\ subsets of~$\mathbb N$.
In other words, at least one c.e.\ set is in~$P$, but not all c.e.\ sets
are in~$P$.
\end{definition}

\begin{example}
The set of nonempty c.e.\ subsets of~$\mathbb N$
is a non-trivial property of c.e.\ sets. The set of finite
subsets of~$N$ is a non-trivial property of c.e.\ sets.
The set of c.e.\ subsets of the even integers is a
non-trivial property of c.e.\ sets.
\end{example}

\begin{definition}
Let $M$ be an algorithm that enumerates integers. We let $L^e(M)$ denote
the set enumerated by~$M$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}
Suppose $P$ is a non-trivial collection of subsets
of~$\mathbb N$. Let $X$ be the set of G{\"o}del numbers
of algorithms that enumerate a member of~$P$,
\[
     X := \{ \lgn M \rgn : L^e(M) \in P \}.
\]
Then $X$ is not decidable.
\end{theorem}

Rice's Theorem is stated (as is usual) about computably enumerable
subsets of~$\mathbb N$. Similar results also hold
for partial computable functions.
For this, see Exercise~\ref{exer:RicePC}

\begin{proof}
Suppose, for the sake of a contradiction, that $M$ is an algorithm
that decides the set~$X$. Let $M_1$ and $M_2$ be algorithms
such that $L^e(M_1) \in P$ and $L^e(M_2) \notin P$; these exist
since $P$ is a non-trivial property of c.e.\ sets.
Let $N$ be the following algorithm:
\begin{tabbing}
\AlgFirstIn\=\Algin\=\Algin\=\Algin\=\Algin\=\kill
\>\underline{Assumption:} $M$ decides $X$.\\
\>\underline{Input:} $w \in \Sigma^*$ \\
\>\underline{Algorithm $N$:} \\
\>If $M(w)$ accepts, \\
\>\>Run $M_2(\epsilon)$, outputting whatever $M_2(\epsilon)$ outputs. \\
\>If $M(w)$ rejects, \\
\>\>Run $M_1(\epsilon)$, outputting whatever $M_1(\epsilon)$ outputs.
\end{tabbing}
Use the Diagonal Theorem to form $D_N$. This means that
$D_N(\epsilon)$ runs by first checking whether $M(\lgn D_N\rgn)$ accepts.
If so, $D_N(\epsilon)$~enumerates (outputs) the strings in~$L^e(M_2)$.  Otherwise,
$M(\lgn D_N\rgn)$ rejects, and $D_N(\epsilon)$~enumerates the strings in~$L^e(M_1)$. Thus,

\begin{implicationsnarrow}
$M(\lgn D_N \rgn)$ accepts
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $L^e(D_N) = L^e(M_2)$ \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $L^e(D_N) \notin P$ \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $M(\lgn D_N \rgn)$ rejects
\end{implicationsnarrow}
The second equivalence holds since $L^e(D_N)$ is either
$L^e(M_1)$ or~$L^e(M_2)$ depending on whether
$M(\lgn D_M \rgn)$ accepts or rejects.

This gives the desired contradiction.
\end{proof}
\index{Rice's Theorem|)}

\medskip

We have finished our proofs of undecidability for now.
So far, our proofs have worked with an informal notion
of algorithms, under the assumptions of uniform representations
and malleability of algorithms. The next chapter will define Turing machines.
Once Turing machines have been formalized, we will have a justification
for the Church-Turing Thesis, and it will be evident that
the corresponding problems for Turing machines
are likewise undecidable. For example, the three formulations
of the halting problem, $\THalt_\TSelf$, $\THalt_1$, and $\THalt_0$,
when recast to work with G{\"o}del numbers of Turing machines
are undecidable. Likewise, the Diagonal Theorem,
Theorem~\ref{thm:AcceptRejectInsep}
on computable inseparability, and Rice's Theorem all still hold
when working with Turing machines.
