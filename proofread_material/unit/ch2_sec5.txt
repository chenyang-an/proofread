\section{Constructing \texorpdfstring{$\PL$}{PL}-Proofs}\label{sec:constructingPL}

The previous two sections have given a large variety of tools
for proving statements such as ``$\Gamma \vdash A$'' or
``$\Gamma$~is inconsistent''.  The ones that tend to be the
most useful in practice include the following (1)-(5) and (6)-(9).

\begin{itemizeLoose}
\item[(1)] To prove $\Gamma \vdash A\limplies B$, it suffices to
prove $\Gamma, A \vdash B$.
\item[(2)] To prove $\Gamma \vdash \lnot A$, it suffices to
prove $\Gamma\cup\{A\}$ is inconsistent.
\item[(3)] To prove $\Gamma \vdash p_i$, it suffices to
prove that $\Gamma \cup \{ \lnot p_i \}$ is inconsistent.
\item[(4)] To prove $\Gamma, \lnot A$ is inconsistent,
it suffices to prove that $\Gamma \vdash A$.
\item[(5)] To prove $\Gamma\cup\{A\limplies B\}$ is inconsistent,
it suffices to prove first that $\Gamma \vdash A$
and second that $\Gamma \cup \{B\}$ is inconsistent.
\end{itemizeLoose}
Item~(5) can be justified by Theorem~\ref{thm:casesAimpliesB}(b).

It can be shown that repeatedly applying the reductions
of items (1)-(5) is sufficient to generate a $\PL$-proof of any
$\{\lnot, \limplies \}$-tautology.
For this, see Exercise~\ref{exer:altProfCompletePL}.
Some additional
principles that are also very useful in practice include:

\begin{itemizeLoose}
\item[(6)] To prove $\Gamma \vdash A$, it suffices to
prove that $\Gamma \cup \{ \lnot A \}$ is inconsistent. (This is
a more general form of item~(3), the first version of proof by contradiction.)
\item[(7)] To prove $\Gamma \vdash B$, it suffices to prove both
that $\Gamma, A \vdash B$ and that $\Gamma, \lnot A \vdash\penalty10000 B$.
(This is \idxnm{proof-by-cases}.)
\item[(8)] Suppose $\Gamma$ contains the two formulas $A$ and $A\limplies B$.
Let $\Gamma^*$ be $\Gamma \cup\{B\}$.
To prove that $\Gamma$ is inconsistent,
it suffices to prove that $\Gamma^*$~is inconsistent.
To prove that $\Gamma\vdash C$,
it suffices to prove that $\Gamma^*\vdash C$.
(This is justified by Modus Ponens.)
\item[(9)] Suppose $\Gamma$ contains the two formulas $\lnot B$ and $A\limplies B$.
Let $\Gamma^*$ be $\Gamma \cup\{\lnot A\}$.
To prove that $\Gamma$ is inconsistent,
it suffices to prove that $\Gamma^*$~is inconsistent.
To prove that $\Gamma\vdash C$,
it suffices to prove that $\Gamma^*\vdash C$.
(This is justified by \idxnm{Modus Tollens}.)
\item[(10)] Suppose $\Gamma$ contains the two formulas $A\limplies B$ and $B\limplies C$.
Let $\Gamma^*$ be $\Gamma \cup\{A\limplies C\}$.
To prove that $\Gamma$ is inconsistent,
it suffices to prove that $\Gamma^*$~is inconsistent.
To prove that $\Gamma\vdash D$,
it suffices to prove that $\Gamma^*\vdash D$.
(This is justified by \idxnm{Hypothetical Syllogism}.)
\end{itemizeLoose}

Of course, the previous sections gave several other possible principles that can help with proving
the existence of $\PL$-proofs. For example, a formula $\lnot\lnot A$ may be replaced
with the formula~$A$.
Nonetheless, items (1)-(10) are often (but not always) good strategies to follow.
There will generally be multiple possibilities for which of (1)-(10) to apply;
it can require some cleverness (or luck) to
decide whether and how to best apply items (1)-(10), or other strategies.

Item~(7) can be much harder to use than the other eight items
since it requires choosing the formula~$A$. For this,
it helps to have some intuition on what formula~$A$ could help with showing the existence
of a $\PL$-proof.

Items (8) and~(9) are still valid if $\Gamma^*$ is instead defined
to omit the formula $A\limplies B$.

A chart of the main definitions and theorems that can be used for
proving the existence of $\PL$-proof is shown on page~\pageref{page:constructingPL}.

\AxiomC{$A$}

{\bf Abbreviations:}
$A\lor B$, $A\land B$ and $A\liff B$ abbreviate, respectively,
\newline \hspace*{1in}$\lnot A\limplies B$, $\lnot (A\limplies \lnot B)$ and $(A\limplies B)\land (B\limplies A)$.

\vspace*{1.0em}

{\bf \idxnm{Deduction Theorem}:} $\Gamma \vdash A\limplies B$ iff $\Gamma, A \vdash B$.
\vspace*{0.5em}

{\bf Proof by Contradiction:} \hfill \\[0.5em]
\hspace*{5em} $\Gamma \vdash A$ iff $\Gamma \cup \{ \lnot A \}$ is inconsistent. \hfill \\[0.25em]
\hspace*{5em} $\Gamma \vdash \lnot A$ iff $\Gamma \cup \{ A \}$ is inconsistent.

\vspace*{0.5em}

{\bf Proof by Cases:} If $\Gamma, A \vdash B$ and $\Gamma,\lnot A \vdash B$, then $\Gamma \vdash B$.

\begin{example} We show that
\[
A\limplies B \lor C,\, B\limplies D,\, C\limplies D \vdash A\limplies D.
\]
Since $B\lor C$ is an abbreviation for $\lnot B \limplies C$, this is identical to
\[
A\limplies \lnot B \limplies C,\, B\limplies D,\, C\limplies D \vdash A\limplies D.
\]
By item (1), or the Deduction Theorem, it suffices to show
\[
A,\, A\limplies \lnot B \limplies C,\, B\limplies D,\, C\limplies D\vdash D.
\]
Using item (6), or proof by contradiction, it suffices to show that
\[
\hbox{$\{ A,\, A\limplies \lnot B \limplies C,\, B\limplies D,\, C\limplies D ,\, \lnot D \}$ is inconsistent.}
\]
Using item (9) twice, namely using Modus Tollens on the last three formulas, 
it suffices to show that
\[
\hbox{$\{ A,\, A\limplies \lnot B \limplies C,\, \lnot B,\, \lnot C ,\, \lnot D \}$ is inconsistent.}
\]
Using item (8) twice, namely using Modus Ponens, it suffices to show that
\[
\hbox{$\{ A,\,C,\, \lnot B,\, \lnot C ,\, \lnot D \}$ is inconsistent.}
\]
This is inconsistent as it contains both $C$ and $\lnot C$ as members.
\end{example}

\begin{example} Now we show that $A \land B \vdash B \land A$.  By convention, $A\land B$ and $B\land A$
are abbreviations for $\lnot(A\limplies \lnot B)$ and $\lnot(B\limplies \lnot A)$. So we wish 
to show 
\[
\lnot(A\limplies \lnot B)\, \vdash\,\lnot(B\limplies \lnot A) .
\]
Writing ``STS'' for ``it suffices to show'', we can argue step-by-step as follows:
\begin{itemize} 
\item By proof by contradiction, STS:  $\{\lnot(A\limplies \lnot B),\, B\limplies \lnot A\}$ is inconsistent.
\item By proof by contradiction, STS:  $B\limplies \lnot A \, \vdash A\limplies \lnot B $.
\item By the Deduction Theorem, STS: $A,\, B\limplies \lnot A \, \vdash \lnot B $.
\item By proof by contradiction, STS: $B, \, A,\, B\limplies \lnot A \}$ is inconsistent.
\end{itemize}
The last set is inconsistent, since it contains~$A$, and since it has proof of~$\lnot A$
by Modus Ponens.
\end{example}
