\section{Introduction to Propositional Logic}\label{sec:introProp}

Propositional logic is used to express statements
using ``propositions'' or ``sentences''
that take on true/false values. Propositional logic
is sometimes called ``sentential logic'' or ``Boolean logic''; the latter
name is because it deals with the
Boolean values of ``True'' and ``False''.
As a simple example, consider three propositions
denoted by variables $r, s, w$:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{c@{~-~}l}
$r$ & ``It is raining'' \\[0.5ex]
$s$ & ``The sun is shining'' \\[0.5ex]
$w$ & ``The grass is wet''
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
As propositions, they are assumed to take on definite true/false
values. For example, it is assumed that $r$ will assume a definite truth value
of ``True'' or ``False'' indicating either that it is raining or that it
is not raining;
say at some particular time or place and
without any ambiguity that might arise from issues such as whether
a light mist counts as rain. Likewise, it is assumed that $s$ and~$w$
will assume definite truth values as to whether the sun is shining
and the grass is wet.

More complex propositions are formed by combining
propositional variables with operators such as
``not''~($\lnot$)\index{not@{not ($\lnot$)}},
``and''~($\land$)\index{and@{and ($\land$)}}, and
``or''~($\lor$)\index{or@{or ($\lor$)}}.
For example,
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{c@{~-~}l}
$\lnot r$ & ``It is not raining'' \\[0.5ex]
$r \land w$ & ``It is raining and the grass is wet'' \\[0.5ex]
$r \lor s$ & ``It is raining or the sun is shining'' \\[0.5ex]
$\lnot( r \land s)$ & ``It is not the case that both it is raining and the sun is shining''
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
When the variables $r$, $w$, and~$s$ have true/false values, they induce
true/false values for the complex propositions.
The $\lnot$~(``not'') operator is also called \emph{logical negation} or just \idxem{negation}.
The $\land$~(``and'') operator is also called \idxem{conjunction}.
The $\lor$~(``or'') operator is also called \idxem{disjunction}.
The connective~$\lor$ denotes the ``\idxnm{inclusive or}'' in that it allows the possibility
that both arguments are true. Thus the formula $r \lor s$ is also true when both $r$ and~$s$
are true.

Other common connectives include
``if \ldots then \ldots''\index{if-then@{if-then ($\limplies$)}} ($\limplies$) and
``if and only if''~($\liff$)\index{if and only if@{if and only if ($\liff$)}}.
The $\limplies$ operator is
often called \idxem{implication}; and the $\liff$ operator is the
\idxem{equivalence} operator.
For example,
\nopagebreak
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{c@{~~~-~}l}
$r \limplies w$ & ``If it is raining then the grass is wet'' \\[0.5ex]
$w \limplies r$ & ``The grass is wet only if it is raining'' \\[0.5ex]
$r \limplies w$ & ``The grass is wet if it is raining'' \\[0.5ex]
$w \liff r$ & ``The grass is wet if and only if it is raining''
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
Some care needs to be taken in translating English sentences to propositional
formulas since a \idxnm{natural language} such as English allows many nuances that
are completely lost in propositional logic.  However,
as shown in some of the above examples, ``$B$~if~$A$''
generally means the same thing
as ``if $A$ then~$B$''. It also means the same thing as ``$A$~only if~$B$''.

Some less explicit uses of propositional connectives occur with ``but''
and ``unless''; for example,
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{c@{~~~-~}l}
$r \land \lnot w$ & ``It is raining but the grass is not wet'' \\[0.5ex]
$(\lnot w) \lor r$ & ``The grass is not wet unless it is raining.''
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
These illustrate that ``but'' may translate to the propositional connective $\land$~(``and''),
and ``$A$ unless~$B$'' may translate to $A \lor B$. The latter can also translate
to $(\lnot A) \limplies B$ because this means the same as ``if~$\lnot A$ then~$B$'' or
``if $A$ is false, then $B$ is true''.
On a related note, ``If $A$, then~$B$'' means the same thing in
propositional logic as $(\lnot A) \lor B$.

For somewhat more problematic correspondences between English
assertions and propositional logic, consider
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{c@{~-~}l}
$b$ & ``You may buy a ticket'' \\[0.5ex]
$e$ & ``You are over eighteen years old''
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
that are used to form the compound propositions
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{c@{~~~-~}l}
$e \limplies b$ & ``You may buy a ticket if you are over eighteen'' \\[0.5ex]
$b \limplies e$ & ``You may buy a ticket only if you are over eighteen'' \\[0.5ex]
$b \liff e$ & ``You may buy a ticket if and only if you are over eighteen''
\end{tabular}
\label{table:concertEighteen}
\end{center}
If someone were to make these English statements in a real-world situation, they
would probably use any of the three to mean $b \liff e$,
i.e.\ that you may buy a ticket if and only if you are over eighteen.\footnote{This
ignores preconditions such as whether the ticket sales office is
open and you have enough money.}
However, we use ``if'' and ``only if'' in more specialized
senses, as is common in logic and more generally in mathematics. This illustrates the
need for caution in translating from a natural language to propositional
logic.

Mathematical logic avoids the ambiguity of English. Instead, the
propositional connectives $\lnot$, $\land$, $\lor$,
$\limplies$, $\liff$, etc., are purely \idxnm{truth functional},
with values shown in Figure~\ref{fig:propTruthDefn}.
For instance, whether the formula $p \limplies q$ is true or false
is determined solely by the truth or falsity of $p$ and~$q$,
and it does not matter whether there is some reason why the truth of~$p$
should imply the truth of~$q$. Indeed, $p \limplies q$ will always have
the same truth value as $(\lnot p) \lor q$.

\begin{figure}
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{c|c}
$p$ & $\lnot p$ \\
\hline
$\True$ & $\False \taller$ \\
$\False$ & $\True$ \\
\end{tabular}
\hspace*{3em}
\begin{tabular}{cc|cccc}
$p$ & $q$ & $p\lor q$ & $p \land q$ & $p \limplies q$ & $p \liff q$ \\
\hline
$\True$ & $\True$ & $\True$ & $\True$ & $\True$ & $\True \taller$ \\
$\True$ & $\False$ & $\True$ & $\False$ & $\False$ & $\False$ \\
$\False$ & $\True$ & $\True$ & $\False$ & $\True$ & $\False$ \\
$\False$ & $\False$ & $\False$ & $\False$ & $\True$ & $\True$
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
\caption{The values of propositional connectives. ``$\True$'' and ``$\False$''
denote the Boolean values ``True'' and ``False''.}
\label{fig:propTruthDefn}
\end{figure}

This can be confusing sometimes, as it is somewhat counterintuitive that
``if $p$, then~$q$'' should be
true when $p$ is false,
independently of whether $q$ is true or false. This is counterintuitive
for the simple reason that in English,
there is generally a distinction between
``if $A$ then $B$'' and ``$B$ or not $A$''.
For instance, consider the rather dramatic
difference in meanings
of the two sentences
\[
\hbox{``If you will go to your house today, you will find your passport today'',}
\]
and
\[
\hbox{``You
will find your passport today, or you will not go to your house today''.}
\]
The ``if $A$ then $B$'' form of the statement sounds like helpful
advice on how to find your missing passport. On the other hand, the
``$B$ or not $A$'' form of the statement sounds like a threat from
a border guard. These nuances are completely lost when translating
the statements from English into propositional logic, since
$A\limplies B$ means exactly the same thing as $B \lor \lnot A$ in
propositional logic.

In natural languages\index{natural language}, a statement ``if $A$ then~$B$'' 
usually means there is a reason why the truth of~$A$ implies the truth of~$B$.
In propositional logic,
however, statements like ``If pigs have wings then horses can fly'' can be
true (because pigs do not have wings) even though wings on
pigs would presumably have nothing to do with flying horses!

For an
example of why this treatment of implications
makes sense when dealing with mathematical statements,
consider the propositions
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{c@{~-~}l}
$P(x)$ & ``$x$ is a prime greater than~2'' \\[0.5ex]
$O(x)$ & ``$x$ is odd''
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
Of course, we want the statement $\forall x (P(x)\limplies O(x))$,
\[
\hbox{``For all~$x$, if $x$ is a prime greater than~2, then $x$ is odd''}
\]
to be true for $x$ ranging over all integers.
Figure~\ref{fig:primeIfThen}
gives the truth values of $P(x)$ and $O(x)$ and $P(x)\limplies O(x)$ when
$x=1$, $x=2$ and $x=3$.
We want $P(x)\limplies\penalty10000 O(x)$ to be true in all three cases, including
the two cases where $P(x)$ is false.  Thus we want ``$\False\limplies \False$'' and
``$\False\limplies \True$'' both to evaluate as true to give the correct results in
the cases where $x$ is equal to 1 or~2.

\begin{figure}
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{c|ccc}
$x$ & $P(x)$ & $O(x)$ & $P(x)\limplies O(x)$ \\
\hline
$1$ & $\False$ & $\True$ & $\True$ \\
$2$ & $\False$ & $\False$ & $\True$ \\
$3$ & $\True$ & $\True$ & $\True$
\end{tabular}

\end{center}
\caption{Some values of $P(x) \limplies O(x)$.}
\label{fig:primeIfThen}
\end{figure}
