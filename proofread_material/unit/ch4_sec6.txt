\section{Cardinalities and L\"owenheim-Skolem Theorems}\label{sec:LowenheimSkolem}

Theorem~\ref{thm:overspillSatisfiabilty} already established the
overspill property that a theory with arbitrarily large finite
models has an infinite model. This section will prove further
properties about the cardinalities of models of a theory. We discuss
countable languages and models. We do not give full proofs for
the theorems on
uncountable languages and models; these results are not used
in the rest of the book, and readers can skip them if they wish.

We assume the reader has some basic knowledge of cardinalities in
set theory. To simplify working with cardinalities, we assume that
the Axiom of Choice holds. We write $|X|$ for the \idxnm{cardinality}
of a set~$X$. Any set~$X$ can be categorized in exactly
one of the following ways:
\begin{enumerateLoose}
\item[(a)] $X$ is \idxem{finite}. That is $|X| \in \mathbb N$.
\item[(b)] $X$ is \idxem{countably infinite}. That is, $X$~is equinumerous with~$\mathbb N$.
\item[(c)] $X$ is \idxem{uncountable}. That is, $|X| > |\mathbb N|$.
\end{enumerateLoose}
A set is called \idxem{countable} if it is either finite or countably infinite.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:completenessCountable}
Let $\Gamma$ be a consistent set of $L$-sentences where $L$ is countable.
Then $\Gamma$ has a countable model.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
This is a direct consequence of the proof of the Completeness Theorem.
That proof added countably many constant symbols~$d_i$ to form
a countably infinite language~$L^\plus$ (when constructing a strongly
Henkin extension of~$\Gamma$). It then formed an $L^\plus$-structure~$\mathfrakA$
with domain consisting of the equivalence classes~$[s]$ where $s$ is
a closed $L^\plus$-term.  Since $L^\plus$ is countable, there are countably many
closed~$L^\plus$-terms. Thus there are countably many equivalence classes~$[s]$.
Therefore, $|\mathfrakA|$ is countable, so $\mathfrakA$ is
countable.
\end{proof}

This last theorem has some simple, but surprising, corollaries. The first states
that there is a countable structure that is elementarily equivalent to
the real numbers $\mathcal R = (R,0, 1, + ,\cdot)$.

\begin{corollary}
There is a countable model of $\TTh {\mathcal R}$.
\end{corollary}

This means that
first-order logic is unable to fully characterize the real numbers since it cannot
exclude the existence of a countable model.
The same holds for $\ZF$, the first-order Zermelo-Fraenkel \idxnm{set theory}.
This is often called the ``\idxnm{Skolem paradox}'' (because it was first proved
by Skolem in 1922):

\begin{corollary}
If $\ZF$ is consistent, there is a countable model of $\ZF$.
\end{corollary}

The Skolem paradox is not an actual paradox, but it superficially seems paradoxical
since the theory~$\ZF$ can prove the existence of uncountable sets, but still has
a countable model. The resolution of the ``paradox'' is that in any
countable model~$\mathfrakA$ of~$\ZF$, there will be an object~$a$ that satisfies
(relative to truth in~$\mathfrakA$) the first-order property of being an infinite set.
Specifically, an object~$a$ in~$|\mathfrakA|$ is uncountable in the sense of~$\mathfrakA$ if there is
no bijection in~$\mathfrakA$ between $a$ and the interpretation of the natural numbers
in~$\mathfrakA$.

By the proofs of Theorems \ref{thm:nonstdIntegers} and~\ref{thm:completenessCountable},
we also get:

\begin{corollary}\label{coro:nonstdIntegersCtbl}
There is a countable \idxnm{nonstandard model} of the integers.
\end{corollary}

We next define the notion of countable categoricity. For this,
two models are said to be isomorphic if they are identical up to
renaming the objects in the universe. Formally, this is defined by:
\begin{definition}\label{def:isomorphic}
Two $L$-structures $\mathfrakA$ and $\mathfrakB$ are \idxem{isomorphic},
written $\mathfrakA \cong \mathfrakB$,
if there is a \idxnm{bijection} $\pi : |\mathfrakA| \rightarrow |\mathfrakB|$ such that
\begin{enumerate}
\item For every constant symbol $c\in L$, $\pi(c^\mathfrakA) = c^\mathfrakB$.
\item For every $k$-ary function symbol $f\in L$ and
every $a_1,\ldots,a_k \in |\mathfrakA|$,
\[
\pi ( f^\mathfrakA(a_1,\ldots, a_k) ) ~=~ f^\mathfrakB ( \pi(a_1), \ldots, \pi(a_k) ).
\]
\item For every $k$-ary predicate symbol $P \in L$, and every
$a_1,\ldots,a_k \in |\mathfrakA|$,
\[
\langle a_1, \ldots, a_k \rangle \in P^\mathfrakA
\quad\hbox{if and only if}\quad
\langle \pi(a_1), \ldots, \pi(a_k) \rangle \in P^\mathfrakB .
\]
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

Recall that the notation $\mathfrakA \equiv \mathfrakB$ indicates
that $\mathfrakA$ and~$\mathfrakB$ are
\idxnm{elementarily equivalent}.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:isomorphElemEquiv}
If $\mathfrakA \cong \mathfrakB$, then $\mathfrakA \equiv \mathfrakB$.
\end{theorem}

The idea behind Theorem~\ref{thm:isomorphElemEquiv} is clear:
if $\mathfrakA$ and~$\mathfrakB$ are identical except for the identities
of objects in their universes, then they satisfy the same first-order
properties. We leave the proof to Exercise~\ref{exer:isomorphElemEquivThm}.

For the next definition, $\aleph_0$\index{aleph naugh@{$\alpha_0$}}~is used to denote the
cardinality of~$\mathbb N$. (``$\aleph$''~is the Hebrew letter ``aleph''.)

\begin{definition}
A set of sentences $\Gamma$ is $\aleph_0$-\idxem{categorical} if
$\Gamma$ has exactly one countably infinite model up to isomorphism.
\end{definition}

The terminology ``$\omega$-categorical'' is often used instead
of ``$\aleph_0$-categorical''.

\begin{theorem}[Countable \L o\'s-Vaught Test]
\label{thm:LosVaughtTest}\index{Los-Vaught Test@{\L{}o\'s-Vaught Test}}
Suppose $L$ is countable. Also suppose that $T$~is an $\aleph_0$-categorial $L$-theory
and that $T$~has no finite models.
Then $T$~is complete.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Suppose $T$ is not complete, so there is a sentence~$A$ such that
neither $A$ nor~$\lnot A$ is a consequence of~$T$. Therefore
$T\cup\{A\}$ and $T\cup\{\lnot A \}$ are both consistent. By
Theorem~\ref{thm:completenessCountable}, there are countable
models $\mathfrakA$ and~$\mathfrakB$ of $T\cup\{A\}$ and $T\cup\{\lnot A \}$.
Since $T$ has no finite models, $\mathfrakA$ and~$\mathfrakB$ are
countably infinite.
Since $T$ is $\aleph_0$-categorical, they are isomorphic. But in light
of Theorem~\ref{thm:isomorphElemEquiv}, this is a
contradiction since they disagree on the truth of~$A$.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}
The theory of dense linear order (DLO) without
endpoints\index{dense linear order!without endpoints}
is an example of an $\aleph_0$-categorial theory. This was originally
proved by Cantor in the 1890's, but we shall omit the proof.
It follows that the theory of DLO without endpoints is complete.

On the
other hand, the theory DLO is not complete since the axiom about the
existence of a left (say) endpoint is neither provable nor refutable
from the DLO axioms. It is also not $\aleph_0$-categorical. For instance,
let $\mathbb Q^{\ge 0}$ be the set of nonnegative rationals and
$\mathcal Q^{\ge 0}$ be the structure $(\mathbb Q^{\ge 0}, <)$.
Then $\mathcal Q$ and~$\mathcal Q^{\ge 0}$ are countable models
of DLO but are not isomorphic.
\end{example}

\begin{example}
The theory $\TTh {\mathcal N}$ of the integers is complete but
not $\aleph_0$-categorical by Corollary~\ref{coro:nonstdIntegersCtbl}.
\end{example}

The Countable \L{}os-Vaught Test can also be stated for a set~$\Gamma$ of
sentences.  Let $\TCn \Gamma$\index{consequences of ($\TCnBase$)}
denote the set of sentences which
are logical consequences of~$\Gamma$, namely
$\TCn \Gamma = \{ A : \hbox{$A$ is a sentence and $\Gamma \vDash A$} \}$.
Note $\TCn \Gamma$ must be a theory. Theorem~\ref{thm:LosVaughtTest}
implies that if $\Gamma$ is $\aleph_0$-categorical and has
no finite models, then $\TCn \Gamma$ is complete.

We now discuss uncountable languages.

\begin{definition}
Let $L$ be a language. The \idxem{cardinality} of~$L$ is denoted $\Tcard(L)$
and is equal to the maximum of~$|L|$ and $\aleph_0$.
\end{definition}

Equivalently, $\Tcard(L) = \aleph_0$ if $L$~is countable, and $\Tcard(L) = |L|$
if $L$~is infinite. The point of the definition of $\Tcard(L)$ is that
there are a total of $\Tcard(L)$ many formulas.

The Soundness, Completeness,
and Compactness Theorems all hold for uncountable languages as well as
countable languages. The proof of
the Completeness Theorem needs modification to apply to uncountable
languages: the primary modification is in the proof of
Theorem~\ref{thm:stronglyHenkinDelta}
constructing a strongly Henkin extension of~$\Gamma$.
See Exercise~\ref{exer:completeUncountable} for an outline of the proof.

The proof as sketched in Exercise~\ref{exer:completeUncountable} proves a strengthened
form of the Completeness Theorem for languages of arbitrary cardinality:

\begin{theorem}[Completeness Theorem]\label{thm:completenessUnountable}%
\label{thm:completeUncountable}\index{Completeness Theorem}
Let $\Gamma$ be a consistent set of $L$-sentences. Then
$\Gamma$ has a model of cardinality at most $\Tcard(L)$.
\end{theorem}

As a consequence, we get the following result, which is a
variant of the L\"owenheim-Skolem\index{Lowenheim Skolem Theorem@{L\"owenheim-Skolem Theorem}} theorem.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:LowenheimSkolemUpward}
Let $\Gamma$ be a set of $L$-sentences. Suppose that $\Gamma$ has an infinite
model or that $\Gamma$~has arbitrarily large finite models.
Then for all cardinals $\lambda \ge \Tcard(L)$,
$\Gamma$~has a model of cardinality~$\lambda$.
\end{theorem}

By Theoren~\ref{thm:overspillSatisfiabilty}, if $\Gamma$ has arbitrarily large models,
then $\Gamma$~has an infinite model. So the last part of the hypothesis is
redundant.

\begin{proof}
Let $\kappa = \Tcard(L)$ and $\lambda \ge \kappa$.
Let $\{ d_\alpha : \alpha<\lambda \}$ be a set of $\lambda$~many new
constant symbols.\footnote{The definition of cardinals in set theory
ensures that for any cardinal~$\lambda$, there are
there are $\lambda$ many cardinals $\alpha<\lambda$.} Let $L^\prime$ be $L \cup \{ d_\alpha : \alpha<\lambda\}$.
Clearly $\Tcard(L^\prime) = \lambda$.

Let $\Pi$ be $\Gamma \cup \{ d_\alpha \neq d_\beta : \alpha<\beta<\lambda \}$.
We claim that $\Pi$ is finitely satisfiable. Any
finite subset~$\Delta$ of~$\Pi$ can
mention only $k$~many $d_\alpha$'s for some finite~$k$. Since $\Gamma$~has a
model~$\mathfrakA$ of cardinality $\ge k$, this means $\Delta$~can be satisfied
by expanding~$\mathfrakA$ to have distinct interpretations for the~$d_\alpha$'s
mentioned in~$\Delta$. Since every finite subset~$\Delta$ of~$\Pi$
is satisfiable, $\Pi$~is consistent. Therefore, by the
Completeness Theorem~\ref{thm:LowenheimSkolemUpward}, $\Pi$~is satisfied
by some structure~$\mathfrakA$ of cardinality at most~$\lambda$.
Since $\Pi$ contains the sentences $d_\alpha \neq d_\beta$, any model
of~$\Pi$ must have cardinality at least~$\lambda$.
Since $\Pi\supset \Gamma$, it follows that
$\mathfrakA$~is the desired model of~$\Gamma$ of
cardinality~$\lambda$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{coro:nonstdIntegersUnctbl}
There is an uncountable \idxnm{nonstandard model} of the integers.
\end{corollary}

\begin{definition}
Let $\kappa$ be a cardinality.
A set of sentences $\Gamma$ is $\kappa$-\idxem{categorical} if
$\Gamma$ has exactly one model of cardinality~$\kappa$ up to isomorphism.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[\L o\'s-Vaught Test]
\label{thm:LosVaughtTestUncount}\index{Los-Vaught Test@{\L{}o\'s-Vaught Test}}
Suppose that $\lambda \ge \Tcard(L)$. Also suppose that $T$~is an $\lambda$-categorial $L$-theory
and that $T$~has no finite models.
Then $T$~is complete.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The proof of this is essentially identical to the proof
of the Countable \L o\'s-Vaught Test in Theorem~\ref{thm:LosVaughtTest}.
Suppose $T$ is not complete, so there is a sentence~$A$ such that
neither $A$ nor~$\lnot A$ is a consequence of~$T$. Therefore
$T\cup\{A\}$ and $T\cup\{\lnot A \}$ are both consistent. By
Theorem~\ref{thm:completenessUnountable}, there are
models $\mathfrakA$ and~$\mathfrakB$ of $T\cup\{A\}$ and $T\cup\{\lnot A \}$.
Since $T$ has no finite models, $\mathfrakA$ and~$\mathfrakB$ are
both infinite.  Thus, by Theorem~\ref{thm:LosVaughtTestUncount},
there are models $\mathfrakA^\prime$ and~$\mathfrakB^\prime$
of $T\cup\{A\}$ and $T\cup \{\lnot A \}$ of cardinality~$\lambda$.
Since $T$ is $\lambda$-categorical,
$\mathfrakA^\prime$ and~$\mathfrakB^\prime$ are isomorphic. But in light
of Theorem~\ref{thm:isomorphElemEquiv}, this is a
contradiction since they disagree on the truth of~$A$.
\end{proof}
