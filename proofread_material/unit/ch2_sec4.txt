\section{Consistency, Inconsistency, and Proof by Contradiction}\label{sec:conInconContra}
\def\cursectionname{Consistency, Inconsistency, and Proof by Contradiction}

\paragraph*{Consistency and inconsistency.}
A set $\Gamma$ is said to be ``\idxnm{inconsistent}'' if it is possible to derive
a contradiction from~$\Gamma$. Otherwise, it is ``\idxnm{consistent}''.
Formally, these are defined as follows:

\begin{definition}\label{def:consistent}
A set~$\Gamma$ of formulas is \idxem{inconsistent} if,
for some formula~$A$, both $A$ and~$\lnot A$ are theorems
of~$\Gamma$.
In other words, if both $\Gamma \vdash A$ and $\Gamma \vdash \lnot A$.
Otherwise, $\Gamma$~is \idxem{consistent}.
\end{definition}

\begin{example}
As a simple example, the set $\Gamma = \{\lnot p_1\limplies p_1,\, \lnot\lnot p_1\limplies \lnot p_1 \}$ is
inconsistent. To see this, first note that the PL4 axiom
$(\lnot p_1\limplies p_1) \limplies p_1$ and Modus Ponens can be used to
show that $\Gamma\vdash p_1$. Similarly, the PL4 axiom $(\lnot \lnot p_1\limplies \lnot p_1) \limplies \lnot p_1$
means that $\Gamma \vdash \lnot p_1$. Thus $\Gamma$~is inconsistent.
\end{example}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:consisSubset}
If $\Gamma$ is consistent and $\Pi\subseteq \Gamma$,
then $\Pi$~is consistent.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
If $\Pi$ is inconsistent, there are $\PL$-proofs of $A$ and $\lnot A$
from the hypotheses~$\Pi$.
These are also $\PL$-proofs from the hypotheses~$\Gamma$.
\end{proof}

It is a remarkable, but simple, fact that if $\Gamma$~is inconsistent,
then it can be used to prove {\em any} formula:

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:consistency}
$\Gamma$ is inconsistent if and only if $\Gamma \vdash B$ for every formula~$B$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Certainly, if $\Gamma\vdash B$ for all formulas~$B$, then $\Gamma \vdash p_1$
and $\Gamma \vdash \lnot p_1$ and hence $\Gamma$~is inconsistent.

Conversely, suppose $\Gamma$~is inconsistent and thus
$\Gamma \vdash A$ and $\Gamma \vdash \lnot A$ for some
formula~$A$.  Let $B$ be any formula. Now, $\lnot A \limplies( A \limplies B)$
is an instance of Axiom~PL3. From this, with two uses of Modus Ponens, we conclude
that $\Gamma \vdash B$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{coro:consistency}
Let $A$ and~$B$ be formulas.  Then $A, \lnot A \vdash B$.
\end{corollary}

Note that the corollary is the analogue of Theorem~\ref{thm:AandNotA}(a)
for provability in place of tautological implication.
It is an immediate consequence of Theorem~\ref{thm:consistency}.
In fact, the last two sentences of the proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:consistency}
were just a proof of the corollary.
\hfill $\qed$

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:finiteConsistEasy}
If $\Gamma$ is inconsistent, then there is a finite
subset~$\Gamma_0$ of~$\Gamma$ which is inconsistent.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Suppose $\Gamma\vdash A$ and $\Gamma\vdash \lnot A$. By
Theorem~\ref{thm:subsetHypsProp}(b) there are finite subsets $\Gamma_1$ and~$\Gamma_2$
of~$\Gamma$ such that $\Gamma_1 \vdash A$ and $\Gamma_2 \vdash \lnot A$. Let
$\Gamma_0 = \Gamma_1 \cup \Gamma_2$. Then $\Gamma_0\subseteq \Gamma$ and
both $A$ and $\lnot A$ are theorems of~$\Gamma_0$. That is, $\Gamma_0$ is
a finite, inconsistent subset of~$\Gamma$.
\end{proof}

The converse
to Theorem~\ref{thm:finiteConsistEasy} also holds,
and its proof is immediate from Theorem~\ref{thm:consisSubset}.
That is, $\Gamma$~is inconsistent if and only if some finite subset
of~$\Gamma$ is inconsistent.
For more on this, see Section~\ref{sec:compactPL} on the Compactness
Theorem.

\paragraph*{Proof by contradiction.} Proof by contradiction\index{proof by contradiction}
is a powerful technique for finding and constructing proofs.
The idea is that to prove a formula~$A$, it is sufficient to
assume that $A$ is false and obtain a contradiction.

\begin{theorem}[Proof by Contradiction, First Version]
\label{thm:proofbyContra1}\index{proof by contradiction}
\[
\Gamma \vdash A \qquad \hbox{if and only if} \qquad
   \hbox{$\Gamma\cup\{\lnot A\}$ is inconsistent.}
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
First, suppose $\Gamma \vdash A$. Then clearly, from the hypotheses
$\Gamma\cup\{\lnot A\}$ there are $\PL$-proofs of both $A$ and~$\lnot A$.
Therefore, $\Gamma\cup\{\lnot A\}$ is inconsistent.

Now suppose $\Gamma \cup \{\lnot A\}$ is inconsistent.  By Theorem~\ref{thm:consistency},
$\Gamma\cup\{\lnot A\}$ has a $\PL$-proof of every formula; in particular,
it has a $\PL$-proof of~$A$. Now,
\begin{implications}
\multicolumn{3}{l}{$\Gamma,\lnot A \vdash A$} \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $\Gamma \vdash \lnot A \limplies A$ & Deduction Theorem \\
& $\Rightarrow$ & $\Gamma \vdash A$ & By PL4 axiom $(\lnot A\limplies A)\limplies A$ and Modus Ponens
\end{implications}
Hence $\Gamma \vdash A$.
\end{proof}

\pagebreak[2]

\begin{corollary}\label{coro:notnotAimpliesA}
$\vdash \lnot\lnot A \limplies A$.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
By the Deduction Theorem and Theorem~\ref{thm:proofbyContra1},
$\vdash \lnot\lnot A \limplies A$ holds if and only
if $\{\lnot \lnot A, \lnot A\}$ is inconsistent. But this is obvious
since both $\lnot A$ and $\lnot \lnot A$ have $\PL$-proofs from the
hypotheses $\{\lnot \lnot A, \lnot A\}$.
\end{proof}

The second version of the Proof by Contradiction theorem
interchanges the roles of $A$ and~$\lnot A$.

\begin{theorem}[Proof by Contradiction, Second Version]
\label{thm:proofbyContra2}\index{proof by contradiction}
\[
\Gamma \vdash \lnot A \qquad \hbox{if and only if} \qquad
   \hbox{$\Gamma\cup\{A\}$ is inconsistent.}
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
First, suppose $\Gamma \vdash \lnot A$. Then $\Gamma \cup \{A\}$ has
both $A$ and $\lnot A$ as theorems and thus is inconsistent.

Second, suppose $\Gamma\cup \{ A \}$ is inconsistent. We have
\begin{implications}
\multicolumn{3}{l}{$\Gamma\cup \{ A \}$ is inconsistent} \\
& $\Rightarrow$ & $\Gamma, A \vdash \lnot A$ & Theorem \ref{thm:consistency} \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $\Gamma \vdash A \limplies \lnot A$ & Deduction Theorem \\
& $\Rightarrow$ & $\Gamma \vdash \lnot\lnot A \limplies \lnot A$
      & By $\vdash \lnot \lnot A \limplies A$ (Corollary \ref{coro:notnotAimpliesA}) and Hypothetical Syllogism \\
& $\Rightarrow$ & $\Gamma \vdash \lnot A$
      & By PL4 axiom $(\lnot \lnot A \limplies \lnot A) \limplies \lnot A$ and Modus Ponens
\end{implications}
Thus we have $\Gamma \vdash \lnot A$ and the theorem is proved.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{coro:AimpliesnotnotA}
$\vdash A \limplies \lnot\lnot A$.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
By the Deduction Theorem, it suffices to prove that $A \vdash \lnot \lnot A$.
By the previous theorem, $A \vdash \lnot \lnot A$ holds if and only
if $\{A, \lnot A\}$ is inconsistent. But this is obvious
since both $A$ and $\lnot A$ have $\PL$-proofs from the
hypotheses $\{A, \lnot A\}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}
We show that $\{A \land \lnot A\}$ is inconsistent.
Recalling the convention that $\land$ is used
to abbreviate a $\{\lnot, \limplies \}$-formula,
this is identical to showing that
$\{\lnot(A\limplies \lnot\lnot A)\}$ is inconsistent.
By the first version of Proof by Contradiction (Theorem~\ref{thm:proofbyContra1}),
this is equivalent to
$\vdash (A\limplies \lnot\lnot A)$.  And this is true by
Corollary~\ref{coro:AimpliesnotnotA}.
\end{example}

\paragraph*{Modus Tollens.}
The \idxnm{Modus Tollens} inference rule has the form
\begin{prooftree}
\AxiomC{$A \limplies B$}
\AxiomC{$\lnot B$}
\BinaryInfC{$\lnot A$}
\end{prooftree}
This turns out to be a valid \idxnm{derived rule} for~$\PL$.
Its validity is justified by the following.
\begin{theorem}\label{thm:modustollens}\index{Modus Tollens}
For any formulas $A$ and~$B$, $\vdash (A\limplies B)\limplies (\lnot B \limplies \lnot A)$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
We have
\begin{implications}
\multicolumn{3}{l}{$\vdash (A\limplies B)\limplies (\lnot B \limplies \lnot A)$} \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $A\limplies B,\, \lnot B \vdash \lnot A$ & Deduction Theorem (used twice) \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $\{A\limplies B, \lnot B, A\}$ is inconsistent & Proof by Contradiction (2nd version) \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $A\limplies B,\, A \vdash B$ & Proof by Contradiction (1st version)
\end{implications}
The final assertion holds by Modus Ponens.
\end{proof}
\begin{corollary}\label{coro:modustollens}\index{Modus Tollens}
If $\Gamma \vdash A\limplies B$ and $\Gamma \vdash \lnot B$, then $\Gamma \vdash \lnot A$.
\end{corollary}

\paragraph*{Proof by Cases.}
The technique of ``\idxnm{proof-by-cases}'' allows proving a formula~$B$ by splitting
into two cases depending on whether another formula~$A$ is true or false.
That is to say, $B$~can be proved by first proving $B$ assuming that $A$ is true
and then proving~$B$ assuming that $A$ is false.
Specifically, it means proving both $A\limplies B$ and $\lnot A \limplies B$.
Since at least one of $A$ and $\lnot A$ must be true (for any particular
truth assignment), this suffices to prove~$B$.
\begin{theorem}\label{thm:byCases}
Suppose $\Gamma \vdash A \limplies B$ and $\Gamma \vdash \lnot A \limplies B$.
Then $\Gamma \vdash B$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
To prove $\Gamma \vdash B$, it suffices to prove that $\Gamma \cup \{\lnot B\}$ is
inconsistent. Since $\Gamma \vdash A\limplies B$, we have $\Gamma, \lnot B \vdash \lnot A$
by Modus Tollens. Similarly, since $\Gamma \vdash \lnot A \limplies B$, we have
$\Gamma,\lnot B \vdash \lnot \lnot A$.  Since both $\lnot A$ and $\lnot \lnot A$
are theorems of $\Gamma \cup \{\lnot B\}$, the set $\Gamma \cup \{\lnot B\}$ is inconsistent.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{coro:byCases}
Suppose $\Gamma, A \vdash B$ and $\Gamma, \lnot A \vdash B$. Then $\Gamma \vdash B$.
\end{corollary}

An important special case of the \idxnm{proof-by-cases} principle
is Corollary~\ref{coro:extendConsis} which states that a consistent~$\Gamma$
can be extended to at least one of 
$\Gamma\cup\{A\}$ or $\Gamma \cup \{\lnot A\}$ and
remain consistent. This will be a corollary of the next theorem.
\begin{theorem}\label{thm:extendConsis}
$\Gamma$ is inconsistent if and only if both $\Gamma\cup\{A\}$ and $\Gamma\cup\{\lnot A\}$
are inconsistent.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
By Theorem~\ref{thm:consisSubset},
if $\Gamma$ is inconsistent, then so are both
$\Gamma\cup\{A\}$ and $\Gamma\cup\{\lnot A\}$.

So suppose that both of $\Gamma\cup\{A\}$ and $\Gamma\cup\{\lnot A\}$ are inconsistent.
Let $B$ be an arbitrary formula. We want to show $\Gamma \vdash B$, as that will
establish that {\em all} formulas are theorems of~$\Gamma$. We have that both
$\Gamma\cup\{A\}\vdash B$ and $\Gamma\cup\penalty10000 \{\lnot A\}\vdash\penalty10000 B$ since those two
theories are presumed to be inconsistent.  Thus, by
proof-by-cases (Corollary~\ref{coro:byCases}), $\Gamma \vdash B$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{coro:extendConsis}
Suppose $\Gamma$ is consistent. Then at least one of
$\Gamma \cup\{A\}$ and $\Gamma \cup \{\lnot A\}$ is consistent.
\end{corollary}

For another example of how to use proof-by-cases, we prove the following.
\begin{theorem}\label{thm:casesAimpliesB}
~~
\begin{itemize}
\item[\rm (a)] $\Gamma, (A\limplies B) \vdash C$ if and only if
both $\Gamma, \lnot A \vdash C$ and $\Gamma, B \vdash C$.
\item[\rm (b)] $\Gamma, (A\limplies B)$ is inconsistent
if and only if both $\Gamma\cup\{\lnot A\}$ and $\Gamma\cup\{B\}$ are inconsistent.
\end{itemize}
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Since a set is inconsistent if and only if it has all formulas~$C$ as theorems,
part~(a) implies part~(b). So we prove~(a).  First suppose $\Gamma,A\limplies B \vdash C$.
Recall that $\lnot A \limplies (A\limplies B)$ is an axiom~PL3.  Thus $\Gamma, \lnot A \vdash A\limplies B$.
It follows readily that $\Gamma, \lnot A \vdash C$.  Also recall that $B \limplies (A \limplies B)$
is an axiom~PL1. By similar reasoning, it follows readily again that $\Gamma,B \vdash C$.

Now suppose that both $\Gamma, \lnot A \vdash C$ and $\Gamma, B \vdash C$.
To prove~(a), we use proof-by-cases, using the formula~$A$ for the cases. Namely,
it suffices to prove that
\begin{itemizeLoose}
\item[(i)] $\Gamma, (A\limplies B), A \vdash C$, and
\item[(ii)] $\Gamma, (A\limplies B), \lnot A \vdash C$
\end{itemizeLoose}
Item~(ii) is obvious, since by hypothesis even $\Gamma, \lnot A \vdash C$. To establish~(i),
use Modus Ponens to derive $B$ from $A\limplies B$ and $A$. Then use
the assumption that $\Gamma, B \vdash C$.
\end{proof}
Proof-by-cases\index{proof-by-cases} can be a powerful technique for proving the existence of $\PL$-proofs, sometimes
greatly simplifying the proofs. However, it can be tricky to choose the formula to use
for defining the two cases. The proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:casesAimpliesB} used
$A$ and $\lnot A$ for the two cases of the proof-by-cases argument.
It would have also worked well to use $B$ and~$\lnot B$ for the
two cases of the \idxnm{proof-by-cases} argument.
However, sometimes it can be very difficult
to find a good formula for defining the two cases.
