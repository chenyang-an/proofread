\section{Deduction Theorem}\label{sec:propDeductionThm}
\index{Deduction Theorem|(}

The Deduction Theorem is our first main tool for proving
the existence of $\PL$-proofs.  It is the analogue for provability
of Theorem~\ref{thm:deductionSemanticProp}, which stated that
$\Gamma \vDash A\limplies B$ is equivalent to $\Gamma, A \vDash B$.

The intuition behind the Deduction Theorem is that proving
the implication $A\limplies B$ is equivalent to assuming
that $A$ holds and proving~$B$ with the aid of
the hypothesis~$A$.

\begin{theorem}[Deduction Theorem]\label{thm:deduction}
$\Gamma, A \vdash B$ if and only if
$\Gamma \vdash A \limplies B$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The ``if'' direction is very easy. Suppose $\Gamma \vdash A \limplies B$.
Then also $\Gamma, A \vdash\penalty10000 A\limplies\penalty10000 B$. Since
$\Gamma, A \vdash A$, Modus Ponens gives $\Gamma, A \vdash B$.

The ``only if'' direction is the important direction.  Suppose that
$\Gamma, A \vdash B$ and that
\[
C_1,\, C_2,\, C_3, \ldots,\, C_\ell
\]
is a $\PL$-proof of~$B$ from $\Gamma, A$ so that $C_\ell$ is the formula~$B$.
Each $C_i$ is an axiom, a member of~$\Gamma$ or the formula~$A$, or is
inferred by Modus Ponens.

We prove by induction on~$i$ that $\Gamma \vdash A\limplies C_i$.
The proof by induction splits into three cases.
\begin{description}
\item[Case 1:] Suppose that $C_i$ is either an axiom or a member of~$\Gamma$.
Then, certainly $\Gamma \vdash C_i$. Also, $C_i\limplies (A \limplies C_i)$ is
an instance of Axiom~PL1. Thus, by Modus Ponens,
$\Gamma \vdash A \limplies C_i$, as desired.

\item[Case 2:] Suppose $C_i$ is~$A$. By Example~\ref{example:AimpliesA},
$\Gamma \vdash A\limplies A$, which is the same as $\Gamma \vdash A\limplies C_i$,
as desired.

\item[Case 3:] Suppose $C_i$ is inferred by Modus Ponens from
$C_j$ and~$C_k$ with $j,k <i$. Without loss of generality,
$C_k$ is equal to $C_j\limplies C_i$. The induction hypotheses
for $C_j$ and $C_k$ are that $A\limplies C_j$ and $A\limplies C_k$
are theorems of~$\Gamma$. We prove that there is a $\PL$-proof of $A\limplies C_i$ from~$\Gamma$
as follows:
\begin{twocolproof}
$\Gamma \vdash A \limplies C_j$ & Induction hypothesis \\
$\Gamma \vdash A \limplies C_j \limplies C_i$ & Induction hypothesis \\
$\Gamma \vdash (A \limplies C_j \limplies C_i) \limplies (A\limplies C_j) \limplies (A \limplies C_i)$ & Axiom PL2 \\
$\Gamma \vdash (A\limplies C_j) \limplies (A \limplies C_i)$ & Modus Ponens \\
$\Gamma \vdash A \limplies C_i$ & Modus Ponens
\end{twocolproof}
\end{description}
That completes the proof by induction and,
since $A\limplies C_\ell$ is the same as $A\limplies B$, it also
completes the proof of the Deduction Theorem.
\end{proof}

Note how Axiom PL2 is exactly what is needed to make the argument work easily 
in Case~3 above.

Theorem~\ref{thm:PLclosedsubst} and the Deduction Theorem are some
first examples of ``\idxnm{metamathematics}'', that is the use of mathematical
tools to study mathematical objects such as theorems and proofs. We have
given formal definitions of ``theorems'' and ``proofs'' as mathematical
objects, and now Theorem~\ref{thm:PLclosedsubst} and the Deduction Theorem
are theorems that state properties about (PL-)theorems; their proofs use
induction on a given $\PL$-proof to prove the existence of another $\PL$-proof.

\index{Deduction Theorem|)}

\paragraph*{Hypothetical Syllogism.}\index{Hypothetical Syllogism}
As an example of how to use the Deduction Theorem, we show that
the inference rule of \idxnm{Hypothetical Syllogism},
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:HypSyl}
\hbox{
\AxiomC{$A\limplies B$}
\AxiomC{$B\limplies C$}
\BinaryInfC{$A\limplies C$}
\DisplayProof ,}
\end{equation}
is a \idxnm{derived rule} of inference for~$\PL$.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:HypSyl}
For $A$, $B$ and~$C$ any formulas,
\[
\vdash (A\limplies B) \limplies ((B\limplies C) \limplies (A\limplies C)) .
\]
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
We use ``$\Leftrightarrow$'' to mean ``if and only if''.  Applying the
Deduction Theorem three times gives the equivalences
\begin{implications}
\multicolumn{3}{l}{$\vdash (A\limplies B) \limplies ((B\limplies C) \limplies (A\limplies C))$} \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $A\limplies B \vdash (B\limplies C) \limplies (A\limplies C)$ & Deduction Theorem \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $A\limplies B,\ B\limplies C \vdash A\limplies C$ & Deduction Theorem \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $A\limplies B,\ B\limplies C,\, A \vdash C$ & Deduction Theorem \\
\end{implications}
And $A\limplies B,\ B\limplies C,\, A \vdash C$ holds by combining the three hypotheses
with two uses of Modus Ponens to prove~$C$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{coro:HypSyl}
If $\Gamma \vdash A\limplies B$ and $\Gamma \vdash B\limplies C$, then
$\Gamma \vdash A\limplies C$.
\end{corollary}

The corollary implies that the Hypothetical Syllogism inference rule is
admissible in $\PL$-proofs as a ``\idxnm{derived rule}'' of inference.\footnote{Derived
rules of inference are sometimes called ``admissible rules'' of inference.}
That is,
although Modus Ponens is the only allowed rule of inference for $\PL$-proofs,
we may nonetheless allow Hypothetical Syllogism inferences when proving
the existence of a $\PL$-proof. This is because Hypothetical Syllogism can be
simulated by multiple steps in a $\PL$-proof. Consequently, 
it would not make
the proof system~$\PL$ any stronger to include
\idxnm{Hypothetical Syllogism} as an additional rule of inference.
