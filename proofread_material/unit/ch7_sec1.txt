¥section{Intensional and Extensional Approaches}¥label{sec:IntensionalExtensional}

There are two traditional ways to prove the
¥idxnm{First Incompleteness Theorem}. The first way,
sometimes called the ``¥idxnm{intensional}'' approach,
works with the first-order Peano Arithmetic
theory‾($¥PA$). Peano Arithmetic is formalized over
the language‾$L_¥PA$ with induction axioms as its
primary axioms. Although Peano Arithmetic can talk
only about integers, it can in fact formalize
the metamathematical syntax of first-order logic.
It does this by encoding metamathematical concepts
such as ``first-order formula'' and ``$¥FO$-proof''
with G{¥"o}del numbers, namely with integers.
Via G{¥"o}del numbers, Peano Arithmetic can formulate
and reason about the syntactic properties of first-order
formulas, sentences, proofs, and theorems; in particular,
it can formulate the syntactic properties of $¥FO$-proofs and
theorems of‾$¥PA$ itself. This includes all the syntactic
concepts developed in Chapters ¥ref{chap:FOsyntaxSemantics}
and‾¥ref{chap:proofsFO}, including the concepts such
as provability and consistency. This allows Peano Arithmetic
to ``reason'' about its own consistency, and to formulate
a sentence $¥TCon_¥PA$ expressing that Peano Arithmetic
itself is consistent.

The ¥idxnm{First Incompleteness Theorem} for Peano Arithmetic
can be proved by using a diagonal construction similar
to the Diagonal Theorem‾¥ref{thm:Diag} but now using a
sentence‾$A$ that expresses ``The sentence‾$A$ does not have
a $¥PA$-proof'', or informally, the sentence‾$A$ asserts
``I am not $¥PA$-provable''.  The First Incompleteness Theorem
is proved by showing that (under the assumption that $¥PA$ is consistent)
this sentence‾$A$ is true in‾$¥mathcal N$, but is not
a theorem of Peano Arithmetic.
The G{¥"o}del ¥idxnm{Second Incompleteness Theorem}
takes this further by
showing that Peano Arithmetic (unless inconsistent) cannot
prove the sentence $¥TCon_¥PA$ expressing the consistency
of Peano Arithmetic. Thus $¥TCon_¥PA$ is an
explicit and comprehensible example of a true sentence that
cannot be proved by Peano Arithmetic.

Peano Arithmetic is limited to dealing with finite
objects and cannot reason directly with infinite sets.
It can however reason indirectly about definable infinite sets.
For instance, Peano Arithmetic can
formalize the notions of Turing machines and
computations of Turing machines. It can thus indirectly
reason about Turing decidable sets and Turing enumerable
sets in the sense that it can reason about the members of the sets (rather
than about the sets themselves).¥footnote{In fact, Peano Arithmetic can define
many sets beyond decidable, c.e.¥ and co-c.e.¥ sets.} This permits Peano
Arithmetic to formalize a version of the
Completeness Theorem. However, Peano Arithmetic cannot
formalize the definition of truth in a general way. Hence,
it cannot even state the general form of the Soundness Theorem.

¥medskip

The second traditional way to prove the
¥idxnm{First Incompleteness Theorem} is the ``¥idxnm{extensional}''
approach. For this, we use a very weak subtheory‾$¥TQ$ of‾$¥PA$
(called ``¥idxnm{Robinson's theory}‾$¥TQ$'').
For stronger results, an even weaker
subtheory called‾$¥TR$ can be used. The language of both $¥TQ$ and‾$¥TR$
is‾$L_¥PA$. The axioms of‾$¥TQ$ state simple
defining properties of zero, successor, addition, and multiplication,
but $¥TQ$‾has no induction axioms. The theory‾$¥TQ$ is quite weak;
unlike $¥PA$, it cannot even prove elementary things such
as that addition is commutative,
$¥forall x¥, ¥forall y¥,(x+¥penalty10000 y=¥penalty10000 y+¥penalty10000 x)$.
In the {¥em intensional}
approach, $¥PA$‾does prove this.  In the {¥em extensional} approach,
we have only that $¥TQ$ can prove particular instances of
$x+y=y+x$; e.g., $¥TQ$‾can prove
$¥underline 3 + ¥underline 4 = ¥underline 4 + ¥underline 3$ where
``$¥underline 3$'' and ``$¥underline 4$''‾denote  the terms
$S(S(S(0)))$ and $S(S(S(S(0))))$.

We shall see later that $¥TQ$‾can ``¥idxnm{represent}'' every Turing decidable
set‾$R$ in an extensional fashion. What this means is that there is
a formula‾$G_R(x)$ defining membership in‾$R$ such that for any
particular integer‾$n$, $¥TQ$‾can prove either $G_R(¥underline n)$ or
$¥lnot G_R(¥underline n)$ depending on whether $n$‾is in‾$R$ or not.
Similarly, every Turing computable function‾$f$ can be represented by
a formula‾$G_f$ in‾$¥TQ$. This means that, for any $n¥in¥mathbb N$, if
$f(n)=m$, then $¥TQ$‾can prove the sentence
$¥forall x ¥, (G_f(¥underline n,x)¥liff x=¥underline m)$. (The precise
definitions for ``represent'' are given below.)

Perhaps surprisingly, the converse holds too and
any set which is representable in‾$¥TQ$ is
Turing decidable. This has a very general proof that has little to
do with‾$¥TQ$ itself. Namely, since $¥TQ$‾is finitely axiomatized,
its logical consequences‾$¥TCn Q$ are Turing enumerable. Thus, to
decide whether $n¥in R$, one can enumerate the consequences
of‾$¥TQ$ until obtaining either $G_R(¥underline n)$ or $¥lnot G_R(¥underline n)$.
One of these must appear, and it tells us whether $n$ is in‾$R$ or not.
A similar proof shows that every representable function of‾$¥TQ$
is Turing computable.

From these results, it will follow
that a set is representable in‾$¥TQ$ if and only
it is Turing decidable. In light of the Church-Turing Thesis,
we have that any‾$R$ is decidable if and only if it
is representable in‾$¥TQ$.

The First Incompleteness Theorem will be proved from this
fact.  Specifically, a diagonal construction rather
similar to the Diagonal Theorem‾¥ref{thm:Diag} for
decidable sets can be used to form an $L_¥PA$-sentence‾$D$
that asserts it is not a consequence of‾$¥TQ$.  From the
consistency of‾$¥TQ$, it will follow that neither $D$ nor‾$¥lnot D$
is a consequence of‾$¥TQ$.

Nearly all the claims just made about‾$¥TQ$ are also true for
the weaker theory‾$¥TR$. In particular, $¥TR$‾also represents 
precisely the relations that are decidable and the functions
that are computable.  The First Incompleteness Theorem also applies
to‾$¥TR$.

In fact, the same arguments apply to any consistent, axiomatizable
theory‾$T$ containing‾$¥TR$. In particular,
for any such theory‾$T$, the sets representable in‾$T$
are precisely the Turing decidable sets. The diagonal
method thus implies that
there is no complete, consistent, axiomatizable theory‾$T$
containing‾$¥TR$. From this, it follows that $¥TTh {¥mathcal N}$
is not axiomatizable and hence not (Turing) decidable.

¥bigskip

This chapter will carry out the extensional approach
in full detail. There are several reasons for using the
extensional approach. The first, and most important
reason, is that it is technically easier than
the intensional approach. Second, it provides more
justification for the Church-Turing Thesis, since it shows
that the decidable sets can be robustly defined in
terms of any consistent axiomatizable theory $T¥supseteq R$.
In other words, stronger theories do not give more
decidable sets.
Third, by working with $¥TR$ and‾$¥TQ$, we delineate more-or-less
exactly where undecidability
kicks in.

approach, Section‾¥ref{sec:SecondIncomplete} will sketch a high-level proof
of how the intensional approach proceeds. This high-level proof
will be based on Peano Arithmetic‾$¥PA$.¥footnote{%
