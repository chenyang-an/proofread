¥section{Boolean Functions and DNF and CNF}¥label{sec:booleanFunctions}
¥def¥cursectiontitle{Boolean Functions ¥& DNF and CNF}

This section will show how Boolean functions
can be represented by propositional formulas, and introduce
Disjunctive Normal Form (DNF) and Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF)
formulas.

The motivation for looking at Boolean functions is to justify
the use of the five connectives $¥lnot$, $¥land$, $¥lor$, $¥limplies$, and‾$¥liff$
for propositional formulas.  As a first observation, it is not really necessary
to use all five of these. In fact, we could use just the two connectives
$¥lnot$ and‾$¥lor$, and still be able to express everything that can be expressed
using $¥lnot,¥land,¥lor,¥limplies,¥liff$. Similarly, we could use
just $¥lnot$ and‾$¥land$, or just $¥lnot$ and‾$¥limplies$, without any loss
of expressiveness.  (These facts will be proved later in
Theorems ¥ref{thm:adequateRecapBis} and‾¥ref{thm:notImpliesAdequate}.)
On the other hand, we can not omit‾$¥lnot$, as there is
no way to express negation using only $¥land, ¥lor, ¥limplies, ¥liff$.
(For this, see Theorem‾¥ref{thm:notAdequate2} and Exercise‾¥ref{exer:fourNotAdequate}.)

This raises the question of whether it is enough to use
only $¥lnot, ¥land, ¥lor, ¥limplies, ¥liff$. Could it be that
there is some other connective beyond these that cannot be expressed
using $¥lnot, ¥land, ¥lor, ¥limplies, ¥liff$?  The answer to this is
no: the five connectives $¥lnot, ¥land, ¥lor, ¥limplies, ¥liff$ can
express any Boolean function. To formalize this, we define
the notion of a ``¥idxnm{Boolean function}''. A $k$-ary Boolean function can
be viewed as a type of $k$-ary propositional connective. Our first
goal will be to prove that every Boolean function can be represented
by a propositional formula.

¥begin{definition}¥label{def:BooleanFunction}
Let $k¥ge 1$. A $k$-ary¥index{arity} ¥idxem{Boolean function}‾$f$ takes as input
$k$ true/false values and outputs a true/false value; namely,
it is a mapping
¥[
f: ¥{¥True, ¥False¥}^k ¥rightarrow ¥{¥True, ¥False¥} .
¥]
¥end{definition}

¥begin{example}¥label{example:BooleanFunction}
The following define three Boolean functions $f_{¥lnot p_1}$,
$f_{p_1 ¥land p_2}$ and $f_{p_1¥oplus p_2¥oplus p_3}$:
¥begin{eqnarray*}
f_{¥lnot p_1}(x) &=& ¥left¥{ ¥begin{array}{ll}
¥True ‾‾ &¥hbox{if $x$ equals $¥False$} ¥¥
¥False & ¥hbox{if $x$ equals $¥True$}.
¥end{array}¥right. ¥¥
f_{p_1¥land p_2}(x_1,x_2) &=& ¥left¥{ ¥begin{array}{ll}
¥True ‾‾ &¥hbox{if $x_1$ and $x_2$ both equal $¥True$} ¥¥
¥False & ¥hbox{otherwise}.
¥end{array}¥right. ¥¥
f_{p_1¥oplus p_2 ¥oplus p_3}(x_1,x_2,x_3) &=& ¥left¥{ ¥begin{array}{ll}
¥True ‾‾ &¥hbox{if an odd number of $x_1,x_2,x_3$ equal $¥True$} ¥¥
¥False & ¥hbox{if an even number of $x_1,x_2,x_3$ equal $¥True$}.
¥end{array}¥right.
¥end{eqnarray*}
Note that
$f_{¥lnot p_1}$ is ¥idxnm{unary} (1-ary),
$f_{p_1¥land p_2}$ is ¥idxnm{binary} (2-ary) and
$f_{p_1¥oplus p_2 ¥oplus p_3}$ is 3-ary.
¥end{example}

The functions in the example are named $f_A$ where the subscript‾$A$ is a
formula. The third one, $p_1¥oplus p_2 ¥oplus p_3$, uses the
``parity¥index{parity@{parity ($¥oplus$)}} connective''‾$¥oplus$, also called ``¥idxnm{exclusive or}''
or ``xor'' for short. The parity connective
will be discussed more in the next section; the notation ``$¥oplus$''
comes from the fact that if $¥True$ and $¥False$ are identified
with the constants 1 and‾0, then $¥oplus$‾becomes addition mod‾2.

¥paragraph*{Adequacy of $¥lnot, ¥lor, ¥land, ¥limplies, ¥liff$.}
The idea is that the formulas‾$A$ in the subscripts in the previous example ``¥idxnm{represent}''
the Boolean function. This is formalized in the next definition.

¥begin{definition}¥label{def:defineBooleanFunction}
Let $k¥ge 1$ and let $A$ be a propositional formula that uses (at most) the variables
$p_1,¥ldots, p_k$. The $k$-ary Boolean function $f_A(x_1,x_2,¥ldots, x_k)$
is defined by
¥[
¥hbox{$¥displaystyle f_A(x_1,¥ldots, x_k) ‾=‾ ¥varphi(A)$,
‾‾‾‾ where $¥varphi(p_i)=x_i$ for $i=1,2,¥ldots, k$.}
¥]
The propositional formula‾$A$ is said to ¥idxem{represent} the Boolean
function‾$f_A$.
¥end{definition}
It is important to note that this gives a well-defined‾$f_A$, 
since the values of the truth assignment‾$¥varphi$
on $p_1,¥ldots, p_k$ uniquely determine the truth value‾$¥varphi(A)$
of‾$A$.¥footnote{Strictly speaking, we ought to use the notation
$f_{A,k}$ instead of $f_A$ since it is not required that $p_k$
actually appears in‾$A$. However, we use the simpler notation‾$f_A$
as this should never cause any confusion.}

From the definition, every propositional formula
represents a Boolean function. Conversely, the next
theorem shows that every Boolean
function is represented by some propositional formula.

¥begin{theorem}[Adequacy of $¥lnot, ¥lor, ¥land, ¥limplies, ¥liff$]
¥label{thm:adequateStd}¥index{adequacy}
Let $f$ be a $k$-ary Boolean function. Then there is
a propositional formula‾$A$ that represents‾$f$, i.e.,
with $f_A = f$.
¥end{theorem}

We first give an example to illustrate the proof idea, and then will give the
general proof. Consider the Boolean function‾$f$ defined by
¥[
f(x_1, x_2) ‾=‾ ¥left¥{ ¥begin{array}{ll}
¥True ‾‾‾ & ¥hbox{if $x_1 = ¥True$ or $x_2 = ¥False$} ¥¥
¥False & ¥hbox{otherwise.}
¥end{array}¥right.
¥]
You might notice that $f$ is the same as $f_{p_2¥limplies p_1}$, but let's
pretend we do not notice that. The complete list of $f$'s values is:
¥begin{center}
¥begin{tabular}{cc|c}
$x_1$ & $x_2 $ & $f(x_1,x_2)$ ¥¥
¥hline
$¥True$ & $¥True¥taller$ & {¥boldmath$¥True$} ¥¥
$¥True$ & $¥False$ & {¥boldmath$¥True$}  ¥¥
$¥False$ & $¥True$ & $¥False$ ¥¥
$¥False$ & $¥False$ & {¥boldmath$¥True$}
¥end{tabular}
¥end{center}
There are three lines in the table for‾$f$ where $f(x_1,x_2) = ¥True$.
This tells us that $f(x_1,x_2)$ is true precisely when
$x_1=x_2=¥True$, when $x_1=¥True$ and $x_2=¥False$, and when
$x_1 = x_2=¥False$. In other words, $f(x_1,x_2)$ is represented by
the formula
¥begin{equation}¥label{eq:definesEx}
(p_1 ¥land p_2) ¥lor (p_1 ¥land ¥lnot p_2) ¥lor (¥lnot p_1 ¥land ¥lnot p_2).
¥end{equation}
The first disjunct of‾(¥ref{eq:definesEx}) is $p_1¥land p_2$;
this is true exactly when the first line of the table of $f$'s value is
applicable. The second and third disjuncts similarly
correspond to the two other ways that $f$ can be true.

By the way, the formula‾(¥ref{eq:definesEx}) is probably not
the ``best'' formula that represents‾$f$. For instance, $p_1 ¥lor ¥lnot p_2$
is probably a better choice. But (¥ref{eq:definesEx})‾is
the formula representing‾$f$ that comes
out of the general proof that we give next.

¥begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem‾¥ref{thm:adequateStd}]
Let $f$ be a $k$-ary Boolean function. If $f(x_1,¥ldots,x_k) = ¥False$
for all inputs $x_1,¥ldots, x_k ¥in ¥{¥True,¥False¥}$, then $f$‾is represented
by the formula $p_1¥land ¥lnot p_1$.  So, suppose there is at least
one input where $f$'s value is‾$¥True$.

Since $f$ has $k$ inputs, each of which has two possible values, there
are $2^k$ many distinct inputs to‾$f$. Let $¥varphi_1, ¥ldots, ¥varphi_{2^k}$
be the $2^k$ many possible truth assignments to $p_1,¥ldots, p_k$. Although
it is not important for the proof, it might be helpful to think of $¥varphi_1$
as being the truth assignment that sets each $p_i$ to‾$¥True$, and $¥varphi_{2^k}$ as
being the truth assignment that sets each $p_i$ to‾$¥False$, and the other $¥varphi_i$'s
as being the truth assignments
listed in the usual order that lines appear in a truth table.

For $1¥le i ¥le 2^k$ and $1 ¥le j ¥le k$, define the formula‾$L_{i,j}$ by
¥[
L_{i,j} ‾=‾ ¥left¥{ ¥begin{array}{ll}
p_j ‾‾‾ & ¥hbox{if $¥varphi_i(p_j) = ¥True$} ¥¥
¥lnot p_j ‾‾‾ & ¥hbox{if $¥varphi_i(p_j) = ¥False$.}
¥end{array}¥right.
¥]
(``$L$'' stands for ``¥idxnm{literal}''.)
Clearly $L_{i,j}$ is either $p_j$ or $¥lnot p_j$,
and $¥varphi_i(L_{i,j}) = ¥True$. Now define¥footnote{The
``big and'' notation,
$¥bigand$, is similar to a summation sign ($¥sum$): it means
the indicated conjunction (and) of the formulas $L_{i,1}$ through $L_{i,k}$.
}¥index{big and@{big and ($¥bigand$)}}
¥[
C_i ‾=‾ ¥bigand_{j=1}^k L_{i,j} ‾:=‾ L_{i,1} ¥land L_{i,2} ¥land ¥cdots ¥land L_{i,k}.
¥]

We have $¥varphi_i(C_i) = ¥True$ since $¥varphi_i(L_{i,j}) = ¥True$ for all‾$j$.
Furthermore, if $i¥neq i^¥prime$, then $¥varphi_{i^¥prime}(C_i) = ¥False$.
This is because $¥varphi_{i^¥prime}(p_j) ¥neq ¥varphi_i(p_j)$ for at least one value of‾$j$,
and hence $¥varphi_{i^¥prime}(L_{i, j})=¥False$ for at least one‾$j$.

Now let $¥mathcal I$ be the set of values $i$ such that
¥[
f(¥varphi_i(p_1), ¥varphi_i(p_2), ¥ldots, ¥varphi_i(p_k) ) ‾=‾ ¥True.
¥]
We can write $¥mathcal I = ¥{i_1,i_2, ¥ldots, i_¥ell¥}$. 
Note $¥ell¥ge 1$ since
$f$‾is not identically equal to‾$¥False$ for all inputs.  Then define the
formula‾$A$ to be¥index{big or@{big or ($¥bigor$)}}
¥begin{equation}¥label{eq:adequateStd}
A ‾=‾ ¥bigor_{m=1}^¥ell C_{i_m} ‾:=‾ C_{i_1} ¥lor C_{i_2} ¥lor ¥cdots ¥lor C_{i_¥ell}.
¥end{equation}
By inspection, $A$‾represents the function‾$f$: this is because the $C_{i_m}$'s and their
associated truth assignments‾$¥varphi_{i_m}$ enumerate
the cases where‾$f$ is true, and because each $C_i$ is satisfied by a unique‾$¥varphi_i$.
This completes the proof of
Theorem‾¥ref{thm:adequateStd}.
¥end{proof}

¥paragraph*{Disjunction and conjunctive normal forms.}
Theorem‾¥ref{thm:adequateStd} showed that
every function can be represented by a formula that
uses only the connectives $¥lnot$, $¥lor$ and‾$¥land$.
In fact, it shows that ``¥idxnm{disjunctive normal form}'' formulas suffice.

Any formula‾$A$ expressed in the form
¥[
A_1 ¥lor A_2 ¥lor ¥cdots ¥lor A_k,
¥]
with arbitrary parenthesization ({¥em not} necessarily associated from right to left)
is called a ¥idxem{disjunction}. It is permitted that $k=1$.  Each $A_i$ is called a ¥idxem{disjunct} of‾$A$.
We will use the big or¥index{big or@{big or ($¥bigor$)}} notation $¥bigor_{i=1}^k A_i$ to denote‾$A$; however, this notation
is often reserved for the case where the parenthesization is the usual right-to-left order.

Similarly, any formula‾$B$ expressed in the form
¥[
B_1 ¥land B_2 ¥land ¥cdots ¥land B_k,
¥]
again with arbitrary parenthesization, not necessarily associated from right to left,
is called a ¥idxem{conjunction}.  Each $B_i$ is called a ¥idxem{conjunct} of‾$B$.
We will use the big and¥index{big and@{big and ($¥bigand$)}}
notation $¥bigand_{i=1}^k B_i$ to denote‾$B$; however, again
often with parenthesization in the usual right-to-left order.

¥begin{definition}¥label{def:literalProp}
A ¥idxem{literal} is a formula of the form $p_i$ or $¥lnot p_i$.
In other words, a literal is a variable or the negation of a variable.
¥end{definition}

¥begin{definition}¥label{def:DNF}
A ¥emph{conjunction of literals} is a formula of the form¥footnote{In
computer science, a conjunction of literals is sometimes called a
``term''; however, we avoid this terminology,
since ``term'' has a different, well-established meaning in
first-order logic.}
¥[
L_1 ¥land L_2 ¥land ¥cdots ¥land L_k,
¥]
where $k¥ge 1$ and each $L_i$ is a literal.

A ¥idxem{disjunctive normal form} formula, or ¥emph{DNF}
formula for short, is any formula of the form
¥[
C_1 ¥lor C_2 ¥lor ¥cdots ¥lor C_¥ell,
¥]
where $¥ell ¥ge 1$ and each $C_i$ is a conjunction of literals.
¥end{definition}

¥begin{definition}¥label{def:clauseAndCNF}
A ¥idxem{clause} is a disjunction of literals, namely a formula
of the form
¥[
L_1 ¥lor L_2 ¥lor ¥cdots ¥lor L_k,
¥]
where $k¥ge 1$ and each $L_i$ is a literal.

A ¥idxem{conjunctive normal form} formula, or ¥emph{CNF}
formula for short, is any formula of the form
¥[
C_1 ¥land C_2 ¥land ¥cdots ¥land C_¥ell,
¥]
where $¥ell ¥ge 1$ and each $C_i$ is a clause.
¥end{definition}

Theorem‾¥ref{thm:adequateStd} shows that
every Boolean function‾$f$ can be represented by a
propositional formula. In fact, what the proof showed
is that the propositional formula representing‾$f$ can be taken to be a DNF formula.
That is, we have already proved the next theorem.

¥begin{theorem}[Adequacy of DNF Formulas]
¥label{thm:adequateStdDNF}¥index{adequacy}
Let $f$ be a $k$-ary Boolean function. Then there is
a ¥idxnm{disjunctive normal form} (DNF) formula‾$A$ that represents‾$f$, i.e.,
with $f_A = f$.
¥end{theorem}

¥begin{proof}
The proof of Theorem‾¥ref{thm:adequateStd} already established this.
If $f$‾is the constant $¥False$ function then $p_1 ¥land ¥lnot p_1$
represents‾$f$; this formula is a DNF formula (and also a CNF formula).
Otherwise, the formula‾(¥ref{eq:adequateStd}) is a DNF formula that
represents‾$f$.
¥end{proof}


The same thing holds also for CNF formulas:

¥begin{theorem}[Adequacy of CNF Formulas]
¥label{thm:adequateStdCNF}¥index{adequacy}
Let $f$ be a $k$-ary Boolean function. Then there is
a ¥idxnm{conjunctive normal form} (CNF) formula‾$A$ that represents‾$f$, i.e.,
with $f_A = f$.
¥end{theorem}

¥begin{proof}(Sketch)
The proof of Theorem‾¥ref{thm:adequateStd} could
be reworked to prove this directly, using the lines
in the truth table for‾$f$ where $f$ takes on the value‾$¥False$.
Instead of doing this, however, we sketch how to obtain
Theorem‾¥ref{thm:adequateStdCNF} as a corollary of
Theorem‾¥ref{thm:adequateStdDNF}.

Let $f^¥lnot$ be the $k$-ary Boolean function which is the negation
of‾$f$. That is,
¥[
f^¥lnot(x_1, ¥ldots, x_k) = ¥left¥{¥begin{array}{ll}
¥True ‾‾‾& ¥hbox{if $f(x_1,¥ldots,x_k) = ¥False$} ¥¥
¥False ‾‾‾& ¥hbox{if $f(x_1,¥ldots,x_k) = ¥True$}
¥end{array}¥right.
¥]
We just proved that $f^¥lnot$ is represented by
a DNF formula‾$A$. Therefore $f$‾is represented by‾$¥lnot A$.
The formula $¥lnot A$ is the negation of a disjunction
of conjunctions of literals.  By ¥idxnm{De Morgan's law}
(see Section‾¥ref{sec:TautExamples}),
the negation of a conjunction of formulas is tautologically
equivalent to the disjunction of the negations of the same
formulas. Likewise, the negation of a disjunction of formulas is tautologically
equivalent to the conjunction of the negations of the same
formulas. Furthermore, the negation of a literal is equivalent
to a literal (possibly by canceling out
two negation signs).

This allows $¥lnot A$ to be rewritten as a tautologically
equivalent CNF formula by using De‾Morgan's laws¥index{De Morgan's law} to
push the negation sign inside the formula.  We leave the details
to the reader.
¥end{proof}

¥begin{corollary}¥label{coro:DNFCNF}
Any propositional formula‾$A$ is tautologically equivalent
to some disjunctive normal form (DNF) formula, and also tautologically
equivalent to some conjunctive normal form (CNF) formula.
¥end{corollary}

¥begin{proof}
Let $p_1,¥ldots, p_k$ include all the variables in‾$A$.
Let $f_A$ be the $k$-ary Boolean function represented by‾$A$.
By Theorem‾¥ref{thm:adequateStdDNF}, $f_A$‾is represented
by some DNF formula‾$B$. Then, since $A$ and‾$B$
represent the same Boolean function, it must be that
$¥varphi(A)=¥varphi(B)$ for all truth assignments.
Therefore, $A$ and‾$B$ are tautologically equivalent.

The proof that $A$ is tautologically equivalent
to a CNF formula‾$C$ is identical, except
it uses Theorem‾¥ref{thm:adequateStdCNF}
to obtain a CNF formula‾$C$ which defines‾$f_A$.
¥end{proof}

¥begin{example}
Let $f_{p_1¥liff p_2}$ be the Boolean function represented by $p_1 ¥liff p_2$.
We have $f(x_1,x_2) = ¥True$ precisely when either $x_1$ and‾$x_2$ are both equal
to‾$¥True$ or $x_1$ and‾$x_2$ are both equal
to‾$¥False$. Therefore, using the construction from the proof
of Theorem‾¥ref{thm:adequateStd}, $f_{p_1¥liff p_2}$‾is represented
by
¥begin{equation}¥label{eq:liffDNF}
(p_1 ¥land p_2 ) ¥lor ( ¥lnot p_1 ¥land ¥lnot p_2).
¥end{equation}
Consequently, $p_1 ¥liff p_2$ is tautologically equivalent
to this formula‾(¥ref{eq:liffDNF}).

Similar reasoning shows that $f^¥lnot_{p_1¥liff p_2} = f_{¥lnot(p_1¥liff p_2)}$
is represented by the DNF formula
¥begin{equation}¥label{eq:nliffDNF}
(¥lnot p_1 ¥land p_2) ¥lor (p_1 ¥land ¥lnot p_2).
¥end{equation}
The negation of this represents $f_{p_1¥liff p_2}$.
We can use De Morgan's law¥index{De Morgan's law} to obtain a CNF formula representing
$f_{p_1¥liff p_2}$ as follows:
¥begin{implications}
¥multicolumn{3}{l}{$¥lnot[ (¥lnot p_1 ¥land p_2) ¥lor (p_1 ¥land ¥lnot p_2) ]$} & Negation of (¥ref{eq:nliffDNF}) ¥¥
& $¥tautEq$ & $[ ¥lnot(¥lnot p_1 ¥land p_2) ¥land¥lnot(p_1 ¥land ¥lnot p_2) ]$ & De Morgan's Law ¥¥
& $¥tautEq$ & $[ (¥lnot¥lnot p_1 ¥lor ¥lnot p_2) ¥land (¥lnot p_1 ¥lor ¥lnot¥lnot p_2) ]$ & De Morgan's Law ¥¥
& $¥tautEq$ & $[ (p_1 ¥lor ¥lnot p_2) ¥land (¥lnot p_1 ¥lor p_2) ]$ & Double Negation
¥end{implications}
Thus, $(p_1 ¥lor ¥lnot p_2) ¥land (¥lnot p_1 ¥lor p_2)$ is a CNF formula representing $f_{p_1 ¥liff p_2}$, and
therefore is tautologically equivalent to $p_1¥liff p_2$.
¥end{example}

Theorem‾¥ref{thm:adequateStdCNF} was proved by starting with a DNF formula
representing the negated function‾$f^¥lnot$, adding a negation sign, and using
De‾Morgan's laws¥index{De Morgan's law} to convert it to an equivalent CNF formula. An alternate
way to prove that theorem would be to start with a DNF formula
for‾$f$ (not for the negated function‾$f^¥lnot$) and apply the
distributive laws¥index{distributivity} for $¥lor$ and‾$¥land$ and
the law of the ¥idxnm{excluded middle} to convert it to
a CNF. As an example, we just saw that $(p_1 ¥land p_2 ) ¥lor ( ¥lnot p_1 ¥land ¥lnot p_2)$
is a DNF formula representing $f_{p_1¥liff p_2}$. This can be converted using distributive laws
as follows, using $¥top$¥index{true, nullary connective@{true, nullary connective ($¥top$)}} as a constant with truth value‾$¥True$:
¥begin{implications}
¥multicolumn{3}{l}{$[p_1 ¥land p_2] ¥lor (¥lnot p_1 ¥land ¥lnot p_2)$} & DNF formula (¥ref{eq:nliffDNF}) ¥¥
& $¥tautEq$ & $([p_1 ¥land p_2] ¥lor ¥lnot p_1) ¥land ([p_1 ¥land p_2] ¥lor ¥lnot p_2)$ & Distributive Law ¥¥
& $¥tautEq$ & $((p_1 ¥lor ¥lnot p_1) ¥land (p_2 ¥lor ¥lnot p_1))¥land ((p_1 ¥lor ¥lnot p_2) ¥land (p_2 ¥lor ¥lnot p_2))$ & Distributive Law ¥¥
& $¥tautEq$ & $(¥top ¥land (p_2 ¥lor ¥lnot p_1))¥land ((p_1 ¥lor ¥lnot p_2) ¥land ¥top)$ & Law of Excluded Middle ¥¥
& $¥tautEq$ & $(p_2 ¥lor ¥lnot p_1)¥land (p_1 ¥lor ¥lnot p_2)$ & $¥top¥land A ¥tautEq A$ ¥¥
¥end{implications}
The final formula is another CNF formula for $f_{p_1¥liff p_2}$; it is the same as obtained in
the previous example after using the commutativity of‾$¥lor$.
