\section{Proof of the Completeness Theorem}\label{sec:completenessProofFO}

We now prove the Completeness Theorem~\ref{thm:completeFO}. For this,
it suffices to prove part~(a), so we assume that $\Gamma$~is
a consistent set of formulas and need to prove that $\Gamma$~is
satisfiable by constructing a structure~$\mathfrakA$ which
satisfies~$\Gamma$. The proof will use the same ideas as the
proof of the Completeness Theorem for the propositional
proof system~$\PL$; however, there are substantial additional
ingredients needed to handle quantifiers and to construct a
model~$\mathfrakA$ of~$\Gamma$.  Very briefly, the proof requires the
following steps: First, additional constant symbols are introduced
and $\Gamma$ is extended to a set~$\Delta$ of sentences which
is ``strongly Henkin''. Second, \idxnm{Lindenbaum's Theorem}
is used to further extend~$\Gamma$ to
to a set~$\Pi$ of sentences which
is complete and still consistent and strongly Henkin.
Third, a structure~$\mathfrakA$ is constructed that satisfies~$\Pi$.

We will need to be careful at times about what language
is being used. We assume that $\Gamma$ is a set of
$L$-sentences. We also use a larger language~$L^\plus$ which
extends $L$ with the addition of constant symbols~$d_i$
so that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Lplus}
L^\plus ~=~ L \cup \{ d_1, d_2, d_3,\ldots \}.
\end{equation}
The constant symbols~$d_i$ are new and are not in~$L$.
The sets $\Delta$ and~$\Pi$ will be sets of $L^\plus$-sentences.
The structure~$\mathfrakA$ will be an $L^\plus$-structure,
but in the end, $\mathfrakA$~will be restricted to the language~$L$
so as to obtain an $L$-structure satisfying~$\Gamma$.

\paragraph*{Henkin and strongly Henkin.}

Recall that a \idxnm{closed term} is a term that does not contain any variables~$x_i$.
For example, $0+S(0)$ is a closed $L_\PA$-term, but $0+x_1$ is not.

\begin{definition}
Let $\Gamma$ be a set of $L$-sentences.
The set~$\Gamma$ is \idxem{Henkin} if the following holds:
For any sentence $\exists x\, A(x)$ such that $\Gamma \vdash \exists x\, A(x)$,
there is a closed $L$-term~$t$ such that $\Gamma \vdash A(t)$.
\end{definition}

The point of being Henkin is that, for any provable
sentence~$\exists x\, A(x)$ that asserts the
existence of an object, there is a closed term~$t$ that
gives an example of such an object.  The closed term~$t$,
being variable-free, serves as a name for a particular object; it can
be considered to be ``\idxnm{witness}'' for the truth
of $\exists x\, A(x)$.

The Henkin property was stated using $\exists x$.
By convention, $\exists x$~is
an abbreviation for $\lnot \forall x \lnot$. Therefore, an equivalent way to state
the Henkin property is as follows:
For any formula~$A$, if $\Gamma \vdash \lnot \forall x\, A(x)$,
then for some closed term~$t$, $\Gamma \vdash \lnot A(t)$.
The term~$t$ can be viewed as a ``\idxnm{counterexample}''
that witnesses the falsity of $\forall x\, A(x)$.

\begin{definition}
Let $\Gamma$ be a set of $L$-sentences.
The set~$\Gamma$ is \idxem{strongly Henkin} if the following holds:
For any sentence $\forall x\, A(x)$,
there is a constant symbol~$c\in L$ such that
the sentence
\[
A(c) \limplies \forall x \,  A(x)
\]
is a member of~$\Gamma$.
\end{definition}
Note that if $\Gamma$~is strongly Henkin, then $\Gamma$~is Henkin.
This is because $c$ is a closed term, and by the previous remark
and Modus Tollens.

Given that $\Gamma$ is consistent, we wish to form a larger
set~$\Delta$ of sentences which augments~$\Gamma$
to be both consistent and strongly Henkin. For this, we must introduce
new constant symbols. (Indeed, the language~$L$ may not
have any constant symbols at all, and thus there may not even be any
closed $L$-terms.)

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:stronglyHenkin}
Suppose $\exists x\, A(x)$ is an $L$-sentence and $\Gamma$~is a
consistent set of $L$-sentences.
Let $c$ be a new constant symbol, so $c\notin L$. Then
\[
\hbox{ $\Gamma \cup \{ A(c) \limplies \forall x\, A(x)\}$ is consistent.}
\]
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Suppose the conclusion fails. Then, by the
Proof by Contradiction
Theorem~\ref{thm:ProofByContradictionFO}(b),
\[
\Gamma \vdash \lnot [ A(c) \limplies \forall x\, A(x) ].
\]
Therefore, by the TAUT rule, $\Gamma \vdash \lnot \forall x\, A(x)$
and $\Gamma \vdash A(c)$. From the latter, since $c$ is new,
the Theorem on Constants~\ref{thm:constantsSyntactic} implies that
$\Gamma \vdash \forall x\, A(x)$. This contradicts the consistency
of~$\Gamma$.
\end{proof}

Henceforth, we let $d_1,d_2,d_3,\ldots$ be new constant symbols
that do not appear in~$\Gamma$, and let $L^\plus$ be the language~(\ref{eq:Lplus})
consisting of $L$ plus the constant symbols~$d_i$.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:stronglyHenkinDelta}
Suppose $\Gamma$ is a consistent set of $L$-sentences.
Then there is a consistent and strongly Henkin set~$\Delta$
of $L^\plus$-sentences such that $\Delta \supseteq \Gamma$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We will form $\Delta$ by adding sentences to~$\Gamma$ one at a time
while preserving consistency.  Enumerate all $L^\plus$-sentences that start
with a universal quantifier in an infinite sequence as
\[
\forall x_{k_1} A_1(x_{k_1}), \,
\forall x_{k_2} A_2(x_{k_2}), \,
\forall x_{k_3} A_3(x_{k_3}), \, \ldots
\]
so that no constant symbol $d_j$ appears in any $\forall x_{k_i} A_i(x_{k_i})$
with $i\le j$.\footnote{One way to do this is to enumerate
the $L^\plus$-sentences in arbitrary order, discarding the ones that do not
start with the symbol~$\forall$. To ensure that the constant symbol~$d_j$
does not appear in the first $j$~formulas, one can insert
extra sentences into the sequence, say of the form $\exists x_1 \, x_1=x_1$
(if the equality sign,~$=$, is in~$L$)
or $\forall x_1\, (P(x_1,\ldots,x_1)\liff P(x_1,\ldots,x_1))$. These
extra inserted sentences serve no purpose except to delay the appearance of
a constant symbol~$d_j$ until after the $j$-th sentence.}
The important point is that every sentence of the form $\forall x\, A(x)$
appears in the sequence at least once.
It is OK if the same sentence appears multiple times in the sequence.

Define $\Gamma_0 = \Gamma$ and for each $i > 0$, define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:stronglyHenkinThm}
\Gamma_i ~=~ \Gamma_{i-1} \cup \{ A_i ( d_i ) \limplies \forall x_{k_i}\, A(x_{k_i}) \}.
\end{equation}
We claim that each $\Gamma_i$ is consistent. This is readily proved by
induction on~$i$; the induction step can use Lemma~\ref{lem:stronglyHenkin}
since $d_i$ does not appear in $\Gamma_{i-1}$ or in $A(x_{k_i})$.

The set $\Delta$ is defined as $\Delta = \bigcup_i \Gamma_i$.
Note $\Gamma_i \supset \Gamma_{i-1}$ for all~$i$. If $\Delta$~was inconsistent,
then since $\FO$-proofs are finite, some finite subset of~$\Delta$ would be
inconsistent. That would imply that some $\Gamma_i$ is inconsistent.
Therefore, $\Delta$~must be consistent.  By construction, $\Delta$~contains
all sentences of the form $A_i ( d_i ) \limplies \forall x_{k_i}\, A(x_{k_i})$ and thus is
strongly Henkin.
\end{proof}

\paragraph*{Lindenbaum's theorem.}
\idxnm{Lindenbaum's Theorem} was earlier proved for the propositional
proof system~$\PL$. The same construction works also for
sets of first-order sentences.

\begin{definition}
A set~$\Gamma$ of sentences is \idxem{complete} if, for
every sentence $A$, either $A\in \Gamma$ or $\lnot A \in \Gamma$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Lindenbaum's Theorem]
\label{thm:lindenbaumFO}\index{Lindenbaum's Theorem}
Suppose $\Gamma$ is a consistent set of sentences. Then there is
a consistent, complete set~$\Pi$ of sentences such that $\Gamma \subseteq \Pi$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The proof is identical to the proof of Lindenbaum's Theorem~\ref{thm:lindenbaumPL}
except that now we are working with first-order sentences instead of
propositional formulas and that Theorem~\ref{thm:extendConsisFO}
is used instead of Corollary~\ref{coro:extendConsis}.
\end{proof}

So far, we have established that $\Gamma$ can be extended
to a complete and strongly Henkin set of sentences.

\begin{corollary}\label{coro:lindenbaum Henkin}
Suppose $\Gamma$ is a consistent set of~$L$-sentences. There
there is a consistent, complete, strongly Henkin set $\Pi$
of $L^\plus$-sentences such that $\Gamma \subseteq \Pi$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
First use Theorem~\ref{thm:stronglyHenkinDelta} to
form $\Delta$ as a strongly Henkin, consistent set of
$L^\plus$-sentences such that $\Delta \supset \Gamma$. Then apply
Lindenbaum's Theorem~(\ref{thm:lindenbaumFO}) to
obtains a consistent, strongly Henkin, complete set~$\Pi$
of $L^\plus$-sentences such that $\Pi \supset \Delta$.
\end{proof}

Note that Lindenbaum's Theorem is applied to~$\Delta$, not~$\Gamma$.
Namely, the strongly Henkin construction is done before
Lindenbaum's Theorem is invoked.

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:gammaProperties}
Let $\Pi$ be a strongly Henkin, consistent, complete set
of $L^\plus$-sentences as above. Suppose $A$, $B$ and $\forall x\, C(x)$
are $L^\plus$-sentences.
\begin{itemize}
\item[(a)] $A\in \Pi$ if and only if $\lnot A \notin \Pi$.
\item[(b)] $A\limplies B \in \Pi$ if and only if $A\notin\Pi$ or $B\in \Pi$.
\item[(c)] If $\Pi \vdash A$, then $A\in \Pi$.
\item[(d)] $\lnot \forall x\, C(x) \in \Pi$ if and only if, for some closed term~$t$, $\lnot C(t) \in \Pi$.
\item[(e)] $\forall x\, C(x) \in \Pi$ if and only if, for all closed terms~$t$, $C(t) \in \Pi$.
\end{itemize}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The proofs of parts (a) and~(b) are identical to
the proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:completePLconds} except
that now $A$ and~$B$ are first-order sentences instead
of propositional formulas.
For part~(c), suppose
$\Pi\vdash A$. Then
$\lnot A$ cannot be in~$\Pi$, since otherwise $\Pi$ would
be inconsistent. Then, by~(a), $A \in \Pi$.

For (d), first suppose $\lnot \forall x\, C(x) \in \Pi$.
Since $\Pi$ is strongly Henkin, it is also Henkin;
therefore, $\Pi \vdash \lnot C(t)$ for some closed
term~$t$. By~(c), $\lnot C(t)$ is in~$\Pi$.
Second suppose $\lnot C(t) \in \Pi$.
Since $\lnot C(t) \vdash \lnot \forall x\, C(x)$,
part~(c) implies that $\lnot \forall x\, C \in\penalty10000 \Gamma$.
That proves~(d).

Part~(e) is immediate from parts (a) and~(d).
\end{proof}

\paragraph*{Construction of a Henkin model.}
The next step in the proof of the Completeness Theorem
is to define an $L^\plus$-structure~$\mathfrakA$ that will be shown
to satisfy~$\Pi$. The intuition for constructing~$\mathfrakA$ is
that the complete set~$\Pi$ will guide the choice of what is
true in~$A$. This is analogous to what was done in the
proof of the Completeness Theorem~\ref{thm:completePL}
for propositional logic~($\PL$), where
a truth assignment was defined by letting $\varphi(p_i)=\True$
if and only if $p_i\in\Pi$. And, indeed, the
structure~$\mathfrakA$ will be defined so that
$P^\mathfrakA(t_1,\ldots, t_k)$ is true if and only
if $P(t_1,\ldots, t_k) \in \Pi$.

But, since we are working in first-order logic,
we also must define the
universe~$|\mathfrakA|$ and give interpretations
to the constant symbols and function symbols.
The solution to this is simultaneously
ingenious and straightforward: the universe
of~$\mathfrakA$ consists of (essentially) the
closed $L^\plus$-terms. If the equality sign is not
included in the language~$L$, this idea works without
modification. However, if the equality sign,~$=$,
is part of the language~$L$, then the universe
of~$\mathfrakA$ consists of the set of closed $L^\plus$-terms,
but identifying terms $s$ and~$t$ such that $s=t$ is
in~$\Pi$.  This identification is done by showing
that ``$s=t\in \Pi$'' is an equivalence relation~$\sim$ on terms.
We will write $[s]$ to denote
the equivalence class containing~$s$. The universe~$|\mathfrakA|$ will
be the set of equivalence classes~$[s]$,
for $s$ ranging over the closed $L^\plus$-terms.

\begin{definition}[Definition of $\sim$]
Let $s$ and $t$ be closed $L^\plus$-terms.
\begin{enumerateLoose}
\item If $=$ is not a symbol of~$L$, then $s \sim t$ holds if $s$ and~$t$
are the same term.  (In other words, $\sim$~is the identity relation.)
\item If $=$ is a symbol of~$L^\plus$, then $s\sim t$ holds if
the sentence $s=t$ is in~$\Pi$.
\end{enumerateLoose}
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}\label{thm:simEquivalence}
The relation~$\sim$ is an equivalence relation.\index{equivalence relation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
By definition, $\sim$~is an \idxnm{equivalence relation}
if is reflexive\index{reflexivity}, symmetric\index{symmetry}
and transistive\index{transitivity}. If $=$ is not in the language,
then $\sim$~is the identity relation and of course is an equivalence
relation. So suppose that the equality sign,~$=$, is in~$L$.

To show reflexivity, we must prove that $s\sim s$ by
proving that $s=s$ is in~$\Pi$.  It is easy to check that
$\Pi \vdash s=s$ (using an EQ1 axiom and the Substitution rule).
Hence, by Lemma~\ref{lem:gammaProperties}(c), $s=s \in \Pi$.

To show symmetry, we must show that if $s\sim t$ then $t\sim s$,
or equivalently, that if $s=t \in \Pi$ then $t=s\in \Pi$.
By EQ2 and the Substitution rule, $\vdash s=t \limplies t=s$.
Suppose $s=t \in \Pi$. Then $\Pi \vdash t=s$; thus $t=s \in \Pi$
by Lemma~\ref{lem:gammaProperties}(c).

Transitivity works similarly: we must show that if $r\sim s$ and $s\sim t$,
then $r\sim t$. Suppose $r=s\in \Pi$ and $s=t \in \Pi$.
It will suffice to show $r  = t \in \Pi$.
Now $\vdash r=s\limplies s=t \limplies r=t$, as it is a substitution
instance of an EQ3 axiom. Then, using Modus Ponens twice,
$\Gamma\vdash r=t$, so $r=t \in \Gamma$.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}
The $L^\plus$-structure~$|\mathfrakA|$ is defined by letting
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fmathfrakDef}
|\mathfrakA| ~=~ \{ [s] : \hbox{$s$ is a closed~$L^\plus$-term} \},
\end{equation}
and defining the interpretations of the non-logical
symbols by
\begin{eqnarray*}
c^\mathfrakA &=& [c] \\[1ex]
f^\mathfrakA &=& 
      \{ \langle [s_1],\ldots, [s_k], [f(s_1,\ldots, s_k)]\rangle : 
          \hbox{$s_1,\ldots s_k$ are closed $L^\plus$-terms} \} \\[1ex]
P^\mathfrakA &=& \{ \langle [s_1],\ldots,[s_k]\rangle : P(s_1,\ldots, s_k) \in \Pi \}.
\end{eqnarray*}
\end{definition}
A more intuitive way to express the
definition of the $k$-ary function~$f^\mathfrakA$ is that
\[
f^\mathfrakA([s_1],\ldots, [s_k]) ~=~ [f(s_1,\ldots, s_k)].
\]
Note that each $[s_i]$ is an object in~$|\mathfrakA|$.  The function~$f^\mathfrakA$
takes as input the $k$~objects $[s_1], \ldots, [s_k]$; it outputs
the object $[f(s_1,\ldots, s_k)]$.  Similarly, a more intuitive
way to write the definition of the $k$-ary predicate~$P^\mathfrakA$ is that
\[
\hbox{$P^\mathfrakA([s_1],\ldots,[s_k])$ is true}
\qquad \hbox{if and only if}\qquad
P(s_1,\ldots,s_k) \in \Pi.
\]

We still need to show that
$f^\mathfrakA$ and $P^\mathfrakA$
are \idxem{well-defined} by the above definition.
The issue is that $f^\mathfrakA([s_1],\ldots, [s_k])$ was defined
in terms of particular representatives $s_1,\ldots, s_k$
of equivalence classes. To show that $f^\mathfrakA$ has a well-definition,
we must show that, for all closed $L^\plus$-terms $s_1,\ldots, s_k$
and $r_1,\ldots, r_k$,
\[
\hbox{If $r_i \sim s_i$ for all~$i$, then $f(r_1,\ldots,r_k)\sim f(s_1,\ldots,s_k)$.}
\]
This is equivalent to showing that the definition of~$f^\mathfrakA$ as a
set of $(k+1)$-tuples defines the graph of a (single-valued) function.

Similarly, to show that $P^\mathfrakA$ is well-defined, we
must show that,
\[
\hbox{If $r_i \sim s_i$ for all~$i$, then $P(r_1,\ldots,r_k)\in \Pi$ iff $P(s_1,\ldots,s_k) \in \Pi$.}
\]

\begin{lemma}
The functions~$f^\mathfrakA$ and predicates $P^\mathfrakA$
are \idxnm{well-defined}.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Suppose that $r_i \sim s_i$ for $i=1,\ldots, k$. Thus
$r_i = s_i \in \Pi$ for all~$i$.  In addition, if $f$~is a
$k$-ary function symbol, then
\[
\vdash r_1=s_1 \limplies r_2=s_2 \limplies \cdots \limplies r_k=s_k
       \limplies f(r_1,\ldots,r_k)=f(s_1,\ldots,s_k),
\]
since this is a substitution instance of an EQ${}_f$ axiom.
Therefore, $\Pi \vdash f(r_1,\ldots,r_k)=\penalty10000 f(s_1,\ldots,s_k)$,
so $f(r_1,\ldots,r_k) \sim f(s_1,\ldots,s_k)$.
In addition, if $P$~is a $k$-ary predicate symbol,
\[
\vdash r_1=s_1 \limplies r_2=s_2 \limplies \cdots \limplies r_k=s_k
       \limplies ( P(r_1,\ldots,r_k)\liff P(s_1,\ldots,s_k) ).
\]
Thus, $\Pi \vdash P(r_1,\ldots,r_k)$ if and only if
$\Pi \vdash P(s_1,\ldots,s_k)$. Thus
$P(r_1,\ldots,r_k)\in \Pi$ if and only if
$P(s_1,\ldots,s_k)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:sigmaValue}
Let $\mathfrakA$ be as defined above and let $\sigma$~be
an object assignment. For all closed $L^\plus$-terms~$t$,
we have $\sigma(t) = [t]$. Hence $t^\mathfrakA = [t]$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
This is proved by induction on~$t$. The base case is where $t$
is a constant symbol. By the definition of truth,
$\sigma(c) = c^\mathfrakA = [c]$.

The induction step is where $t$ is $f(s_1,\ldots, s_k)$. The
$k$~many induction hypotheses state that $\sigma(s_i) = [s_i]$.
Thus
\begin{implicationsnarrow}
$(f(s_1,\ldots, s_k))^{\mathfrakA}$
& = & $\sigma(f(s_1,\ldots, s_k))$ & Definition~\ref{def:tClosedValue} \\
& = & $f^\mathfrakA(\sigma(s_1), \ldots \sigma(s_k))$ & Definition of truth \\
& = & $f^\mathfrakA([s_1],\ldots,[s_k])$ & Induction hypotheses \\
& = & $[f(s_1,\ldots, s_k)]$ & Definition of $f^\mathfrakA$  \qedhere
\end{implicationsnarrow}
\end{proof}

\paragraph*{Final stage of the proof of the Completeness Theorem.}
We are now ready to finish the proof of the Completeness
Theorem. We have constructed a consistent, complete, strongly Henkin
set~$\Pi$ of sentences with $\Gamma \subseteq \Pi$. We have also
defined an $L^\plus$-structure~$\mathfrakA$.  The remaining
step is to show that $\mathfrakA \vDash \Pi$.  From that, it
follows that $\mathfrakA \vDash \Gamma$.  Of course, $\mathfrakA$
is an $L^\plus$-structure not an $L$-structure, but by taking the
restriction of~$\mathfrakA$ to the language~$L$, i.e.\ by
discarding the interpretations of the constant symbols~$d_i$
in $L^\plus\setminus L$, we obtain an $L$-structure that
satisfies~$\Gamma$. (See Theorem~\ref{thm:truthInExpansion}.)

\begin{lemma}
$\mathfrakA \vDash \Pi$.  Hence $\mathfrakA \vDash \Gamma$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
We shall prove:

\begin{claim}
For every $L^\plus$-sentence~$A$, we have $\mathfrakA \vDash A$ if and only if $A\in \Pi$.
\end{claim}

The proof of the claim is by induction on the logical complexity of~$A$.
Two of the induction steps are very similar to the proof of the
claim on page~\pageref{claim:completenessPL} used for the proof of the Completeness
Theorem for~$\PL$, but we include them here for completeness.\footnote{Pun not intended!}

\begin{description}
\item[Base case \#1:] Suppose $A$ is an atomic formula $P(s_1,\ldots, s_k)$.
By Lemma~\ref{lem:sigmaValue}, $\sigma(s_i) = [s_i]$ for any truth assignment~$\sigma$.
Therefore, by the definition of truth, and the definition of~$P^\mathfrakA$,
\[
\mathfrakA \vDash P(s_1,\ldots, s_k) ~\Leftrightarrow~
    \langle [s_1],\ldots, [s_k] \rangle\in P^\mathfrakA ~\Leftrightarrow
    P(s_1,\ldots,s_k)\in \Pi.
\]
\item[Base case \#2:] Suppose $A$ is an atomic formula $s_1=s_2$.
By Lemma~\ref{lem:sigmaValue}, $\sigma(s_1) = [s_1]$ and $\sigma(s_2) = [s_2]$
for any truth assignment~$\sigma$. Therefore, $\mathfrakA \vDash\penalty10000  s_1=\penalty10000 s_2$
if and only if $[s_1]=[s_2]$. That holds if and only if $s_1\sim s_2$, and that
is further equivalent to $s_1=s_2 \in \Pi$ by the definition of~$\sim$.
Thus, $\mathfrakA \vDash s_1=s_1$ is true if and only if $s_1=s_2$ is in~$\Pi$.
\item[Induction step \#1:] Suppose $A$ is $\lnot B$.
The induction hypothesis tells that the claim holds for~$B$. Thus,
\begin{implicationsnarrow}
$\lnot B \in \Pi$
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $B \notin \Pi$ & Lemma~\ref{lem:gammaProperties}(a) \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $\mathfrakA \nvDash B$ & Induction hypothesis \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $\mathfrakA \vDash \lnot B$ & Definition of truth
\end{implicationsnarrow}

\item[Induction step \#2:] Suppose $A$ is $B\limplies C$.
The induction hypothesis holds for both $B$ and~$C$. Then
\begin{implicationsnarrow}
$(B\limplies C) \in \Pi$
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $B\notin\Pi$ or $C \in \Pi$ & Lemma~\ref{lem:gammaProperties}(b) \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $\mathfrakA \nvDash B$ or $\mathfrakA \vDash C$ & Induction hypotheses for $B$ and $C$ \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $\mathfrakA \vDash (B\limplies C)$ & Definition of truth
\end{implicationsnarrow}
\item[Induction step \#3:] Suppose $A$ is $\forall x\, B(x)$.
The induction hypothesis holds for $B(t)$ for every closed $L^+$-term~$t$. Then
\begin{implicationsnarrow}
$\forall x\, B(x) \in \Pi$
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & for all closed $L^\plus$-terms, $B(t) \in \Pi$ & Lemma~\ref{lem:gammaProperties}(e) \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & for all closed $L^\plus$-terms $t$, $\mathfrakA \vDash B(t)$
    & Induction hypotheses for $B(t)$ \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & for all closed $L^\plus$-terms $t$, $\mathfrakA \vDash B(t^\mathfrakA)$
    & Theorem \ref{thm:relaxedSubst} \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & for all~$\sigma$, $\mathfrakA \vDash B(x)[\sigma]$ & Lemma~\ref{lem:sigmaValue} \\
& $\Leftrightarrow$ & $\mathfrakA \vDash \forall x\, B(x)$ & Definition of truth \\
\end{implicationsnarrow}
Therefore, $\mathfrakA \vDash \forall x\, B(x)$ if and only if $\forall x\, B(x) \in \Pi$. $\qedhere$
\end{description}
\end{proof}

\noindent
That concludes the proof of the Completeness Theorem.
