\section{Techniques for Representability}\label{sec:representBasic}

The main goal of the next three sections is to prove
Theorem~\ref{thm:decidableAreRepresentable}, that every decidable
predicate and every computable function is representable
in~$\TR$.  We have so far established only a few things to be
representable in~$\TR$. This includes the equality~($=$)
and less-than-or-equal-to~($\le$) relations, and the
successor, addition, and multiplication functions.
We shall ramp up rather quickly to show that many
other relations and functions are representable.

Our theorems about representability will be expressed in
terms of representability in~$\TR$, but of course apply
also to any consistent theory $T\supseteq \TR$. Since $\TR$
is a true theory, any formula~$A_S(x_1,\ldots,x_k)$ that
represents a $k$-ary relation~$S$
also defines that relation in the non-negative integers~$\mathcal N$
(in the sense of Definition~\ref{def:definesRelation}). Likewise,
any formula~$A_f(x_1,\ldots,x_k,y)$ that represents
a $k$-ary function~$f$ also defines the function~$f$ in~$\mathcal N$.

\subsection*{Boolean combinations of relations.}
Boolean combinations of relations are formed by taking
set complementation, union, intersection, etc.  These
operations preserve the property of being representable:

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:BooleanRepresentable}
Let $k\in \mathbb N$ and suppose $S_1$ and~$S_2$ are
$k$-ary relations and
representable in~$\TR$.  Then the following relations
are also representable in~$\TR$:
\begin{itemizeLoose}
\item[(a)]
$\overline {S_1} := \mathbb N^k \setminus S_1 =
     \{ \langle n_1,\ldots, n_k\rangle \in \mathbb N^k
        : \langle n_1,\ldots, n_k\rangle \notin S_1 \}$.
\item[(b)] $S_1 \cap S_2$.
\item[(c)] $S_1 \cup S_2$.
\item[(d)] $S_1 \setminus S_2$.
\end{itemizeLoose}
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Let $A_1(x_1,\ldots, x_k)$ and~$A_2(x_1,\ldots, x_k)$ represent
$S_1$ and~$S_2$ in~$\TR$. For~(a),
we claim that $\lnot A_1$ represents~$\overline{S_1}$. To prove this, we must show that,
for all $n_1,\ldots, n_k \in \mathbb N$,
\begin{enumerateLoose}[label=(\roman*)]
\item If $S_1(n_1,n_2, \ldots, n_k)$ is false,
then $\TR \vdash \lnot A_1(\underline{n_1},\underline{n_2},\ldots,\underline{n_k})$, and
\item If $S_1(n_1,n_2, \ldots, n_k)$ is true,
then $\TR \vdash \lnot \lnot A_1(\underline{n_1},\underline{n_2},\ldots,\underline{n_k})$.
\end{enumerateLoose}
These follow immediately from the definition of what it means
for~$A_1$ to represent~$S_1$.

For~(b), we claim that $A_1\land A_2$ represents $S_1 \cap S_2$.
To prove this, we must show that, for all $n_1,\ldots, n_k \in \mathbb N$,
\begin{enumerateLoose}[label=(\roman*)]
\item If $S_1(\vec n)$ and $S_2(\vec n)$ are both true,
then $\TR \vdash A_1(\underline {\vec n}) \land A_2(\underline {\vec n})$ and
\item If $S_1(\vec n)$ and $S_2(\vec n)$ are not both true,
then $\TR \vdash \lnot (A_1(\underline {\vec n}) \land A_2(\underline {\vec n}))$,
\end{enumerateLoose}
where $\vec n$ and $\underline {\vec n}$ are shorthand notations
for $n_1,\ldots, n_k$ and $\underline{n_1},\underline{n_2},\ldots,\underline{n_k}$.
By the definition of the representability of $S_1$ and~$S_2$, if
$S_1(\vec n)$ and $S_2(\vec n)$ are both true then $\TR$~proves both
$A_1(\underline {\vec n})$ and $A_2(\underline {\vec n})$. Thus (i)~holds.
Otherwise, if $S_1(\vec n)$ and $S_2(\vec n)$ are not both true,
then $\TR$ proves at least one of
$\lnot A_1(\underline {\vec n})$ or $\lnot A_2(\underline {\vec n})$. Thus (ii)~holds.

Part~(c) can be proved by showing similarly that $A_1\lor A_2$
represents $S_1 \cup S_2$. Alternatively,
part~(c) follows from (a) and~(b), since
$S_1 \cup S_2 = \overline{ \overline {S_1} \cap \overline {S_2} }$. Part~(d)
is proved by a similar argument.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}
We show that the greater-than~($>$) relation, the less-than~($<$) and the
greater-than-or-equal-to~($\ge$) relation are representable in~$\TR$.
The greater-than relation is $\{ \langle m, n \rangle : m>n \}$. This
is the complement of the less-than-or-equal-to~($\le$) which
was shown to be representable in Example~\ref{example:representEqLt}.
Hence, by Theorem~\ref{thm:BooleanRepresentable}, the
greater-than~($>$) relation is representable.

The less-than~$<$ relation is the set difference of the less-than-or-equal-to~$\le$
relation and the equality~$=$ relation. Those two relations are represented
by $x_1\le x_2$ and $x_1 = x_2$.
Hence, by Theorem~\ref{thm:BooleanRepresentable}, the
less-than~$<$ relation is
represented by the formula~$x_1 \le x_2 \land x_1 \neq x_2$.
As specified in Notation~\ref{notate:le}, this is the same
as the formula $x_1<x_2$.
\end{example}

\subsection*{Composition with representable functions}

We now take the topic of composition and representability. The goal
is to have flexibility in combining representable relations and
functions to form new representable relations and functions.
As a simple example, we would like to be able to say that
the 2-ary relation defined by $x_1 \le x_2 \land x_2 \le x_3$ is
representable, just because $\le$~is representable. Note this
does not quite fit the framework of the previous theorem on Boolean
combinations, since $x_1 \le x_2$ and $x_2 \le x_3$ involve
different sets of variables. For another example, we would like
to know that $P(x)\cdot P(y) \le x+y$ defines a representable
relation, just because the relation~$\le$ and the
functions~$P$ and~$+$ are representable. All of these will follow from the following
general theorem.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:composeRepresentable}
{\rm (Composition with Representable Functions)}
Work in the theory~$\TR$.
Suppose $g_1, \, g_2, \ldots,g_\ell$ are $k$-ary representable
functions. Let each $g_i$ be represented by the formula $A_{g_i}(x_1,\ldots,x_k,y)$.
We write $\vec x$ and $\vec n$ to denote
$x_1,\ldots,x_k$ and $n_1,\ldots, n_k$.
\begin{itemizeLoose}
\item[(a)] Suppose $S$ is an $\ell$-ary representable relation
and is represented by the formula~$A_S(y_1,\ldots, y_\ell)$. Then there
is a formula~$A_{S^\prime}(x_1,\ldots,x_k)$ that
represents the $k$-ary relation~$S^\prime$ defined by
\[
S^\prime(\vec n) ~\Leftrightarrow~ S(g_1(\vec n),g_2(\vec n), \ldots, g_\ell(\vec n)).
\]
\item[(b)] Suppose $f$ is an $\ell$-ary representable function
and is represented by the formula~$A_f(y_1,\ldots, y_\ell,z)$. Then there is
a formula $A_{f^\prime}(x_1,\ldots,x_k,z)$
that represents the $k$-ary function~$f^\prime$ defined by
\[
f^\prime(\vec n) ~=~ f(g_1(\vec n),g_2(\vec n), \ldots, g_\ell(\vec n)).
\]
\end{itemizeLoose}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By the definition of representability, for all $i=1,\ldots,\ell$ and
all $n_1,\ldots,n_k \in\penalty10000 \mathbb N$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:composeRepresentableThm}
\TR ~\vdash~ \forall y\, [A_{g_i}(\underline{n_1},\ldots,\underline{n_k}, y) \liff y = \underline{g_i(n_1,\ldots,n_k)}]
\end{equation}
To prove~(a), we know that for all $m_1,\ldots,m_\ell \in \mathbb N$,
\begin{itemizeLoose}
\item[(i)] If $S(m_1,\ldots,m_\ell)$ holds, 
then $\TR$ proves $A_S(\underline{m_1},\ldots,\underline{m_\ell})$.
\item[(ii)] If $S(m_1,\ldots,m_\ell)$ is false, then $\TR$ proves $\lnot A_S(\underline{m_1},\ldots,\underline{m_\ell})$.
\end{itemizeLoose}
The intuition is that we would like to let $A_{S^\prime}$ be the ``formula''
\[
A_S(g_1(\vec x), g_2(\vec x),\ldots, g_\ell(\vec x)),
\]
but this makes no sense since the $g_i$'s are not function symbols, they are functions.
Instead, we exploit the representability of the $g_i$'s and
define $A_{S^\prime}(x_1,\ldots,x_k)$
to be the formula\footnote{We could equally as well have used the formula
\[
\forall y_1\,\forall y_2\cdots \forall y_\ell\, \Bigl[
    \bigl(\bigand\nolimits_{i=1}^\ell A_{g_i}(\vec x, y_i) \bigr)
    \limplies A_S(y_1,\ldots, y_\ell) \Bigr] .
\]
}
\[
A_{S^\prime}(x_1,\ldots,x_k) ~:=~ \exists y_1\,\exists y_2\cdots \exists y_\ell\, \Bigl[
    \bigl(\bigand\nolimits_{i=1}^\ell A_{g_i}(\vec x, y_i) \bigr)
    \land A_S(y_1,\ldots, y_\ell) \Bigr] .
\]
We need to show that, for all $n_1,\ldots,n_k \in \mathbb N$,
\begin{itemizeLoose}
\item[(i$^\prime$)] If $S^\prime(n_1,\ldots,n_k)$ holds, then
             $\TR$ proves $A_{S^\prime}(\underline{n_1},\ldots,\underline{n_k})$.
\item[(ii$^\prime$)] If $S^\prime(n_1,\ldots,n_k)$ is false, then
             $\TR$ proves $\lnot A_{S^\prime}(\underline{n_1},\ldots,\underline{n_k})$.
\end{itemizeLoose}
It is straightforward to prove (i$^\prime$), since if $S^\prime( n_1,\ldots, n_k)$
holds, then letting $m_i = g(n_1,\ldots, n_k)$ and using
(\ref{eq:composeRepresentableThm}) and condition~(i) shows that 
$\TR$ proves $A_{S^\prime}(\underline{\vec n})$.
The proof that (ii$^\prime$) holds is similar, using condition~(ii) in place of~(i).

The proof of~(b) is similar to the proof of~(a).
By the representability of~$f$, we have that for all $m_1,\ldots,m_\ell \in \mathbb N$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:composeRepresentableThm2}
\TR ~\vdash~
  \forall z \, [ A_f(\underline{m_1},\ldots,\underline{m_\ell})=z \liff z = \underline{f(m_1,\ldots,m_\ell)} ]
\end{equation}
We let $A_{f^\prime}$ be the formula\footnote{Similarly to
before, we could equally as well have used the formula
\[
\forall y_1\,\forall y_2\cdots \forall y_\ell\, \Bigl[
    \bigl(\bigand\nolimits_{i=1}^\ell A_{g_i}(\vec x, y_i) \bigr)
    \limplies A_f(y_1,\ldots, y_\ell,z) \Bigr].
\]
}
\[
A_{f^\prime}(x_1,\ldots,x_k,z) ~:=~
    \exists y_1\,\exists y_2\cdots \exists y_\ell\, \Bigl[
    \bigl(\bigand\nolimits_{i=1}^\ell A_{g_i}(\vec x, y_i) \bigr)
    \land A_f(y_1,\ldots, y_\ell,z) \Bigr] .
\]
We need to show that, for all $n_1,\ldots,n_k \in \mathbb N$,
\[
\TR ~\vdash~
  \forall z \, [ A_f(\underline{n_1},\ldots,\underline{n_k},z) \liff z = \underline{f(n_1,\ldots,n_k)} ].
\]
Letting $m_i = g_i(n_1,\ldots,n_\ell)$, this follows from instances of
(\ref{eq:composeRepresentableThm}) and~(\ref{eq:composeRepresentableThm2}).
\end{proof}

To make good use of Theorem~\ref{thm:composeRepresentable},
we need to know that the ``\idxnm{projection}'' functions are representable.
\begin{definition}
Let $1\le i \le k $ be integers. The \idxem{projection} function~$\pi^k_i$
is the $k$-ary function defined by
\[
\pi(x_1,\ldots,x_k) ~=~ x_i .
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:projectionRepresentable}
The projection functions $\pi^k_i$ are representable in~$\TR$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
This is very simple to prove. Let $A_{\pi^k_i}(x_1,\ldots, x_k,y)$ be the
formula $y=x_i$. The fact that $A_{\pi^k_i}$ represents~$\pi^k_i$ is
easy to prove with the $\TR_{\textstyle\neq}$ axioms.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}
We show that the 3-ary relation
$S = \{ \langle n_1,n_2,n_3 \rangle : n_1\le\penalty10000 n_2\le\penalty10000 n_3 \}$,
which was discussed before the statement of
Theorem~\ref{thm:composeRepresentable}, is representable.
Define the relations
\begin{eqnarray*}
S_0 &:=& \{ \langle n_1,n_2 \rangle : n_1\le n_2 \} \\
S_1 &:=& \{ \langle n_1,n_2,n_3 \rangle : n_1\le n_2 \} \\
S_2 &:=& \{ \langle n_1,n_2,n_3 \rangle : n_2\le n_3 \} .
\end{eqnarray*}
Example~(\ref{example:representEqLt}) showed that $S_0$ is representable.
Since
for all~$n_1,n_2,n_3$
\begin{eqnarray*}
S_1(n_1,n_2,n_3) &\Leftrightarrow& S_0(\pi^3_1(n_1,n_2,n_3),\pi^3_2(n_1,n_2,n_3)) \\
S_2(n_1,n_2,n_3) &\Leftrightarrow& S_0(\pi^3_2(n_1,n_2,n_3),\pi^3_3(n_1,n_2,n_3)),
\end{eqnarray*}
Theorem~\ref{thm:composeRepresentable}(a) gives that $S_1$ and~$S_2$
are representable. Since $S = S_1 \cap S_2$, Theorem~\ref{thm:BooleanRepresentable}
implies that $S$ is also representable.
\end{example}

\begin{example}\label{example:composeRepresentable}
Now consider
$S = \{ \langle n_1, n_2 \rangle : P(n_1)\cdot P(n_2) \le n_1+n_2 \}$.
We show this is representable using Theorem~\ref{thm:composeRepresentable}.
Since the predecessor function~$P$
and the multiplication function~$\cdot$ are representable,
Theorems \ref{thm:composeRepresentable}
and~\ref{thm:projectionRepresentable} tell us that
\[
h(x_1,x_2) ~=~ P(x_1)\cdot P(x_2) ~=~ P(\pi^2_1(x_1,x_2))\cdot P(\pi^2_2(x_1,x_2))
\]
is a representable function.
Then, with $S_0$ the relation~$\le$ as in the previous example,
\[
S ~=~ \{ \langle n_1,n_2 \rangle : S_0(h(n_1,n_2),n_1+n_2) \}
\]
is representable by Theorem~\ref{thm:composeRepresentable}
since addition~($+$) is representable.
\end{example}

As a general principle, if $L$ is a first-order language
for the integers
containing functions symbols interpreted by representable
functions and containing
predicate symbols interpreted by representable
relations, then any $L$-term defines a representable
function, and any atomic $L$-formula defines a
representable relation.  This should be clear from
the last two examples, so we omit the proof. (A formal
proof would first use induction on the complexity of terms
along with Theorems \ref{thm:composeRepresentable}
and~\ref{thm:projectionRepresentable}, and then
use induction on the complexity of quantifier-free formulas
using Theorem~\ref{thm:BooleanRepresentable}.)

\subsection*{Bounded quantifiers}\label{sec:boundedQuantifier}
\index{bounded quantifier}

We now show that bounded quantifiers can be used
freely when defining representable functions
relations.

\begin{definition}[Bounded quantifiers]
Let $A$ be a formula, $x$~be a variable
and $t$~be a term not involving~$x$.
The notations $\forall x{\le}t\, A$ and
$\exists x{\le}t\, A$ are abbreviations for the formulas
$\forall x\, (x\le t \limplies A)$ and $\exists x\, (x\le t \land A)$.
The constructions
``$\forall x{\le}t$'' and  ``$\exists x{\le}t$''
are called \index{bounded quantifier}\emph{bounded quantifiers}.

The notations $\forall x{<}t\, A$ and
$\exists x{<}t\, A$ are defined similarly.
\end{definition}

\begin{example}\label{example:boundedQuants}
Here are some examples of properties that can be expressed with
the aid of bounded quantifiers:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ccl}
$y \mid x$ & $\exists z{\le}x\, (y\cdot z = x)$ & $x$ is a multiple of $y$, or $y$ \idxnm{divides} $x$ \\[1ex]
$\lfloor x/y \rfloor = z$ & $ \exists r{<}y\, (y\cdot z +r = x)$ & $x$ divided\index{division, integer} by $y$ rounded down \\[1ex]
$\TRem(x,y)=r$ & $r < y \land \exists z{\le}x \,(z\cdot y+r=x)$ & Remainder\index{reminder} of $x$ divided by $y$
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
The second and third examples only make sense for non-zero values of~$y$. Our convention
is that $\lfloor x/0\rfloor = 0$ and $\TRem(x,0) = 0$.
\end{example}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:boundedQuants}
Suppose that $S$ is a
$k$-ary representable relation, represented in~$\TR$ by
the formula $A_S(x_1,\ldots,x_k,y)$. Then
the formulas
\[
\forall y{\le}x_k\,A_S(x_1,\ldots,x_k,y)
\qquad\hbox{and}\qquad
\exists y{\le}x_k\,A_S(x_1,\ldots,x_k,y)
\]
represent, in~$\TR$, the $k$-ary relations
\[
S_\forall ~:=~ \{ \langle m_1,\ldots,m_k \rangle : \hbox{for all $n\le m_k$, $S(m_1,\ldots,m_k,n)$} \}
\]
and
\[
S_\exists ~:=~ \{ \langle m_1,\ldots,m_k \rangle : \hbox{for some $n\le m_k$, $S(m_1,\ldots,m_k,n)$} \}
\]
\end{theorem}
The theorem also holds for bounded quantifiers
$\forall y{<}x_k\, A$ and
$\exists y{<}x_k\, A$ with strict inequality.

\begin{proof}
We prove the $\forall$ case. First suppose $m_1,\ldots,m_k\in\mathbb N$ and
$S_\forall (m_1,\ldots,m_k)$ is true. Since $A$ represents~$S$, this
implies that, for all $n\le m_k$, $\TR$~proves
$A_S(\underline{m_1},\ldots, \underline{m_k},\underline{n})$.
Let $A_{S_\forall}(x_1,\ldots,x_k)$ be the formula
$\forall y {\le} x_k\, A_S(x_1,\ldots,x_{k-1},y)$.
Recall from Theorem~\ref{thm:RleAndlt}(b) that $\TR$ proves that
\[
y\le \underline{m_k} \liff y=0 \lor y=\underline 1 \lor \cdots \lor y=\underline{m_k}.
\]
It thus follows that $\TR$ proves
$\forall y{\le}\underline{m_k}\, A_S(\underline{m_1},\ldots, \underline{m_k})$.
In other words, $\TR$~proves $A_{S_\forall}(\underline{m_1},\ldots, \underline{m_k})$.

Second, suppose that $S_\forall (m_1,\ldots,m_k)$ is false. Thus
there is some $n\le m_k$ such that $S(m_1,\ldots,m_k,n)$ is false
and by the representability of~$S$, the theory~$\TR$ proves
$\lnot A_S(\underline{m_1},\ldots, \underline{m_k},\underline{n})$.
Since $\TR \vdash \underline n \le \underline{m_k}$, it follows
that $\TR$~proves
$\lnot A_{S_\forall}(\underline{m_1},\ldots, \underline{m_k})$ as desired.

The $\exists$ case of the theorem follows immediately
from the $\forall$ by duality since
$\exists y{\le}x_k\, A_S(x_1,\ldots,x_k,y)$ is
logically equivalent
$\lnot \forall y{\le}x_k\,\lnot A_S(x_1,\ldots,x_k,y)$
and since Theorem~\ref{thm:BooleanRepresentable}
states that the set of representable relations
is closed under complementation.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{coro:simpleRep_A}
The following relations are representable in~$\TR$
(the first three are from Example~\ref{example:boundedQuants}):
\begin{enumerateLoose}
\item The binary relation $y \mid x$.
\item The graph of the binary function $\lfloor x / y \rfloor$.
\item The graph of the binary function $\TRem(x,y)$.
\item The unary relation $\TPowerTwo := \{ n : \hbox{$n$ is a power of~$2$} \} = \{ 2^i : i\in \mathbb n \}$.
\end{enumerateLoose}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The representability of $y \mid x$ follows
from the formula in Example~\ref{example:boundedQuants}, and from
Theorem~\ref{thm:boundedQuants}.
The representability of the graph of $\lfloor x / y \rfloor$
also follows from Example~\ref{example:boundedQuants}, but adjusted
to handle the case where $y=0$. Namely, the 3-ary relation $\lfloor x/y \rfloor = z$
can be defined by
\[
[y\neq0 \land \exists r{<}y\, (y\cdot z +r = x)] \lor [y=0 \land z=0].
\]
The representability of the 3-ary relation $\TRem(x,y) = z$ is
proved similarly.

The representability of the property $\TPowerTwo(x)$ follows from
the fact that it can be expressed as:
\[
(\forall z{\le}x)[z\mid x ~ \limplies ~ z=\underline 1 \,\lor\, \underline 2 \mid z].
\qedhere
\]
\end{proof}

The earlier mentioned ``general principle'' discussed
after Example~\ref{example:composeRepresentable} can now
be updated to allow bounded quantifiers to be used in formulas
representing relations and functions.  As before,
suppose $L$ is a first-order language
for the integers
containing function symbols interpreted by representable
functions and
predicate symbols interpreted by representable
relations.
Then any $L$-formula formed from atomic $L$-formula using propositional
connectives and bounded quantifiers represents a
relation (in~$\TR$).  This can be proved formally by
induction on the complexity of formulas using
Theorems
\ref{thm:BooleanRepresentable},
\ref{thm:composeRepresentable},
\ref{thm:projectionRepresentable}
and~\ref{thm:boundedQuants}.


\subsection*{Graph of a function.}\index{graph of a function}
We next show that
a function~$f$ is representable if and only if its
graph~$G_f$
is a representable relation.

\begin{example}
The \idxnm{predecessor} function $P(n) = n\dotminus 1$ was defined
in Theorem~\ref{thm:incDecTuring}. Its graph is the set of
ordered pairs
\[
G_P ~:=~ \{ \langle m, n \rangle : P(m)=n \} ~=~
         \{ \langle m, n \rangle : S(n)=m \lor (m=0 \land n=0) \} .
\]
The graph~$G_P$ of~$P$ is a binary relation. We claim it is represented
by the formula~$A_{G_P}$ defined by
\[
A_{G_P}(x_1, x_2) ~:=~ S(x_2)=x_1 \lor (x_1=0 \land x_2=0).
\]
To prove this, it is necessary and sufficient to show that $\TR$ proves the
following:
\begin{itemizeLoose}\label{example:representPredRelation}
\item[(i)] $\TR$~proves $S(0)=0 \lor (0=0\land 0=0)$.
\item[(ii)] For $n\neq 0$,
            $\TR$~proves $\lnot[S(\underline n)=0 \lor (0=0\land \underline n=0)]$.
\item[(iii)] For $m>0$, $\TR$~proves
             $S(\underline{m{-}1})=\underline m \lor (\underline m=0\land \underline{m{-}1}=0)$
\item[(iv)] For $m>0$ and $n\neq m-1$, $\TR$~proves
                 $\lnot[S(\underline n)=\underline m \lor (\underline m=0\land \underline n=0)]$.
\end{itemizeLoose}
Items (i) and~(iii) express the conditions that if $n=P(m)$, then
$\TR$ proves~$A_{G_P}(m,n)$.
Items (ii) and~(iv) express the conditions that if $n\neq P(m)$, then
$\TR$ proves~$\lnot A_{G_P}(m,n)$. All of (i)-(iv) are logical
consequences of the $\TR_{\textstyle\neq}$ axioms of~$\TR$.
\end{example}
\begin{example}\label{example:representPredFunc}
Continuing the previous example, we claim that the
\idxnm{predecessor} {\em function} is also representable.  For
this, we need a formula $A_P(x_1,y)$ that satisfies,
for every $m \in \mathbb N$,
\[
\TR ~\vdash~ \forall y\, [A_P(\underline m,y) \liff y=\underline { m\dotminus 1 } ].
\]
Such a formula must define the graph~$G_P$ of~$P$; hence it is tempting
to set $A_P$ equal to the formula~$A_{G_P}$. This, however, does not work,
as $\TR$ does not prove $\forall y\, (Sy \neq 0)$ and hence does not
prove $\forall y\, [A_{G_P}(0,y) \limplies y=0 ]$.  Nonetheless,
the predecessor function~$P$ is representable. The formula~$A_P$
representing~$P$ can be chosen to be
\[
A_P(x_1,y) ~:=~ (x_1\neq 0 \land S(y)=x_1) \lor (x_1=0 \land y=0).
\]
Exercise~\ref{exer:representPredFunc}(a) asks you to prove
that this choice of~$A_P$ represents the predecessor function~$P$.
\end{example}

We have seen that both the predecessor function
and the graph of the predecessor relation
are representable.  This is an example of the next theorem.
\begin{theorem}\label{thm:graphFunctionRep}\label{graph of a function}
Let $f$ be a $k$-ary function on~$\mathbb N$. The graph~$G_f$ of~$f$
is representable in~$\TR$ if and only if $f$~is representable in~$\TR$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
It is easy to prove that if $A_f(x_1,\ldots, x_k,y)$
is representable, then $A_f$~also represents the
graph~$G_f$ of~$f$. This follows by the fact that
the $\TR_{\textstyle\neq}$~axioms and
the condition~(\ref{eq:representFunction}) defining
what it means for $A_f$ to represent~$f$ imply
the conditions (a) and~(b)
in Definition~\ref{def:representRelation}
about the representability of a relation.

Conversely, suppose $A_{G_f}(x_1,\ldots, x_, y)$
represents the $(k+1)$-ary relation~$G_f$. As the previous
examples show, $A_{G_f}$~may not represent~$f$. We instead
define $A_f(x_1,\ldots, x_k, y)$ to be the
formula
\[
A_f(x_1,\ldots, x_k, y)  ~:=~
     A_{G_f}(x_1,\ldots, x_k, y) \,\land\, \forall z{<}y \, (\lnot A_{G_f}(x_1,\ldots, x_k, z)).
\]
We claim that $A_f$ represents~$f$.  To prove this, we show
that, for any $n_1\ldots,n_k \in\penalty10000 \mathbb N$ and for $m = f(n_1,\ldots, n_k)$,
\begin{itemizeLoose}
\item[(i)]  $\TR$ proves $A_f(\underline{n_1},\ldots, \underline {n_k}, \underline{m})$, and
\item[(ii)] $\TR$ proves $A_f(\underline{n_1},\ldots, \underline {n_k}, y ) \limplies y=\underline{m}$.
\end{itemizeLoose}
To help prove (i) and~(ii), we will also prove
\begin{itemizeLoose}
\item[(iii)]  $\TR$ proves
$y<\underline m \limplies \lnot A_f(\underline{n_1},\ldots, \underline {n_k}, y)$, and
\item[(iv)] $\TR$ proves
$\underline m < y \limplies \lnot A_f(\underline{n_1},\ldots, \underline {n_k}, y )$.
\end{itemizeLoose}
Theorem~\ref{thm:RleAndlt}(c) states that $\TR$~proves
that $y<\underline m \limplies y=0 \lor y=\underline 1 \lor\penalty10000 \cdots \lor y = \underline{m{-}1}$.
Since $A_{G_f}$~represents the graph of~$f$, we have that
$\TR$~proves $A_{G_f}(\underline{n_1},\ldots, \underline {n_k}, \underline m)$
and that
$\TR$~proves $\lnot A_{G_f}(\underline{n_1},\ldots, \underline {n_k}, \underline{m^\prime})$
for each $m^\prime<m$.  Items (i) and~(iii) follows immediately from these facts and
the definition of~$A_f$.

Item~(iv) holds since $\TR$~proves $A_{G_f}(\underline{n_1},\ldots, \underline {n_k}, \underline m)$ and
by the definition of~$A_f$.

Theorem~\ref{thm:RleAndlt}(d)
established that $\TR$ proves 
$y<\underline m \lor y=\underline m \lor \underline m < y$.
Therefore, item~(ii) follows from (iii) and~(iv).
\end{proof}

\begin{example}
The functions $\lfloor x/y \rfloor$ and $\TRem(x,y)$ are
representable in~$\TR$. This is because their graphs are
representable by Corollary~\ref{coro:simpleRep_A}.
\end{example}

\begin{example}
The functions $z=\max(x,y)$ and $z=\min(x,y)$
are representable since their
graphs are represented by the formulas
\[
(x\le y \land z=y)\lor(\lnot x\le y \land z=x)
\quad\hbox{and} \quad
(x\le y \land z=x)\lor(\lnot x\le y \land z=y) . 
\]
The $\dotminus$ function $z = x \dotminus y$ is representable
since its graph~$G_\dotminus$ is represented by the
formula $(y\le x \land z+y=x) \lor (\lnot y \le x \land z=0)$.
\end{example}

\subsection*{Regular minimization}

The minimization operator $\mu x \, (\cdots)$ is used to define
a (partial) computable function, letting ``$\mu x (\cdots)$'' mean
``the least $x$ such that~$\cdots$''. We have already encountered
three different forms of the minimization operator in
Example~\ref{example:partialComputeSeekPairPrimes}
Exercises \ref{exer:minimize1} and~\ref{exer:minimize2}.
To repeat the definitions, we have:

\begin{definition}\label{def:minimizeRevisited}\index{minimization ($\mu$)}
Let $S(x_1,\ldots,x_k,y)$ be a $(k+1)$-ary relation.
Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:minimizationFromRelation}
g(x_1,\ldots,x_k) ~=~ \mu y\, S(x_1,\ldots,x_k,y)
\end{equation}
defines the $k$-ary partial function such that $g(n_1,\ldots, n_k) = m$
provided that $m$ is the least value (if any) such that
$S(n_1,\ldots,n_k,m)$ holds. If there is no such~$y$, then
$g(n_1,\ldots,n_k)$ is undefined (diverges).

Let $f(x_1,\ldots,x_k,y)$ be a $(k+1)$-ary
function. Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:minimizationFromFunction}
h(x_1,\ldots,n_k) ~=~ \mu y\, (f(x_1,\ldots,x_k,y)=0)
\end{equation}
defines the $k$-ary partial function such that $h(n_1,\ldots, n_k) = m$
provided that $m$ is the least value (if any) such that
$f(n_1,\ldots,n_k,m)\converges = 0$ and such that for all $m^\prime<m$,
$f(n_1,\ldots,n_m)$ converges and is non-zero.
If there is no such~$y$, then
$h(n_1,\ldots,n_k)$ is undefined (diverges).

If $g$ and~$h$ are total functions,
the definitions (\ref{eq:minimizationFromRelation}) and (\ref{eq:minimizationFromFunction})
are called \idxem{regular minimization}. For instance, the function~$g$ is defined by
regular minimization provided that for all $\vec n\in \mathbb N$, there
is an $m \in \mathbb N$ such that $S(n_1,\ldots,n_k,m)$.
\end{definition}

The method of Example~\ref{example:partialComputeSeekPairPrimes} shows that if
$S$~is decidable, then the function~$g$ defined
by~(\ref{eq:minimizationFromRelation}) is partial
computable. If (\ref{eq:minimizationFromRelation}) is a regular
minimization, then $g$~is computable.  In both cases, the algorithm
for computing $g(n_1,\ldots,n_k)$ acts by successively checking whether $S(n_1,\ldots,n_k,m)$
holds for $m=0,1,2,\ldots$.  Similarly, the function~$h$ defined
by~(\ref{eq:minimizationFromFunction}) is partial computable if
$f$~is partial computable.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:regularMinRep}
\ ~ \newline\begin{itemizeLoose}
\item[(a)] Let $g$~be a $k$-ary function defined
by regular minimization from a relation~$S$
by~(\ref{eq:minimizationFromRelation}). Suppose
that $S$~is representable in~$\TR$.  Then $g$~is representable in~$\TR$.
\item[(a)] Let $h$~be a $k$-ary function defined
by regular minimization from a function~$f$
by~(\ref{eq:minimizationFromFunction}). Suppose
that $S$~is representable in~$\TR$.  Then $h$~is representable in~$\TR$.
\end{itemizeLoose}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $A_S(x_1,\ldots,x_,y)$ represent~$S$.
Then $g(x_1,\ldots,x_k,y)$ is represented by
\[
A_S(x_1,\ldots,x_k,y) \,\land\, \forall z {<} y\, (\lnot A_S(x_1,\ldots,x_k,y)).
\]
That proves part~(a). Part~(b) follows from part~(a), since
if $f$ is representable, then $G_f$~is representable
(by Theorem~\ref{thm:graphFunctionRep}) and
$\mu y\, (f(x_1,\ldots,x_k,y)=0)$ is equal to $\mu y \, G_f(x_1,\ldots,x_k,y,0)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{example} The greatest common divisor and least common divisor are
representable, since
\begin{eqnarray*}
\Tlcm(x,y) &=& \mu z\, (x=0 \lor y=0 \lor (0<z \land x\mid z \land y \mid z))
\\[1ex]
\Tgcd(x,y) &=& \max \{z\le x : z\mid x \land z\mid y \} ~=~ x - \mu w\, ((x-w) \mid x \land (x-w) \mid y ) .
\end{eqnarray*}
\end{example}
The definition of $\Tgcd$ using the construction
``$ x-\mu w\, ( \cdots)$'' illustrates the use of \idxnm{bounded maximization}.
\begin{example}\label{example:2log2}
Corollary~\ref{coro:simpleRep_A} showed that the set $\TPowerTwo$ of powers of~2
is representable. The unary function that gives the least power of~2 greater than~$x$
is equal to
\[
    2^{\lfloor \log_2 x \rfloor+1}  ~=~  \mu w\, (\TPowerTwo(w) \land x<w).
\]
(The left-hand side does not make sense for $x=0$.)
The greatest power of~2 that is less than or equal to~$x$ is equal to
\[
    2^{\lfloor \log_2 x \rfloor}  ~=~  \mu w\, (\TPowerTwo(w) \land x<2\cdot w).
\]
We adopt this last equation as the definition
of $2^{\lfloor \log_2 x \rfloor}$.
This is tantamount to
adopting the convention that $\lfloor \log_2 0 \rfloor = 1$.
\end{example}

The function $2^{\lfloor \log_2 x \rfloor}$ is defined with regular minimization
and thus is representable. This will be an important function for
representing functions associated with sequence coding.
